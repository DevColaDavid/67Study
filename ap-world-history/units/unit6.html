Unit 6: Consequences of Industrialization (c. 1750-c. 1900)

← AP World History
From-Scratch Overview

Unit 6 focuses on imperial expansion, labor migrations, and anti-imperial resistance under industrial capitalism.

Think in system terms: demand for raw materials and markets reshaped political geography.

This unit requires both macro patterns and local case outcomes.

Detailed Core Notes

▼

Industrial powers expanded territorial and informal control in Africa and Asia.

Treaty ports, debt leverage, and gunboat pressure enabled informal imperial domination.

Colonial economies often shifted toward export monocrops or mineral extraction.

Railways, ports, and telegraph systems improved extraction and command functions.

Indentured and contract labor migration linked South Asia, East Asia, Africa, and plantation economies.

Settler colonial systems dispossessed indigenous communities and reorganized land use.

Resistance movements included armed uprisings, legal activism, and religious-political mobilization.

Imperial states often framed expansion as civilizing while enforcing coercive labor and legal hierarchies.

Deeper Explanation and Analysis

▼

Avoid one-directional narratives: colonized actors negotiated, adapted, and resisted in diverse ways.

Economic development claims must be evaluated against extraction, coercion, and unequal political power.

Comparative writing should distinguish direct conquest from financial and legal dependency regimes.

A powerful FRQ paragraph connects infrastructure investment to labor control and commodity flow.

Use demographic evidence where possible: migration volume, urbanization shifts, and labor regime transitions.

Good conclusions emphasize asymmetry with agency, not asymmetry alone.

Practice and Exam Explanation

▼

Evidence cluster: Scramble for Africa maps, colonial concession systems, indentured migration routes, anti-colonial uprisings.

DBQ grouping tip: imperial officials vs colonized voices vs economic data visuals.

Complexity move: compare outcomes in two colonies with different labor regimes.

Always identify actor, mechanism, and consequence in each evidence sentence.

How To Use This In FRQs
  • Open with a claim that uses extent language (high, moderate, limited) rather than vague wording.
  • Build each body paragraph around one analytical category and two specific examples.
  • Use one sentence per paragraph to explain why your evidence matters for the prompt.
  • Conclude by weighing limits or counterevidence to show complexity.
SAQ Practice
Identify one specific development from this unit and explain one significant effect with direct evidence.
DBQ Guidance
Group documents by analytical category first, then add one external fact not directly stated in the set.
LEQ Prompt
Evaluate the extent to which industrialization drove new migration and labor systems from 1750 to 1900.
Common Mistake To Avoid

Do not summarize events without argument. Every paragraph should make and defend a claim tied to the prompt.
