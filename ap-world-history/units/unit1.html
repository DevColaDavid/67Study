Unit 1: The Global Tapestry (c. 1200-c. 1450)

← AP World History
From-Scratch Overview

Unit 1 is your baseline for premodern state systems. You are learning how power worked before Atlantic empires became dominant.

If you start from zero, focus first on governance structures, then on social hierarchy, then on religious and cultural legitimation.

Your AP goal is comparison: show how different regions solved similar political problems with different institutions.

Detailed Core Notes

▼

Song China used scholar-official bureaucracy and exam recruitment, which linked elite education to state service.

Neo-Confucian ethics reinforced hierarchy and family order, helping sustain social discipline and elite legitimacy.

In Dar al-Islam, political fragmentation did not eliminate legal and scholarly connectivity; ulama and commercial cities remained influential.

South and Southeast Asian states blended imported and local traditions, showing adaptation rather than simple cultural replacement.

The Delhi Sultanate governed diverse populations through military power and administrative experimentation.

Srivijaya and related maritime powers show how control over chokepoints could generate state wealth and strategic influence.

In Mesoamerica, Mexica tribute systems tied military conquest to resource extraction.

In the Andes, Inca governance relied on labor drafts, road systems, and redistributive storage to bind vast regions.

Deeper Explanation and Analysis

▼

A high-level AP comparison asks not only who ruled, but how rulers made obedience seem legitimate and durable.

Some states leaned on bureaucracy, others on military coercion, others on sacred authority, and most blended these methods.

Continuity and change should both appear in your writing: institutions evolve, but older social structures often persist.

Do not flatten regions into stereotypes. East Asia, Dar al-Islam, South Asia, Europe, and the Americas each contained internal variation.

For example, elite culture and peasant obligations interacted differently in Song China versus Inca domains.

When you write FRQs, always connect political form to social outcomes such as class, gender, taxation, and labor obligations.

Practice and Exam Explanation

▼

Reliable evidence cluster: Song exams, Neo-Confucian social norms, Delhi Sultanate governance, and Inca mita labor obligations.

Add one cross-regional comparison in every long-form answer to improve complexity points.

Frame causation carefully: belief systems did not simply reflect politics; they also actively structured authority.

Use direct historical nouns in claims (institutions, decrees, labor systems, legal schools) rather than vague wording.

How To Use This In FRQs
  • Open with a claim that uses extent language (high, moderate, limited) rather than vague wording.
  • Build each body paragraph around one analytical category and two specific examples.
  • Use one sentence per paragraph to explain why your evidence matters for the prompt.
  • Conclude by weighing limits or counterevidence to show complexity.
SAQ Practice
Identify one specific development from this unit and explain one significant effect with direct evidence.
DBQ Guidance
Group documents by analytical category first, then add one external fact not directly stated in the set.
LEQ Prompt
Evaluate the extent to which states from 1200 to 1450 relied on institutions rather than military force to maintain authority.
Common Mistake To Avoid

Do not summarize events without argument. Every paragraph should make and defend a claim tied to the prompt.
