Unit 9: Globalization (c. 1900-present)

← AP World History
From-Scratch Overview

Unit 9 explores accelerated global interconnection in economy, culture, migration, governance, and environmental systems.

The AP lens here is dual: globalization can increase integration while also producing inequality, resistance, and fragmentation.

Your argument should include both macro patterns and specific regional consequences.

Detailed Core Notes

▼

Container shipping, aviation, and digital communication compressed time and distance in global exchange.

International institutions and trade regimes shaped state policy options and development strategies.

Transnational corporations reorganized production across supply chains, influencing labor markets worldwide.

Migration and media flows spread ideas and culture while intensifying debates over identity and sovereignty.

Human-rights, labor, and environmental NGOs built transnational advocacy networks.

Financial integration increased growth opportunities but also transmitted crises rapidly across regions.

Environmental stress, climate change, and resource pressure became central global political issues.

AP analysis should reject one-sided stories: include both gains and uneven burdens across regions and social groups.

Deeper Explanation and Analysis

▼

High-value evidence includes digital communication expansion, WTO-era trade debates, climate negotiations, and anti-globalization movements.

Your best thesis will argue that globalization is best understood as contested interdependence rather than simple convergence.

Reject narratives that treat globalization as linear convergence toward one model.

Regional and class inequality can increase under high integration if bargaining power is uneven.

FRQ quality improves when you pair a global process with two contrasting regional outcomes.

A strong conclusion identifies winners, losers, and institutions that mediate these outcomes.

Practice and Exam Explanation

▼

DBQ grouping: integration mechanisms, inequality effects, and governance responses.

Complexity move: compare short-term growth gains with long-term ecological and social costs.

Use quantifiable trends where possible, then interpret significance.

Tie each example to one clear causal mechanism.

How To Use This In FRQs
  • Open with a claim that uses extent language (high, moderate, limited) rather than vague wording.
  • Build each body paragraph around one analytical category and two specific examples.
  • Use one sentence per paragraph to explain why your evidence matters for the prompt.
  • Conclude by weighing limits or counterevidence to show complexity.
SAQ Practice
Identify one specific development from this unit and explain one significant effect with direct evidence.
DBQ Guidance
Group documents by analytical category first, then add one external fact not directly stated in the set.
LEQ Prompt
Evaluate the extent to which globalization since 1900 has reduced global inequality.
Common Mistake To Avoid

Do not summarize events without argument. Every paragraph should make and defend a claim tied to the prompt.
