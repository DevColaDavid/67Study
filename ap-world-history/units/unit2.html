← AP World HistoryExam Weight: 8%-10%
How To Study This Unit

Use the subtopics below in order, and build your notes by combining conceptual understanding, concrete examples, and analytical writing practice. AP World rewards explanation more than memorized lists.

For each section, ask: what changed, what stayed the same, and how does this compare to another region or period? That three-part routine helps you prepare for SAQ, DBQ, and LEQ at the same time.

2.1 The Silk Roads

▼

This subtopic should be approached through broad historical context first, then narrowed to specific evidence. In AP World, strong understanding comes from identifying how commercial expansion developed over time and why it mattered for both rulers and ordinary communities.

A useful method is to track what changed, what stayed continuous, and what spread across regions. In many cases, long-distance trade interacts with political authority, social organization, and economic systems, so your notes should always connect institutions to lived outcomes.

For high-level exam writing, use this subtopic to build precise claims with named examples, then explain how those examples prove your argument. The best responses link local evidence to larger world-historical patterns such as diaspora communities, cross-regional comparison, and long-term causation.

Practice and Explanation

▼
FRQ Writing Method
  • Write a direct claim that answers the prompt with extent or comparison language.
  • Support each paragraph with one named example and one explanation sentence.
  • Connect your evidence to causation, continuity/change, or comparison explicitly.
  • End with a limit or counterpoint to show complexity.
SAQ Practice
Identify one major development from this unit and explain one significant consequence using direct evidence.
DBQ Practice
Group documents by theme first (political, social, economic, environmental), then add one outside example not in the set.
LEQ Practice
Evaluate the extent to which the key transformations in this unit represented continuity versus change over time.
