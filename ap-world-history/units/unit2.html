Unit 2: Networks of Exchange (c. 1200-c. 1450)

← AP World History
From-Scratch Overview

Unit 2 explains how trade networks reshaped Afro-Eurasian societies through economic, cultural, and biological exchange.

Think of three major networks as different systems with overlapping effects: Silk Roads, Indian Ocean, and Trans-Saharan routes.

The strongest AP answers separate causes of network growth from social consequences of that growth.

Detailed Core Notes

▼

The Silk Roads linked East Asia, Central Asia, and parts of Southwest Asia, often emphasizing high-value goods. Caravanserai and imperial protection reduced risk for merchants.

The Indian Ocean network expanded with monsoon knowledge, larger ships, and commercial diasporas. It carried bulk goods more efficiently than overland routes.

Trans-Saharan trade used camel caravans to connect West Africa with North Africa and Mediterranean markets, including major exchanges of gold and salt.

Financial innovations such as paper money, credit arrangements, and banking practices allowed merchants to operate at larger scales.

Cultural diffusion moved religions and ideas: Buddhism spread along Eurasian routes, Islam expanded through trade communities, and new artistic forms circulated broadly.

Biological exchange included disease transmission, most notably the Black Death, which had demographic and labor consequences in multiple regions.

A high-quality AP argument separates cause from effect: state support and technology expanded networks; expanded networks then accelerated diffusion and disruption.

You should also compare pace and pattern: maritime exchange often handled larger volume, while overland routes concentrated luxury transport and diplomatic contact.

Deeper Explanation and Analysis

▼

Use specific evidence clusters in essays: caravanserai, monsoon navigation, diasporic merchant communities, and plague transmission pathways.

The strongest conclusions show that exchange produced both integration and instability, not one-directional progress.

Economic integration created opportunity and instability simultaneously, which is why one-sided narratives score poorly.

Route type mattered: maritime routes favored bulk movement; overland routes often favored concentrated high-value exchange.

State policy and geography shaped node importance, from inland caravan hubs to coastal entrepots.

A sophisticated argument explains both integration and fracture in the same analytical frame.

Practice and Exam Explanation

▼

In DBQs, group documents by economic, cultural, and biological effects before drafting your thesis.

Outside evidence could include specific ports, court sponsorship, or migration evidence tied to trade corridors.

Use temporal language (short-term disruption vs long-term structural change) to improve analytical precision.

Name route mechanics directly to increase specificity: monsoon, caravanserai, and long-distance credit instruments.

How To Use This In FRQs
  • Open with a claim that uses extent language (high, moderate, limited) rather than vague wording.
  • Build each body paragraph around one analytical category and two specific examples.
  • Use one sentence per paragraph to explain why your evidence matters for the prompt.
  • Conclude by weighing limits or counterevidence to show complexity.
SAQ Practice
Identify one specific development from this unit and explain one significant effect with direct evidence.
DBQ Guidance
Group documents by analytical category first, then add one external fact not directly stated in the set.
LEQ Prompt
Evaluate the extent to which trade networks transformed Afro-Eurasian societies from 1200 to 1450.
Common Mistake To Avoid

Do not summarize events without argument. Every paragraph should make and defend a claim tied to the prompt.
