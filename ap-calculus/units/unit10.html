Unit 10: Infinite Sequences and Series

← AP CalculusBC Exam Weight: 17%-18% (BC only unit)
How To Study This Unit

BC only. A sequence \(\{a_n\}\) is a list; a series \(\sum a_n\) is the limit of its partial sums \(S_N=a_1+\cdots+a_N\). Always start with the nth-term test, then match the term shape to geometric, \(p\)-series, comparison, integral, alternating, or ratio tools.

Power series need a radius from the ratio (or root) test, then plug endpoints back into the original series with \([\ ]\) for convergent endpoints and \((\ )\) for divergent ones. Keep Lagrange and alternating error bounds in “\(\leq\)” form with the correct derivative order \((n+1)\) and the first omitted term.

10.1 Defining Convergent and Divergent Infinite Series (BC)

▼

A series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges if the sequence of partial sums \(S_N=\sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) approaches a finite limit \(S\). Otherwise it diverges (including to \(\pm\infty\) or oscillation of partial sums).

Sequence convergence asks whether terms \(a_n\) approach a limit; series convergence asks whether cumulative sums stabilize. Example: \(a_n=\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}\) gives the sequence \(1,\frac12,\frac14,\ldots\to 0\), while \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^{n-1}}=2\).

\(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n = \lim_{N\to\infty}S_N\) when this limit exists.
ObjectQuestionTypical tools
Sequence \(\{a_n\}\)\(\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n\)?Limits, L’Hospital on \(n\) via continuous \(x\)
Series \(\sum a_n\)\(\lim_{N\to\infty}S_N\)?Convergence tests, closed sums (geometric)
Common Mistake

Confusing \(\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=0\) with convergence of \(\sum a_n\) (harmonic series \(\sum\frac1n\) diverges even though \(\frac1n\to 0\)).

10.2 Working with Geometric Series (BC)

▼

\(\sum ar^n\) converges if and only if \(|r|<1\). When it converges, the infinite sum is first term shown divided by \(1-r\) after you align the index so the first displayed term is truly \(a\).

If the sum starts at \(n=0\): \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n=\frac{a}{1-r}\) for \(|r|<1\). Partial sums (for \(r\neq 1\)): \(\displaystyle S_n=\frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}\).
Indexing on the AP Exam
  • If the series starts at \(n=1\), the first term is \(ar\) (when written as \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty ar^{n-1}\), read \(a\) as the \(n=1\) term).
  • Rewrite \(\sum \frac{3^n}{5^n}\) as \(\sum(\frac35)^n\) to spot \(r\).
  • If \(|r|\geq 1\), the series diverges (nth-term test: terms do not go to \(0\) unless trivial edge cases).
\(1+\frac13+\frac19+\cdots\): \(a=1\), \(r=\frac13\), sum \(=\frac{1}{1-1/3}=\frac32\). Also \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty(\frac23)^n=\frac{2/3}{1-2/3}=2\).
Common Mistake

Using \(\frac{a}{1-r}\) with the wrong \(a\) because the series was shifted (e.g. starts at \(n=3\)) without factoring out powers of \(r\) first.

10.3 The nth Term Test for Divergence (BC)

▼

This test only proves divergence from a necessary condition: if \(\sum a_n\) converges, then necessarily \(\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=0\). The contrapositive is what you use on the exam: if \(\lim a_n\neq 0\) or the limit fails to exist, then \(\sum a_n\) diverges.

If \(\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n\neq 0\) or DNE \(\Rightarrow\) \(\sum a_n\) diverges. If \(\lim a_n=0\), the test says nothing (inconclusive).
\(\sum \frac{n}{n+1}\): limit \(1\neq 0\) ⇒ diverges. \(\sum \cos n\): terms oscillate, no limit ⇒ diverges. \(\sum \frac1n\): limit \(0\) but series diverges—need another test.
Always-First Check
  • Compute \(\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n\) (often treat \(n\) as continuous \(x\) and use L’Hospital if indeterminate).
  • If nonzero or nonexistent, stop: divergent.
  • If zero, proceed to structure (geometric, \(p\)-series, alternating, etc.).

10.4 Integral Test for Convergence (BC)

▼

For positive, continuous, eventually decreasing \(a_n=f(n)\), \(\sum_{n=N}^\infty a_n\) and \(\int_N^\infty f(x)\,dx\) share convergence or divergence—but the value of the improper integral is not the sum of the series.

If \(\int_N^\infty f(x)\,dx\) converges, then \(\sum_{n=N}^\infty a_n\) converges; if the integral diverges to \(\infty\), the series diverges.
For \(\sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{1}{n\ln n}\), use \(f(x)=\frac{1}{x\ln x}\). Then \(\int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x\ln x}\,dx=\lim_{b\to\infty}\ln(\ln x)\big|_2^b=\infty\) ⇒ series diverges. For \(\sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{1}{n(\ln n)^2}\), the same substitution gives \(\int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x(\ln x)^2}\,dx=\frac{1}{\ln 2}\) (finite) ⇒ series converges.
Integral Test Checklist
  • Verify \(f\) is positive, continuous, and eventually decreasing on \([N,\infty)\).
  • Align the series lower index with the integral lower limit.
  • Evaluate \(\int_N^\infty f(x)\,dx\) (often \(u=\ln x\) for \(\ln\)-type terms).
Common Mistake

Equating the integral’s numerical value to the series sum, or applying the test when \(f\) is not eventually decreasing (e.g. terms with oscillating signs—integral test needs positive terms).

10.5 Harmonic Series and p-Series (BC)

▼

\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^p}\) converges if and only if \(p>1\). At \(p=1\) you get the harmonic series \(\sum\frac1n\), which diverges even though \(\frac1n\to 0\).

\(p\)-series: converges \(\Leftrightarrow p>1\); diverges for \(p\leq 1\). Famous value (Basel): \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2}=\frac{\pi^2}{6}\).
Series\(p\) or formConclusion
\(\sum 1/n\)\(p=1\)Diverges (harmonic)
\(\sum 1/n^2\)\(p=2\)Converges
\(\sum 1/\sqrt{n}\)\(p=\frac12\)Diverges
\(\sum 1/n^{1.01}\)\(p=1.01\)Converges (barely above \(1\))

10.6 Comparison Tests for Convergence (BC)

▼

Direct comparison (DCT) needs \(0\leq a_n\leq b_n\) termwise (eventually). If \(\sum b_n\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges. If \(\sum a_n\) diverges, then \(\sum b_n\) diverges. If the inequality faces the “wrong” way relative to a known series, DCT gives no conclusion.

Limit comparison (LCT) avoids strict inequalities: for \(a_n,b_n>0\), if \(\displaystyle L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{b_n}\) is finite and positive, \(\sum a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) share convergence behavior. If \(L=0\) and \(\sum b_n\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges; if \(L=\infty\) and \(\sum b_n\) diverges, then \(\sum a_n\) diverges.

Pick \(b_n\) from dominant terms (e.g. \(\frac{3n^2+2}{5n^4+1}\) behaves like \(\frac{1}{n^2}\)). DCT example: \(\frac{1}{n^2+3n+1}\lt\frac{1}{n^2}\) for \(n\geq 1\) and \(\sum\frac1{n^2}\) converges ⇒ original converges.
Choosing a Comparison
  • Strip lower-order terms and constants to guess \(b_n\) (usually geometric or \(p\)-series).
  • Rational-style terms: prefer LCT to a \(p\)-series.
  • When you need a strict upper bound for convergence, use DCT with a convergent \(\sum b_n\).
Common Mistake

From \(a_n\lt b_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) divergent, concluding anything about \(\sum a_n\)—you cannot. Similarly \(a_n>b_n\) with \(\sum b_n\) convergent proves nothing about \(\sum a_n\).

10.7 Alternating Series Test for Convergence (BC)

▼

For \(\sum(-1)^{n+1}b_n\) with \(b_n>0\), the alternating series test (Leibniz-type) says: if \(b_n\) is eventually nonincreasing and \(\lim_{n\to\infty}b_n=0\), then the series converges. The conditions are sufficient, not necessary.

AST: \(b_{n+1}\leq b_n\) for all large \(n\) and \(\lim b_n=0\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(\sum(-1)^{n+1}b_n\) converges.
Alternating harmonic: \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty(-1)^{n+1}\frac1n\) has \(b_n=\frac1n\) decreasing to \(0\) ⇒ converges (to \(\ln 2\), not required on every FRQ).
Common Mistake

Applying AST when signs do not strictly alternate as \((-1)^n b_n\) with a single positive \(b_n\) sequence, or forgetting to verify eventual monotonicity (a derivative test on a continuous extension can help).

10.8 Ratio Test for Convergence (BC)

▼

Let \(L=\lim_{n\to\infty}|a_{n+1}/a_n|\). If \(L<1\), the series converges absolutely; if \(L>1\) or \(L=\infty\), it diverges; if \(L=1\), the ratio test is inconclusive (try \(p\)-series, LCT, integral, or AST).

Ratio: \(\displaystyle L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|\). For \(\sum 1/n^p\), the ratio limit is always \(1\)—do not use the ratio test to classify \(p\)-series.
When the Ratio Test Shines
  • Factorials \(n!\), \((2n)!\), or products like \(n!\,c^n\).
  • Exponential factors \(c^n\) combined with polynomials.
  • Power series coefficients in \(x\) (treat \(|x-a|\) after simplifying the limit).
\(\sum n!/n^n\): ratio simplifies to \(\frac{n^n}{(n+1)^n}=\bigl(1-\frac{1}{n+1}\bigr)^n\to e^{-1}<1\) ⇒ converges absolutely.

10.9 Determining Absolute or Conditional Convergence (BC)

▼

If \(\sum |a_n|\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely (and therefore converges). If \(\sum a_n\) converges but \(\sum|a_n|\) diverges, the convergence is conditional. Check \(\sum|a_n|\) first with ratio, comparison, or integral tests; if that fails, try AST on the original alternating series.

Absolute convergence \(\Rightarrow\) ordinary convergence. Conditional convergence means rearrangements can change the sum (Riemann rearrangement)—absolutely convergent series do not have that pathology.
OutcomeName
\(\sum |a_n|\) convergesAbsolutely convergent
\(\sum |a_n|\) diverges but \(\sum a_n\) convergesConditionally convergent
\(\sum a_n\) divergesDivergent
\(\sum(-1)^{n+1}/n\) is conditional (\(\sum 1/n\) diverges). \(\sum(-1)^{n+1}/n^2\) is absolute (\(\sum 1/n^2\) converges).

10.10 Alternating Series Error Bound (BC)

▼

For an alternating series satisfying AST, the remainder after the \(n\)th partial sum \(S_n\) is bounded by the first omitted term \(b_{n+1}\), not the last term you included. The true sum lies between any two consecutive partial sums.

\(|S-S_n|\leq b_{n+1}\) when AST hypotheses hold (upper bound—actual error can be smaller).
For \(\sum(-1)^{k+1}/k\), to guarantee error \(\leq 0.01\) need \(b_{n+1}=\frac{1}{n+1}\leq 0.01\), so \(n\geq 99\) terms in the partial sum.
Common Mistake

Using \(b_n\) (last included term) instead of \(b_{n+1}\) in the bound, or applying the bound when AST hypotheses fail.

AP-Style Prompt
How many terms of \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^{k+1}/k^2\) are needed so partial sum error is at most \(0.001\)?

10.11 Finding Taylor Polynomial Approximations of Functions (BC)

▼

The Taylor polynomial \(P_n\) of degree \(n\) for \(f\) about \(a\) is the truncation of the Taylor series through the \((x-a)^n\) term: \(P_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k\). A Maclaurin polynomial is the special case \(a=0\).

\(P_n(x)=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\cdots+\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n\). Remainder: \(R_n(x)=f(x)-P_n(x)\).
Coefficient Routine
  • Compute \(f^{(k)}(x)\) through the order you need.
  • Evaluate at the center \(a\).
  • Assemble \(\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k\) for \(k=0,\ldots,n\).

For speed, substitute into standard Maclaurin series (next section) instead of recomputing every derivative when the structure matches \(e^u\), \(\frac{1}{1-u}\), \(\ln(1+u)\), trig, etc.

10.12 Lagrange Error Bound (BC)

▼

If \(P_n\) is the Taylor polynomial of degree \(n\) for \(f\) about \(a\), the remainder is \(R_n(x)=f(x)-P_n(x)\). When \(|f^{(n+1)}(t)|\leq M\) for all \(t\) between \(a\) and \(x\), Lagrange’s inequality bounds \(|R_n(x)|\).

\(\displaystyle |R_n(x)|\leq \frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1}\), where \(M\) is any upper bound for \(|f^{(n+1)}|\) on the interval from \(a\) to \(x\).
Lagrange Bound Routine
  • Identify degree \(n\) of \(P_n\) and center \(a\).
  • Differentiate one more time: \(f^{(n+1)}\).
  • Bound \(|f^{(n+1)}(t)|\) on the segment between \(a\) and \(x\); call the bound \(M\).
  • Plug into \(\frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1}\).
Approximate \(e\) with \(P_3\) for \(e^x\) at \(a=0\) evaluated at \(x=1\): \(f^{(4)}(x)=e^x\), on \([0,1]\) take \(M=e\) (or a cruder safe bound like \(3\)). Then \(|R_3(1)|\leq \frac{M}{4!}=\frac{M}{24}\).
Common Mistake

Using \(f^{(n)}\) instead of \(f^{(n+1)}\), or mismatching factorial/power with the polynomial degree.

10.13 Radius and Interval of Convergence of Power Series (BC)

▼

For \(\sum c_n(x-a)^n\), the ratio test on the general term (treating \(x\) as fixed) gives an interval where the limit is \(\lt 1\): typically \(|x-a|\lt R\) with absolute convergence inside, divergence outside, and endpoints unknown until tested in the original series.

Three outcomes: \(R=0\) (converges only at \(x=a\)), \(0\lt R\lt\infty\) (finite interval; check \(x=a\pm R\)), or \(R=\infty\) (converges for all \(x\)).
\(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n}\): ratio gives \(|x|\lim\frac{n}{n+1}=|x|\), so \(R=1\). At \(x=1\): harmonic ⇒ diverges. At \(x=-1\): alternating harmonic ⇒ converges. Interval of convergence \([-1,1)\)—use \([\ ]\) only where the series converges.
Interval of Convergence
  • Apply ratio or root test to \(\sum|c_n||x-a|^n\) and solve \(|x-a|\lt R\).
  • Substitute \(x=a+R\) and \(x=a-R\) into the original series separately.
  • After term-by-term differentiation or integration of a known series, \(R\) is unchanged but endpoints must be retested.

10.14 Finding Taylor or Maclaurin Series for a Function (BC)

▼

Build \(\sum \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n\) by differentiating \(f\) repeatedly, or—faster—substitute into standard Maclaurin forms with \(u\) a function of \(x\) (e.g. \(e^{x^2}\) from \(e^u\) with \(u=x^2\)).

\(f(x)\)Maclaurin seriesInterval (standard)
\(e^x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}\)\((-\infty,\infty)\)
\(\sin x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty(-1)^n\frac{x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}\)\((-\infty,\infty)\)
\(\cos x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty(-1)^n\frac{x^{2n}}{(2n)!}\)\((-\infty,\infty)\)
\(\dfrac{1}{1-x}\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n\)\((-1,1)\)
\(\ln(1+x)\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty(-1)^{n+1}\frac{x^n}{n}\)\((-1,1]\)
\(\arctan x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty(-1)^n\frac{x^{2n+1}}{2n+1}\)\([-1,1]\)
Substitution: \(\frac{1}{1+x^2}=\sum(-1)^n x^{2n}\) for \(|x|\lt 1\); \(\cos(3x)=\sum(-1)^n\frac{(3x)^{2n}}{(2n)!}\).
Common Mistake

Reporting the open interval \((-1,1)\) from the ratio step as the final answer for a series whose endpoint \(x=-1\) converges by AST—always substitute endpoints.

10.15 Representing Functions as Power Series (BC)

▼

Inside the interval of convergence, power series may be differentiated and integrated term-by-term. The radius \(R\) is unchanged, but endpoints can change: integration often improves convergence at a boundary that was previously divergent (differentiation can worsen it).

If \(f(x)=\sum c_n(x-a)^n\) for \(|x-a|\lt R\), then \(f'(x)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty n c_n(x-a)^{n-1}\) and \(\int f(x)\,dx=C+\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n\frac{(x-a)^{n+1}}{n+1}\), each with the same \(R\) until endpoint retest.
From \(\frac{1}{1-x}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n\) on \((-1,1)\), integrate to \(-\ln(1-x)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{x^n}{n}\); at \(x=-1\) the alternating series converges, so the integrated series represents \(-\ln(1-x)\) on \([-1,1)\).
