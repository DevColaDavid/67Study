Unit 1: Atomic Structure and Properties

How To Use This Unit

Build moles and composition (1.1–1.4), then electron-level models and evidence (1.5–1.6), then periodic patterns and ion formation (1.7–1.8). On every problem: name particles and units first, then apply the equation or trend.

1.1 Moles and Molar Mass

▼

The mole is the chemist’s count of Avogadro’s number of identical particles. Amedeo Avogadro (early 1800s) proposed equal volumes of gases at the same T and P hold equal numbers of particles; that idea became the foundation for relating macroscopic mass to atom counts. The value \(N_A \approx 6.022 \times 10^{23}\ \mathrm{mol^{-1}}\) is named the Avogadro constant.

Molar mass \(M\) (unit \(\mathrm{g/mol}\)) is the mass of one mole of a substance—numerically the same as average atomic or molecular mass in amu, but with \(\mathrm{g/mol}\) for stoichiometry. In \(n = \frac{m}{M}\), \(n\) = amount (moles), \(m\) = sample mass (g), \(M\) = molar mass (g/mol). For a compound, add atomic contributions from the formula.

\(n = \dfrac{m}{M}\), particles \(= n \times N_A\)
How To Solve Mole Conversions
  • Write given quantity with units; circle the unknown.
  • Multiply by a conversion so unwanted units cancel (mole bridge or \(N_A\)).
  • Report with correct significant figures; never round before the final step on multi-step problems.
Example: Molar mass of \({\mathrm{Ca}}\bigl(\mathrm{OH}\bigr)_{2} = 40.08 + 2(16.00) + 2(1.008) = 74.10\ \mathrm{g/mol}\). Mass of \(0.150\ \mathrm{mol}\) is \(m = nM = 0.150 \times 74.10 = 11.1\ \mathrm{g}\).
Common Mistake

Inverting the factor \(\frac{1\ \mathrm{mol}}{M\ \mathrm{g}}\). Place the unit you want in the numerator so the given unit cancels. Also: distinguish formula units in ionic solids from molecules—use the same mole math, but language must match the substance.

1.2 Mass Spectra of Elements

▼

Mass spectrometry ionizes atoms or molecules, sorts them by mass-to-charge ratio (\(m/z\); often \(z=1\)), and plots relative abundance. That is how chemists weigh isotopes in mixture. Isotopes of an element have the same number of protons, different numbers of neutrons—hence different masses.

Periodic table atomic mass is a weighted average of naturally occurring isotopes, not the mass of the most common isotope. If isotope \(i\) has mass \(m_i\) and fractional abundance (decimal) \(f_i\), then

\(\text{Average atomic mass} = \sum_i f_i m_i\)

Here \(f_i\) are the fractional abundances (sum to 1.0); on a spectrum, relative peak heights or areas (after calibration) stand in for the \(f_i\). Peak position (x-axis) gives \(m/z\), not abundance.

Based on the schematic above, the tallest peak is the most abundant isotope; the weighted mass lies between 24–26 depending on the \(f_i\).

Example: Two isotopes with masses 10.0 and 11.0 and abundances 0.20 and 0.80: average \(= 0.20(10.0) + 0.80(11.0) = 10.8\ \mathrm{amu}\).
Common Mistake

Using percent abundances (e.g. 20%) as if they were decimals. Divide by 100 first. Or confusing peak y-value with isotope mass—mass is read on the horizontal axis in \(m/z\).

1.3 Elemental Composition of Pure Substances

▼

Law of definite proportions: a given compound always contains elements in a fixed mass ratio. Empirical formula gives the smallest whole-number atom ratio. Molecular formula = integer multiple of the empirical formula, where that integer is the ratio of molar mass of the real molecule to empirical formula mass.

Given mass \% of each element: convert to moles, divide by the smallest mole amount to get a ratio, then scale to whole numbers if needed (multiply by 2, 3, … to clear 0.33, 0.5, etc.).

How To Find Empirical Formula from Mass \%
  • Assume a 100 g sample so mass \% = grams of each element.
  • \(n = m/M\) for each element using atomic masses.
  • Divide all \(n\) by the smallest; round carefully (ratios 1.99 ≈ 2 if within experimental error).
StepOperationMeaning of symbols
1g \(\rightarrow\) mol\(m\) in g, \(M\) in g/mol
2normalizedivide by smallest mol amount
3molecular (optional)factor \(= \frac{M_{\mathrm{molar}}}{M_{\mathrm{empirical}}}\)
Example: A compound is 40.0\% C, 6.7\% H, 53.3\% O by mass. Moles: C \(40.0/12.01=3.33\), H \(6.7/1.008=6.6\), O \(53.3/16.00=3.33\). Ratio C:H:O \(=1:2:1\) → empirical \(\mathrm{CH_2O}\).
Common Mistake

Reporting \(\mathrm{CH_2O}\) as the molecular formula when the true molar mass is 180 g/mol—the molecular formula is \(\mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6}\) (factor 6). Always check with given molar mass if provided.

1.4 Composition of Mixtures

▼

A mixture contains two or more substances physically combined; its composition can vary. Homogeneous (solution): uniform on the scale of observation. Heterogeneous: distinct regions (phases) visible. Pure substances: fixed composition (element or compound).

Mass percent of component A: \(\text{mass \%} = \frac{m_{\mathrm{A}}}{m_{\mathrm{total}}} \times 100\%\). Mole fraction and molarity appear in later units; here focus on mass-based descriptions and dilute vs concentrated qualitatively.

\(\text{mass \%} = \dfrac{m_{\mathrm{component}}}{m_{\mathrm{total}}} \times 100\%\)
How To Track Mixture Problems
  • Identify what is the total sample (denominator) vs one part (numerator).
  • For a solution, distinguish solute, solvent, and solution total mass or volume (careful with unit consistency).
An 80.0 g aqueous sample contains 12.0 g NaCl. Mass \% NaCl \(= 12.0/80.0 \times 100\% = 15.0\%\). The rest is mostly water and any other solutes.
Common Mistake

Using the solvent mass in the denominator instead of total solution mass. The percent is “parts per hundred of the whole mixture” unless a problem defines otherwise.

1.5 Atomic Structure and Electron Configuration

▼

Atomic number \(Z\): protons; defines the element. In a neutral atom, electrons = protons. Mass number \(A\): protons + neutrons. Isotopes differ in neutron count, so their masses differ, not \(Z\).

Electrons are ordered by the Aufbau (build-up) principle (fill lowest energy orbitals first), Pauli exclusion (max two electrons per orbital with opposite spin), and Hund’s rule (degenerate orbitals: single occupancies first with parallel spins). The periodic table’s block structure (s, p, d, f) matches orbital filling order (approximate: \(1s \rightarrow 2s \rightarrow 2p \rightarrow 3s \rightarrow 3p \rightarrow 4s \rightarrow 3d \rightarrow \ldots\)).

Noble-gas shorthand: \([\mathrm{Ar}]\) represents the electron configuration of argon, then add valence electrons (e.g. \([\mathrm{Ar}]\,4s^2 3d^5\) for \(\mathrm{Mn}\)).

How To Write a Ground-State Configuration
  • Count total electrons (ion charge adjusts: cation = fewer e\(^-\)).
  • Fill subshells in order; d-block often follows \((n-1)d\) after \(ns\) for the first row of transition metals.
  • Check for exceptions (Cr, Cu) only if a question supplies unusual stability reasoning.
Sulfur (\(Z=16\)): \(1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^4\), or \([\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^2 3p^4\). Valence (highest n): 6 e\(^-\) in \(3s/3p\).
Why It Matters

Valence configuration predicts bonding, ion charges, and trends in size and reactivity—the rest of the course depends on it.

1.6 Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

▼

PES (photoelectric effect on gaseous atoms) measures binding energy (BE) of electrons: the minimum photon energy needed to eject an electron from a sublevel, corrected for the electron’s measured kinetic energy. Higher BE means the electron is held tighter (closer to nucleus, or in a more strongly attracted subshell in the same shell).

Each peak corresponds to a sublevel; peak area (relative) is proportional to the number of electrons in that sublevel (e.g. \(1s\) area : \(2p\) area \(=2:6\) for neon’s full \(2p\)).

How To Read a PES Plot
  • Horizontal: binding energy (high BE at right or left—track your axis; AP often higher BE to the left).
  • Vertical: signal intensity—compare relative areas, not “which peak is higher on the y-axis” for energy.
  • Match known shell structure: 1s, 2s, 2p groups for the first two periods; transition metals add 3d under 4s, etc.
Sodium has a small \(3s^1\) peak and filled inner shells; magnesium adds one more electron in \(3s\), so the \(3s\) peak has roughly double the \(3s\) area of Na relative to other subshells when normalized.
Common Mistake

Using peak height as “more binding energy”. Position on the binding energy axis gives energy. Area reflects electron count, not how tightly a single average electron is held in that label.

1.7 Periodic Trends

▼

Effective nuclear charge (\(Z_{\mathrm{eff}}\)): the net positive charge “felt” by valence electrons after shielding from inner shells. It increases across a period (more protons, similar shielding) and is the main reason radius shrinks and ionization energy and electronegativity generally rise across a period.

Down a group, new shells add distance and shielding: atomic radius increases; first ionization energy and electronegativity generally decrease. (Small exceptions, e.g. O/S IE, are rare on AP—cite shielding and pairing if asked.)

TrendAcross period (→)Down group (↓)
Atomic radiusdecreasesincreases
First IEincreases (general)decreases (general)
Electronegativityincreasesdecreases

Ionic radius: cations are smaller than parent atoms; anions are larger—compare to the same isoelectronic series when possible.

Ionic size in an isoelectronic series: compare \(\mathrm{Na^+}\), \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{F^-}\) (all Ne-like). The highest nuclear charge pulls electrons most tightly—small radii. Order: \(r(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}) \lt r(\mathrm{Na^+}) \lt r(\mathrm{F^-})\).

1.8 Valence Electrons and Ionic Compounds

▼

Valence electrons (main-group) often equal the group number (old US A/AA notation: 1–2, 13–18) for the s and p valence set. They drive bonding and common ion charge patterns. Metals (left/bottom) tend to lose electrons to form cations; nonmetals (right/top) tend to gain electrons to form anions—to reach a noble-gas valence or a stable half/full subshell in transition metals (special cases).

Electroneutrality: the sum of positive and negative charge in a formula unit is zero. The “criss-cross” method (charge magnitudes as subscripts) is a shortcut for ionic charge balance: \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{O^{2-}}\) give \(\mathrm{CaO}\); \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\) and \(\mathrm{O^{2-}}\) need \(\mathrm{Al_2O_3}\).

How To Write a Binary Ionic Formula
  • Write ion symbols with charges from group trend (or a table).
  • Find the smallest whole numbers \(a,b\) so \(|a \times c_+| = |b \times c_-|\) for total charge zero.
  • Parentheses for polyatomic ions: \({\mathrm{Ca}}\bigl(\mathrm{OH}\bigr)_{2}\), not \(\mathrm{CaOH_2}\).
\(\mathrm{Na^+ + Cl^-} \rightarrow \mathrm{NaCl}\). For \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) and \(({\mathrm{SO}_4})^{2-}\), cross ratios give \({\mathrm{Fe}}_{2}\bigl(\mathrm{SO}_{4}\bigr)_{3}\).
Common Mistake

Treating covalent molecular formulas (e.g. \(\mathrm{P_4O_{10}}\)) as if they were simple ionic charge balance—identify whether the problem states ionic lattice vs molecular covalent.

Unit 1: Practice (AP-Style)

▼
How these are written

Short-answer and multi-step prompts in the spirit of the AP Chemistry exam: show work, justify with evidence, watch significant figures. Original items (not copied from released exams).

1. (Stoichiometry) A sample of dry ice contains \(4.40\ \mathrm{g}\) of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\). (a) Calculate the amount of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) in moles. (b) How many C atoms are in the sample? Show setup using Avogadro’s number.
(a) \(n = 4.40\ \mathrm{g} / 44.01\ \mathrm{g/mol} = 0.100\ \mathrm{mol}\ \mathrm{CO_2}\) (3 sig figs). (b) 1 formula unit of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) has 1 C, so C atoms \(= 0.100\ \mathrm{mol} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}\ \mathrm{mol^{-1}} = 6.02 \times 10^{22}\) (3 s.f.).
2. (Isotopes) An element has two isotopes: mass \(63.0\ \mathrm{amu}\) with abundance \(0.690\) and mass \(65.0\ \mathrm{amu}\) with abundance \(0.310\). (a) Calculate the weighted average atomic mass. (b) A student used \(63 \times 69 + 65 \times 31\). What error did they make?
(a) Use decimal abundances: \(0.690(63.0) + 0.310(65.0) = 43.47 + 20.15 = 63.62\ \mathrm{amu}\) (report 63.6 if limited by data). (b) They treated percentages as if they were not divided by 100, and they omitted adding the two terms correctly—always convert \% to fraction first.
3. (PES) The PES of a gaseous atom shows a peak labeled \(1s\) to the right of a peak labeled \(2p\) (higher BE on the right on this axis). (a) Which subshell’s electrons require more energy to remove? (b) In one sentence, relate that to effective nuclear charge and distance from the nucleus.
(a) The \(1s\) subshell. (b) The \(1s\) electrons are closer to the nucleus and less shielded, so they experience a larger effective nuclear charge and a higher first ionization energy for that sublevel, matching the higher binding energy on the spectrum.
4. (Trends) Which of the following has the greatest first ionization energy: \(\mathrm{K}\), \(\mathrm{Ca}\), or \(\mathrm{Br}\)? Justify across a period in terms of \(Z_{\mathrm{eff}}\) and valence energy.
\(\mathrm{Br}\) has the greatest first IE: all are in period 4; as atomic number increases across the period, valence \(Z_{\mathrm{eff}}\) increases with similar shielding, so valence electrons are bound more tightly. Order: \(IE(\mathrm{K}) \lt IE(\mathrm{Ca}) \lt IE(\mathrm{Br})\); \(\mathrm{Br}\) is rightmost, so it wins.
