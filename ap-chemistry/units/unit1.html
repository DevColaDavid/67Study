Unit 1: Atomic Structure and Properties

How To Use This Unit

Start with moles and composition (1.1-1.4), then move to atom/electron models (1.5-1.6), and finish with periodic patterns and ions (1.7-1.8). If a problem feels hard, check units first, then check particle meaning.

1.1 Moles and Molar Mass

▼
  • 1 mole = \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) particles (Avogadro constant).
  • Molar mass uses periodic table mass in \(\mathrm{g/mol}\).
  • Dimensional analysis tracks units and cancels correctly.
How To Solve
  • Write what you have and what you need.
  • Use a conversion factor so the old unit cancels.
  • Keep 3-4 significant figures unless instructed otherwise.
Example: \(24.0\text{ g Mg}\times\frac{1\text{ mol Mg}}{24.31\text{ g Mg}}=0.987\text{ mol Mg}\)
Common Mistake

Students invert the conversion factor. Put the target unit on top so the starting unit cancels.

1.2 Mass Spectra of Elements

▼
  • Mass spectrum peaks identify isotopes by mass-to-charge ratio.
  • Peak height/area corresponds to relative abundance.
  • Average atomic mass is a weighted average of isotopic masses.
\(\text{Average atomic mass} = \sum (\text{isotope mass})(\text{fractional abundance})\)
Why It Matters

This idea explains why periodic table masses are decimals instead of whole numbers.

1.3 Elemental Composition of Pure Substances

▼
  • Law of definite proportions: a pure compound has fixed element mass ratios.
  • Empirical formula gives lowest whole-number atom ratio.
  • Molecular formula is an integer multiple of empirical formula.
How To Solve
  • Convert each element mass to moles.
  • Divide all mole values by the smallest value.
  • Scale to whole numbers and write empirical formula.

1.4 Composition of Mixtures

▼
  • Mixtures have variable composition (unlike pure substances).
  • Homogeneous mixtures are uniform; heterogeneous are nonuniform.
  • Mass percent can describe mixture composition.
\(\text{mass percent} = \frac{\text{mass of component}}{\text{total mass}} \times 100\%\)
If a 80.0 g solution has 12.0 g NaCl, then mass % NaCl is \(15.0\%\).

1.5 Atomic Structure and Electron Configuration

▼
  • Atomic number gives proton count; mass number is protons + neutrons.
  • Electron configuration follows Aufbau, Pauli exclusion, and Hund's rule.
  • Ground-state configuration can be written in noble-gas shorthand.
Why It Matters

Electron arrangement is the bridge between atomic structure and chemical reactivity.

1.6 Photoelectron Spectroscopy

▼
  • PES peaks represent electrons in different energy sublevels.
  • Higher binding energy means electrons are held more strongly.
  • Peak area is proportional to number of electrons in that sublevel.
Common Mistake

Confusing peak height with energy. Peak position gives binding energy; area/relative size gives electron count.

1.7 Periodic Trends

▼
  • Atomic radius decreases across a period and increases down a group.
  • Ionization energy generally increases across and decreases down.
  • Electronegativity generally increases across and decreases down.

1.8 Valence Electrons and Ionic Compounds

▼
  • Main-group valence electrons correlate with periodic table group.
  • Metals tend to form cations; nonmetals tend to form anions.
  • Ionic compounds are electrically neutral overall.

Unit 1 Example Questions and Solutions

▼
Q1: How many moles are in 48.6 g of NaCl?
A: \(n=\frac{m}{M}=\frac{48.6}{58.44}=0.832\text{ mol}\).
Q2: If isotopes are 75% at 24 amu and 25% at 25 amu, what is average mass?
A: \(0.75(24)+0.25(25)=24.25\text{ amu}\).
Q3: Why does ionization energy increase across a period?
A: Effective nuclear charge increases, so electrons are held more tightly.
