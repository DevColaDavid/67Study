Unit 2: Compound Structure and Properties

Big Picture

This unit connects electron behavior to real properties. The core AP workflow is: determine bonding and structure first, then predict polarity, intermolecular behavior, and material properties.

2.1 Types of Chemical Bonds

▼

Bonding models answer: where are the valence electrons and how is charge distributed. That in turn explains melting point, hardness, malleability, and conductivity—topics AP links to structure in MCQ and particulate items.

Ionic: transfer of electrons (metal → nonmetal) gives ions in a 3D lattice. Forces are electrostatic (Coulomb) between cations and anions; the solid has no discrete “molecules” in the lattice (formula unit = smallest ratio).

Covalent (molecular and network): valence pairs are shared between nonmetals (molecular) or in an extended network (diamond, quartz). Nonpolar covalent: equal pull on shared pairs; polar covalent: shift toward the more electronegative atom (partial \(\delta^+\) / \(\delta^-\)).

Metallic: cations in a sea of delocalized valence electrons; it explains luster, conductivity, and malleability (slip planes without shattering covalent networks).

Bond classWhat holds the structureAP-style property
IonicIon-ion attraction in a latticeHigh melting point, brittle, conducts molten or aqueous
Covalent (molecular)Shared pairs within each molecule; IMFs between moleculesOften lower MP than network covalent
MetallicDelocalized e\(^-\) among cationsConducts, malleable, metallic luster
Example: \(\mathrm{NaCl}\) is ionic; \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is covalent molecular; \(\mathrm{Fe}\) is metallic. \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) (quartz) is covalent network—very high MP because many strong bonds must be broken to separate atoms.
Common Mistake

Labeling a compound “ionic” or “covalent” as all-or-nothing. Many bonds are polar covalent—the percent ionic character depends on \(\Delta\chi\) (see Unit 3 and electronegativity). Also: giant covalent network vs small molecules—both are “covalent” but properties differ enormously.

2.2 Intramolecular Force and Potential Energy

▼

Between two bound atoms, potential energy (stored in the bond) is lowest at the equilibrium bond length \(r_e\). Long distance: little attraction, high \(U\). Closer: nucleus-electron attractions dominate and \(U\) drops. Very short distance: repulsion between closed shells and between nuclei sends \(U\) up steeply—the curve is a Morse-like well; AP shows simplified curves with a single minimum.

Bond dissociation energy (BDE): energy required to break one mole of bonds in the gas phase homolytically (often to radicals). A deep well with a long bond or strong BDE: stronger bond; the depth is related to how much must be put in to reach separated atoms. Do not confuse bond energy with IMF energy—IMFs in Unit 3 are much smaller in magnitude (often a few percent or less) than of a typical covalent bond.

How To Read a Bond-Energy vs Separation Diagram
  • Horizontal axis: internuclear distance; minimum = equilibrium bond length.
  • Vertical: potential energy. Lower = more stable bound state (relative to separated atoms, depending on the diagram’s reference).
  • Steeper right wall: short-range repulsion; shallow outer curve: long-range attractions.
A shorter bond in \(\mathrm{N_2}\) (triple) vs \(\mathrm{F_2}\) (single) correlates with higher BDE and the triple bond’s stronger net attraction—compare bond orders after Lewis/VSEPR work.

2.3 Structure of Ionic Solids

▼

Ionic substances crystallize in repeating 3D lattices (e.g. rock salt NaCl structure). A formula unit is the smallest whole-number charge-neutral ratio—not a “molecule” drifting alone.

Lattice enthalpy (treated quantitatively in some courses): more negative (more exothermically stable lattice) for higher charges and smaller radii—ions can pack closer, and \(\mathrm{F \propto q_+ q_- / r^2}\) in a simplified picture. In AP, predict trends: higher charge (\(\pm 2, \mp 1\) etc.) and smaller ions mean stronger lattices and often higher melting points than comparable \(\pm1\) cases.

Conductivity: fixed ions in a solid cannot migrate; in molten or dissolved form they move—hence conduction. Brittle: layers slip so like charges align and repel (cleavage, shattering) unlike metals.

How To Predict or Explain Ionic Properties
  • Identify cation/anion charges and their sizes (trends: smaller ion = stronger field).
  • Link lattice strength to MP and hardness qualitatively.
  • Distinguish solid vs solution vs molten for conductivity in FRQ “particulate reasoning.”
Common Mistake

Saying ionic bonds “form molecules of NaCl” in the solid—use “formula units” and lattice. Also, melting ionic does break interionic associations but does not “break covalent bonds” in NaCl—there are no covalent bonds in NaCl.

2.4 Structure of Metals and Alloys

▼

Metals pack in lattices (e.g. cubic close-packed) with valence delocalized over the whole crystal. Substitutional alloy: a host metal atom is replaced by another with similar size (e.g. some brass, bronze)—strength and malleability change with defect pattern. Interstitial alloy: small atoms (C in steel) sit in voids, blocking slip and increasing the material’s hardness.

Compared to pure metal, an alloy’s electrical conductivity can decrease slightly (electron scattering at defects) while mechanical strength can increase—AP may ask to justify both for the same material.

TypeStructural ideaExample effect
SubstitutionalSame lattice sites, different atomSolid-solution hardening, varied corrosion
InterstitialSmall atom in a hole in the host latticeSteel: C in Fe improves hardness
Carbon steel: interstitial C impedes dislocation motion—harder, less malleable than pure Fe in many compositions.

2.5 Lewis Diagrams

▼

Gilbert N. Lewis (1916) used dot pairs to show shared and lone valence pairs. AP expects octet for C, N, O, F (H obeys duet; third-row and below can expand octet when needed—see \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\), \(\mathrm{SF_6}\) with VSEPR in 2.7).

Lewis Workflow (systematic)
  • Count valence e\(^-\): sum atom valences, add one e\(^-\) per \(-1\) charge, subtract for \(+\) on polyatomic cations (e.g. \(({\mathrm{NH}_4})^{+}\)).
  • Central atom: least electronegative (C over O in oxyanions), H and halogens terminal.
  • Connect with single bonds; complete octets on outer atoms first; place leftover on central; form multiple bonds if central lacks octet and reasonable.
  • Check total: sum of e\(^-\) in structure = initial count. Assign formal charge if comparing alternatives (2.6).

Resonance and formal charge (next section) decide between contributing structures. Odd-electron, radicals, and coordinate bonds appear on some exams—pair electrons in order of the algorithm above, then use FC to choose best drawing.

\(\mathrm{CO_2}\): C central, 16 valence e\(^-\) total, double bonds on both sides, no FC on C if drawn symmetrically. \(\mathrm{O_2}\) needs a double bond; \(\mathrm{N_2}\) triple—match bond order to experiment where asked.

2.6 Resonance and Formal Charge

▼

When a single classical Lewis form undervalues e\(^-\) delocalization (e.g. \(\mathrm{O_3}\), \(({\mathrm{NO}_3})^{-}\), \(\mathrm{O_2}\)), resonance = multiple valence-bond images that together describe one real hybrid—electrons are not zipping back and forth; the true wavefunction has lowered energy vs any one drawing.

\(FC = V - N - B/2\): \(V\)= valence e\(^-\) of the free atom, \(N\)=lone e\(^-\) on the atom, \(B\)=e\(^-\) in bonds touching that atom (count shared pairs as the whole pair).

Same formula as: \(\text{formal charge}=\text{valence e}^- - (\text{nonbonding e}^- + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{bonding e}^-)\). Prefer lower |FC| overall; place negative FC on the more EN atom and positive on less EN when you must break ties. For \(\mathrm{CO}\), the structure with a formal negative on O and positive on C is a major contributor because O is more electronegative than C, despite both having octets in some alternates.

How To Choose Best Resonance Contributor
  • Octet rule satisfied (where applicable).
  • Minimize FC magnitudes; avoid like charges on adjacent atoms in same drawing if avoidable.
  • Negative on EN atoms; for \(({\mathrm{NO}_2})^{-}\) and \(({\mathrm{NO}_3})^{-}\) expect equal N-O bond lengths in the true ion (average of resonance).
In \(({\mathrm{NO}_3})^{-}\), three oxygens share a bond order of 4/3 to N—all N-O lengths equal. Experimental bond length is between single and double.
Common Mistake

Drawing a double bond to two different O’s and assuming one is “the real one”—the ion is a single averaged structure; for AP, discuss delocalized \(\pi\) in qualitative terms. Also: confusing FC with oxidation number—different bookkeeping rules (oxidation: assume ionic limit).

2.7 VSEPR and Bond Hybridization

▼

VSEPR means valence-shell electron-pair repulsion: electron domains spread out to minimize repulsion. This section should feel procedural so students can solve any geometry question from scratch.

How To Solve VSEPR Questions
  • Draw Lewis structure first.
  • Count electron domains around central atom (each bond = one domain, each lone pair = one domain).
  • Assign electron geometry from domain count.
  • Assign molecular geometry using only atom positions (lone pairs hidden in shape name).
  • Assign common hybridization by domain count: 2 -> \(sp\), 3 -> \(sp^2\), 4 -> \(sp^3\).
DomainsElectron GeometryMolecular Geometry ExamplesHybridization (AP)
2LinearLinear (e.g. \(\mathrm{CO_2}\))\(sp\)
3Trigonal planarTrigonal planar, Bent (e.g. \(\mathrm{SO_2}\))\(sp^2\)
4TetrahedralTetrahedral, Trigonal pyramidal, Bent (e.g. \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\))\(sp^3\)
5Trigonal bipyramidalSeesaw, T-shaped, linear (e.g. \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\), \(\mathrm{SF_4}\), \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\), \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\))\(sp^3d\)
6OctahedralOctahedral, Square pyramidal, Square planar (e.g. \(\mathrm{SF_6}\), \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\), \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\))\(sp^3d^2\)

Lone pairs occupy more space than bond pairs—they reduce bond angles next to them (e.g. \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) ~104.5° vs ideal tetrahedral 109.5°).

Common Mistake

Students memorize shape names but skip domain counting. Always count domains first. Geometry and hybridization are outputs of that count.

Unit 2: Practice (AP-Style)

▼
How these are written

Items follow the style of AP long/short free response: structure → property → explanation, and formal charges when Lewis structures are involved. Original practice only.

1. (Polarity) For \(\mathrm{CO_2(g)}\) and \(\mathrm{SO_2(g)}\): (a) State the VSEPR molecular geometry (including number of lone pairs on the central atom). (b) Predict whether each molecule is polar or nonpolar and justify using bond dipoles and shape.
(a) \(\mathrm{CO_2}\): two bonding domains, zero lone pairs on C—linear. \(\mathrm{SO_2}\): three electron domains, one lone pair on S—bent. (b) \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) is nonpolar (identical C=O bond dipoles, \(180^\circ\), net dipole 0). \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) is polar (bent geometry; S-O dipoles do not cancel).
2. (VSEPR) For one \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) molecule: (a) Give the electron-domain geometry, molecular geometry, and bond angle around N (as expected from VSEPR, vs ideal tetrahedral). (b) State the hybridization of the central atom.
(a) Four electron domains → tetrahedral electron geometry; trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry; bond angle a bit less than \(109.5^\circ\) because the lone pair repels more. (b) \(sp^3\) on N (four hybrid orbitals for four domains).
3. (Resonance) The experimental N–O bond length in the nitrate ion, \(({\mathrm{NO}_3})^{-}\), is the same for all three oxygen atoms. (a) Explain in terms of resonance and bond order. (b) State whether the true ion is best represented as a single Lewis structure.
(a) The ion is a resonance hybrid of three equivalent forms; the \(\pi\) electron density is delocalized—each N–O has an average order between single and double (e.g. \(1\frac{1}{3}\) on average), so all bonds have the same length. (b) No; one drawing alone is a simplification—the real species has delocalized bonding.
4. (Formal charge) The CO Lewis structure with a triple bond and a lone pair on each atom gives \(FC(\mathrm{C})=+1\) and \(FC(\mathrm{O})=-1\). (a) Identify the most important contributor and explain (octet + charge placement). (b) A student places \(-1\) on C and \(+1\) on O—why is that a weaker contributor?
(a) The triple-bond, octet-satisfied structure is major; the negative formal charge should sit on the more electronegative atom (O), not C. (b) Placing a negative charge on the less EN carbon is destabilizing; formal charge should preferentially reside on the more EN atom in comparable octet structures.
