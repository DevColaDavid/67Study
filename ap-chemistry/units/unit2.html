Unit 2: Compound Structure and Properties

Big Picture

This unit explains why atoms stick together the way they do. If Unit 1 tells you what atoms are, Unit 2 tells you how atomic structure becomes real chemical behavior.

2.1 Types of Chemical Bonds

▼
  • Ionic: electron transfer, electrostatic attraction.
  • Covalent: electron sharing between atoms.
  • Metallic: cations in a sea of delocalized electrons.
Why It Matters

Bond type predicts conductivity, melting point, hardness, and solubility.

2.2 Intramolecular Force and Potential Energy

▼
  • Bond formation lowers potential energy to a minimum at bond length.
  • Shorter than equilibrium distance gives strong repulsion.
  • Bond dissociation requires energy input.

2.3 Structure of Ionic Solids

▼
  • Ionic solids form repeating 3D lattices, not discrete molecules.
  • Lattice energy scales with charge magnitude and ion separation.
  • Many ionic solids are brittle and high-melting.

2.4 Structure of Metals and Alloys

▼
  • Metal bonding explains conductivity and malleability.
  • Alloys are mixtures of metals (or metal + nonmetal) with altered properties.
  • Substitutional and interstitial alloys are common models.

2.5 Lewis Diagrams

▼
  • Count total valence electrons first.
  • Connect atoms, then complete octets where applicable.
  • Hydrogen follows duet rule (2 electrons).
How To Solve
  • Choose central atom (usually least electronegative, not H).
  • Draw single bonds first.
  • Add lone pairs to satisfy octet, then create multiple bonds if needed.

2.6 Resonance and Formal Charge

▼
  • Resonance forms differ only in electron placement, not atom positions.
  • Best contributors minimize formal charge magnitude and place negative charge on more electronegative atoms.
\(\text{formal charge} = \text{valence e}^- - (\text{nonbonding e}^- + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{bonding e}^-)\)

2.7 VSEPR and Bond Hybridization

▼
  • Electron domains predict geometry: linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, trigonal bipyramidal, octahedral.
  • Common hybridizations: sp, sp2, sp3.
  • Molecular polarity depends on bond polarity plus geometry.
Common Mistake

Students confuse electron geometry and molecular geometry. Lone pairs count for geometry setup, but they are not listed as visible atoms in molecular shape names.

Unit 2 Extended Notes

▼
More Detail By Topic
  • 2.1: Electronegativity difference helps estimate ionic vs polar/nonpolar covalent behavior.
  • 2.2: Bond length corresponds to potential-energy minimum where attraction and repulsion balance.
  • 2.3-2.4: Ionic solids and metals differ in electron mobility, so electrical behavior differs strongly.
  • 2.5-2.7: Lewis structure -> formal charge/resonance -> VSEPR/hybridization is the core workflow.

Unit 2 Example Questions and Solutions

▼
Q1: Why does NaCl conduct when molten but not as a solid?
A: In solid NaCl ions are fixed in the lattice; when molten, ions can move and carry charge.
Q2: Which likely has shorter bond length: \(N\equiv N\) or \(N-N\)?
A: \(N\equiv N\), because higher bond order corresponds to shorter, stronger bonds.
Q3: What is the electron-domain geometry for four electron groups?
A: Tetrahedral electron geometry.
