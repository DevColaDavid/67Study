Unit 8: Acids and Bases

Big Picture

Unit 8 is mostly equilibrium with acid/base language. Keep track of species first, then choose strong-acid shortcuts or weak-acid equilibrium methods.

8.1 Introduction to Acids and Bases

▼
  • Arrhenius: acids produce H+, bases produce OH- in water.
  • Bronsted-Lowry: acids donate protons, bases accept protons.

8.2 pH and pOH of Strong Acids and Bases

▼
\(\mathrm{pH=-\log[H^+],\ pOH=-\log[OH^-],\ pH+pOH=14.00\ (25^\circ C)}\)
How To Solve
  • For strong monoprotic acids, \([H^+]\approx C_{\text{acid}}\).
  • For strong bases, get \([OH^-]\) first, then convert to pOH and pH.
  • Use \(pH+pOH=14\) only at \(25^\circ C\).

8.3 Weak Acid and Base Equilibria

▼
  • Weak acids/bases partially ionize and require equilibrium treatment.
  • Ka and Kb values indicate relative strength.

8.4 Acid-Base Reactions and Buffers

▼
  • Buffers contain a weak acid/base and its conjugate partner.
  • They resist pH change when small strong acid/base amounts are added.

8.5 Molecular Structure of Acids and Bases

▼
  • Bond polarity and conjugate-base stability influence acid strength.
  • Electronegativity and resonance can stabilize conjugate species.

8.6 pH and pKa

▼
\(\mathrm{pH=p}K_a+\log\left(\frac{[A^-]}{[HA]}\right)\)
Common Mistake

Using Henderson-Hasselbalch before completing stoichiometric neutralization when strong acid/base is added to a buffer.

8.7 Properties of Buffers

▼
  • Maximum buffer effectiveness occurs near pH approx pKa.
  • Higher total buffer concentration gives greater buffer capacity.

8.8 pH and Solubility

▼
  • For salts with basic anions, lower pH can increase solubility.
  • Acid-base chemistry can shift dissolution equilibria.

Unit 8 Extended Notes

▼
More Detail By Topic
  • 8.1-8.3: Strong species use direct concentration logic; weak species require equilibrium setup.
  • 8.4-8.7: Buffer math is two-step: stoichiometric neutralization first, then equilibrium expression.
  • 8.8: pH can shift dissolution direction if ions are part of acid-base equilibria.

Unit 8 Example Questions and Solutions

▼
Q1: What is pH of \(1.0\times10^{-3}\) M HCl?
A: \(\mathrm{pH}=-\log(10^{-3})=3.00\).
Q2: If \([A^-]=[HA]\), what does Henderson-Hasselbalch give?
A: \(\mathrm{pH}=pK_a\), because \(\log(1)=0\).
Q3: Why do buffers resist pH changes?
A: Added \(H^+\) or \(OH^-\) is consumed by the conjugate pair, limiting net pH shift.
