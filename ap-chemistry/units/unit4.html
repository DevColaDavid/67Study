Unit 4: Chemical Reactions

Big Picture

Unit 4 is where chemistry becomes equation-driven. Focus on balancing first, then mole ratios, then reaction class.

4.1 Introduction for Reactions

▼
  • Balanced equations conserve atoms and charge.
  • Stoichiometric coefficients describe mole ratios, not mass ratios.

4.2 Net Ionic Equations

▼
  • Write strong electrolytes as ions, then cancel spectators.
  • Net ionic equation includes only species that change.
How To Solve
  • Write balanced molecular equation.
  • Split strong electrolytes into ions.
  • Cancel ions unchanged on both sides.

4.3 Representations of Reactions

▼
  • Use symbolic, particulate, and macroscopic representations consistently.
  • Coefficients must match particle and mole-level interpretation.

4.4 Physical and Chemical Changes

▼
  • Physical change alters form/state; chemical change changes composition.
  • Indicators of chemical change include gas formation, precipitate, or energy change.

4.5 Stoichiometry

▼
\(\text{mass A}\rightarrow\text{moles A}\rightarrow\text{moles B}\rightarrow\text{mass B}\)
  • Limiting reactant determines theoretical yield.
Use coefficients as bridge: if equation says \(2\text{ A}\to3\text{ B}\), then moles B = moles A \(\times \frac{3}{2}\).

4.6 Introduction to Titration

▼
  • Titration uses a known concentration to determine an unknown.
  • At equivalence, stoichiometric moles satisfy balanced reaction ratios.

4.7 Types of Chemical Reactions

▼
  • Common classes: precipitation, acid-base, gas-evolution, and redox.

4.8 Introduction to Acid-Base Reactions

▼
  • Strong acid + strong base net ionic form: \(\mathrm{H^+ + OH^- \rightarrow H_2O}\).
  • Bronsted-Lowry acids donate H+, bases accept H+.

4.9 Oxidation-Reduction Reactions

▼
  • Oxidation is electron loss; reduction is electron gain.
  • Oxidation numbers help track electron transfer.
Common Mistake

Mixing up oxidizing agent vs reducing agent. Oxidizing agent is the species that gets reduced.
