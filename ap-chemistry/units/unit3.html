Unit 3: Properties of Substances and Mixtures

Big Picture

Relate bonding and IMFs to physical properties, then use the gas and solution models to compute and interpret. AP will mix macroscopic data with particulate pictures—always name the force or particle you mean before calculating.

3.1 Intermolecular and Interparticle Forces

▼

Intramolecular covalent bonds (Unit 2) are strong. Intermolecular forces (IMFs) act between molecules in liquids and solids; interparticle includes ion–ion in ionic lattices and solute–ion in solutions.

London dispersion (LDF): present in every substance; only force in nonpolar molecules. Strength grows with molar mass and with surface area (longer chain alkanes, branched isomers have lower bp than straight chains of same formula).

Dipole–dipole: permanent dipoles (e.g. HCl) attract each other—stronger on average than LDF alone for similar size. Hydrogen bond: special strong dipole interaction when H is bonded to N, O, or F and interacts with another N/O/F; explains high bp of water and \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), DNA base pairing, and protein folding questions.

Ion–dipole / ion–ion dominate in salts dissolving—why ionic substances often dissolve in polar solvents.

Rank bp (low→high): \(\mathrm{CH_4} \ll \mathrm{HCl} \ll \mathrm{H_2O}\) at 1 bar—LDF only vs dipole— vs H-bonding network in water.
Common Mistake

Calling every attraction between H and another atom a “hydrogen bond.” It requires a very polar H (adjacent to N/O/F) and a lone pair on N/O/F. Also: confusing bond breaking in vaporization (IMFs) with bonding covalent bond breaking—boiling does not break C–C or O–H covalent bonds in ethanol.

3.2 Properties of Solids

▼

Four main classes: ionic (lattice, brittle, high MP, conduction when mobile ions); metallic (delocalized e\(^-\), malleable, conducting); molecular (molecular solid) (IMFs in lattice, e.g. ice, dry ice) often lower MP than network; network covalent (extended covalent bonds—high MP, hard: diamond, \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) quartz).

Conductivity as evidence: metals conduct as solids; covalent molecular and network typically do not (except graphite—delocalized \(\pi\)); ionics conduct when molten or dissolved.

How To Classify a Solid from a Description
  • Identify the smallest repeating unit: ions, metal atoms+sea, distinct molecules, or infinite covalent.
  • Link bonding type to MP (network covalent and ionic often very high) and mechanical behavior (brittle ionic, malleable metal).
\(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) (quartz) is network covalent: melting requires breaking many strong Si–O bonds—MP far above ice (molecular solid with H-bonds, weaker than many covalent network bonds in energy per “object broken”).

3.3 Solids, Liquids, and Gases

▼

Particle density and order: solids—tight fixed positions (vibrational motion only, small). Liquids—close but disordered, flow. Gases—far apart, random motion, fill container.

Phase change vocabulary: solid→liquid fusion (endothermic: absorb heat); liquid→gas vaporization; gas→liquid condensation (exothermic). Sublimation: solid→gas directly. At constant pressure, temperature holds constant during the phase change while latent heat goes into overcoming IMFs, not changing \(T\).

Based on the diagram: as ordered packing (left) gives way to disorder (center) to large spacing (right), the same material stores more energy as thermal motion—hence the heating curve’s flat steps at constant \(T\).

3.4 Ideal Gas Law

▼

Historical Boyle’s (P–V at fixed n,T), Charles’s (V–T), and Avogadro’s (V–n) laws combine in \(PV = nRT\). The ideal gas model neglects particle volume and attractions—good at low P and high T.

Variables: \(P\) = pressure (often atm or kPa; use consistent \(R\)), \(V\) = volume (L for common \(R\)), \(n\) = moles, \(T\) = absolute temperature (K). \(R\) = gas constant—match units:

  • \(R = 0.08206\ \mathrm{L\cdot atm / (mol\cdot K)}\) when \(P\) in atm, \(V\) in L.
  • \(R = 8.314\ \mathrm{J / (mol\cdot K)} = 8.314\ \mathrm{kPa\cdot L / (mol\cdot K)}\) (SI-friendly).
\(PV = nRT, \qquad \dfrac{P_1V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{P_2V_2}{T_2}\ (n\ \text{constant})\)

STP (standard T and P): commonly \(0\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}\) and 1 bar (often still taught as 1 atm for AP practice); 1 mol ideal gas at 0°C and 1 atm is ~22.4 L molar volume (verify which STP definition a problem uses).

How To Solve Gas Problems
  • Convert \(T\) to K: \(T_{\mathrm{K}} = T_C + 273.15\).
  • List unknown; rearrange once; keep \(R\) consistent with P and V.
  • Ideal law fails for some conditions—go to 3.6 for reasoning.
What is \(P\) in atm for 0.200 mol gas in 4.00 L at 300. K? \(P = nRT/V = (0.200)(0.08206)(300.)/4.00 = 1.23\ \mathrm{atm}\).

3.5 Kinetic Molecular Theory

▼

Postulates (ideal gas): large separation (negligible volume of particles), constant random straight-line motion, elastic collisions, no IMFs, average translational kinetic energy \(\propto T\).

Key result: average translational KE of an ideal-gas particle depends only on \(T\), not molar mass. Lighter particles move faster on average at the same \(T\); \(u_{rms} = \sqrt{3RT/M}\) in SI with \(M\) in kg per mole (or use the AP form with consistent constants).

Dalton’s law of partial pressures: for ideal mixtures, \(P_{\mathrm{total}} = \sum P_i\); for each gas, \(P_i = X_i P_{\mathrm{total}}\) where \(X_i\) is mole fraction. Explains “collected over water” (subtract \(P_{\mathrm{H_2O}}\)).

Common Mistake

Assuming all gas particles have the same speed—there’s a distribution (Maxwell–Boltzmann) widened with \(T\); we speak of averages and rms, not a single speed.

3.6 Deviation from Ideal Gas Law

▼

Real gas \(PV/nRT\) is not always 1. At high P, the finite volume of molecules makes \(V\) effectively less than the ideal container— \(PV \gt nRT\) (volume correction) or, depending on model, the measured pressure is higher. At low T, attractions pull particles together—measured pressure can be less than ideal: \(P_{\mathrm{real}} \lt P_{\mathrm{ideal}}\) for a given n,V.

van der Waals (qualitative): parameters \(a\) (attraction) and \(b\) (excluded volume) fix departures—AP often asks to qualitatively place \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) or \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) near condensation vs ideal \(\mathrm{He}\).

3.7 Solutions and Mixtures

▼

Solution = homogeneous; solvent = major component, solute = minor (can swap if comparable amounts—then use mole fraction language).

\(M = \dfrac{n_\mathrm{solute}}{V_\mathrm{solution\ (L)}}\)    Dilution: \(M_1V_1 = M_2V_2\)

\(M\): moles solute per liter of solution (not solvent)—include volume of both when dissolving. Molality \(m\): moles solute per kg solvent (independent of \(T\)—useful in colligative and thermochemistry).

0.25 mol NaOH in 0.50 L of solution: \(M = 0.25/0.50 = 0.50\ \mathrm{M}\). Dilute 50.0 mL to 200.0 mL: new \(M = M_0 \times 50.0/200.0\).

3.8 Representations of Solutions

▼

Particulate pictures must match electrolyte class: strong electrolyte (strong acid, strong base, soluble salt)—show full or nearly full dissociation into ions. Weak electrolyte—mostly unionized molecules, few ions. Nonelectrolyte (e.g. glucose)—only neutral molecules, no free ions in solution.

Relative numbers of particles matter for colligative properties and for \(\Delta T_b\), \(\Delta T_f\) (Unit 4/later); match stoichiometry of dissociation (\(\mathrm{NaCl} \to 2\) ions, \(\mathrm{CaCl_2} \to 3\) particles per formula if fully ionized).

3.9 Separation of Solutions and Mixtures

▼

Choose a property difference: filtration (particle size—insoluble residue); distillation/fractional distillation (bp—volatile vs nonvolatile); chromatography (differential interaction with mobile vs stationary phase—polarity, size); extraction (partition between immiscible solvents—like dissolves like).

Raoult’s law (vapor pressure of solution) preview: nonvolatile solute lowers vapor pressure—used in some separation reasoning (Unit 4/5 tie-ins).

3.10 Solubility

▼

Like dissolves like: polar/ionic solutes often dissolve in polar solvents; nonpolar in nonpolar. For solids in liquids, solubility is often (not always) endothermic in dissolution—Le Châtelier explains why many salts are more soluble at higher T. For gases in liquid, solubility usually decreases with \(T\) (kinetic release of solute) and increases with partial pressure of the gas (Henry’s law region).

Henry’s law: \(C = kP\) (or \(C = k_H P\))—concentration of dissolved gas proportional to partial pressure above the liquid (e.g. \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) in soda, \(\mathrm{O_2}\) in blood concepts).

3.11 Spectroscopy and the Electromagnetic Spectrum

▼

EM radiation ordered by increasing energy (or frequency) and shorter \(\lambda\): radio → microwave → IR → visible → UV → x-ray, \(\gamma\). UV–vis and electronic transitions; IR for molecular vibrations; microwave for rotations (simplified in AP for conceptual links).

Each photon has \(E = h\nu = hc/\lambda\); the spectrum in absorption/emission identifies species and, for molecules, can fingerprint bonds (3.12).

3.12 Properties of Photons

▼

Max Planck and Einstein’s photon model: light carries energy in quanta. \(c = 2.998 \times 10^8\ \mathrm{m/s}\) in vacuum; \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \mathrm{J\cdot s}\).

\(c = \lambda\nu, \qquad E = h\nu = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\)

\(E\): photon energy (J or J per photon—convert to eV by dividing by element charge in some problems). Longer \(\lambda\) = lower frequency = lower photon energy (inverse relationship).

If \(\lambda = 500\ \mathrm{nm}\) (visible green), then \(\nu = c/\lambda\). Energy per mole of photons: \(E = N_A h c/\lambda\) (handy for “per mole of photons” in AP light-driven chemistry questions).
Common Mistake

Doubling \(\lambda\)? Energy is halved (inversely proportional), not doubled—remember \(E \propto 1/\lambda\) at fixed \(c\).

Unit 3 Synthesis

▼

Arguments flow: (1) structure of particles → (2) forces between them → (3) macroscopic property (MP, bp, solubility, P, concentration). The gas and solution mathematical models (ideal gas, molarity, partial pressures) are the tools—state assumptions before you plug in numbers.

Unit 3: Practice (AP-Style)

▼
How these are written

Similar to AP tasks that connect particle views to data and equations: ideal gas, solutions, and photon energy. Original items.

1. (IMFs) At 1 bar, \(\mathrm{F_2}\) is a gas and \(\mathrm{I_2}\) is a solid. (a) Name the only intermolecular forces between \(\mathrm{F_2}\) molecules. (b) Account for the difference in melting behavior using polarizability and molar mass.
(a) London dispersion forces (LDF) only—both are nonpolar. (b) \(\mathrm{I_2}\) is heavier and more polarizable, so LDFs are stronger; more energy is required to reach the solid→liquid→gas transitions at room T, so \(\mathrm{F_2}\) remains a gas at 25°C while \(\mathrm{I_2}\) is a solid.
2. (Ideal gas) A rigid \(2.00\ \mathrm{L}\) vessel contains pure \(\mathrm{O_2(g)}\) at \(0.500\ \mathrm{atm}\) and \(300.\ \mathrm{K}\). Calculate the amount, in moles of gas, in the vessel. Use \(R = 0.08206\ \mathrm{L\cdot atm/(mol\cdot K)}\).
Set up the ideal-gas model: \(n = \dfrac{PV}{RT} = \dfrac{(0.500\ \mathrm{atm})(2.00\ \mathrm{L})}{(0.08206\ \mathrm{L\cdot atm/(mol\cdot K)})(300.\ \mathrm{K})} = 0.0406\ \mathrm{mol}\ \mathrm{O_2}\) (3 s.f. from given data).
3. (Solutions) A student pipettes \(25.0\ \mathrm{mL}\) of \(2.00\ \mathrm{M\ HCl}\) into a \(100.0\ \mathrm{mL}\) volumetric flask, adds water to the mark, and mixes. (a) Find the molarity of the diluted acid. (b) State the assumption the dilution equation encodes about volume.
(a) \(M_1V_1 = M_2V_2\): \(M_2 = (2.00\ \mathrm{M})(25.0/100.0) = 0.500\ \mathrm{M}\). (b) The final volume of the solution is exactly \(100.0\ \mathrm{mL}\) (solutions are additive in the lab approx.; we use total solution volume, not just solvent added).
4. (Photons) The orange line in a discharge spectrum is at a longer wavelength than a blue line. (a) For two photons, one orange and one blue, which has the greater energy? (b) Cite a relationship from the AP equation sheet that supports your answer.
(a) The blue photon: shorter \(\lambda\), so higher frequency. (b) Use \(E = h\nu = hc/\lambda\): energy and wavelength are inversely related for a given photon; blue has smaller \(\lambda\), so larger \(E\).
