Unit 3: Properties of Substances and Mixtures

Big Picture

This unit connects particle-level forces to real-world properties like boiling point, gas behavior, and solution concentration.

3.1 Intermolecular and Interparticle Forces

▼
  • IMFs include London dispersion, dipole-dipole, and hydrogen bonding.
  • Stronger attractions usually raise boiling/melting points.
Why It Matters

This is why water boils much higher than similar-size molecules like methane.

3.2 Properties of Solids

▼
  • Solids can be ionic, metallic, molecular, or network covalent.
  • Type of bonding explains hardness, conductivity, and melting point.

3.3 Solids, Liquids, and Gases

▼
  • Particles are closest in solids and most separated in gases.
  • Phase changes reflect competition between particle energy and attractions.

3.4 Ideal Gas Law

▼
\(PV=nRT\)
  • Use Kelvin for temperature and consistent units for R.
How To Solve
  • Convert temperature to K first.
  • Isolate target variable algebraically.
  • Check unit consistency with your value of \(R\).

3.5 Kinetic Molecular Theory

▼
  • Average kinetic energy is proportional to absolute temperature.
  • Gas particles are in constant random motion with elastic collisions.

3.6 Deviation from Ideal Gas Law

▼
  • Real gases deviate most at high pressure and low temperature.
  • Intermolecular attractions and finite particle volume cause deviation.

3.7 Solutions and Mixtures

▼
  • Solutions are homogeneous mixtures of solute and solvent.
  • Concentration can be expressed with molarity.
\(M=\frac{\text{moles solute}}{\text{liters solution}}\)
Example: \(0.250\) mol NaOH in \(0.500\) L gives \(0.500\) M.

3.8 Representations of Solutions

▼
  • Particulate diagrams show dissolved ions/molecules and relative particle counts.
  • Strong electrolytes dissociate extensively; nonelectrolytes do not ionize.

3.9 Separation of Solutions and Mixtures

▼
  • Techniques include filtration, distillation, chromatography, and extraction.
  • Method choice depends on property differences (size, boiling point, polarity).

3.10 Solubility

▼
  • Solubility depends on intermolecular interactions and temperature.
  • For gases in liquids, increasing pressure generally increases solubility.

3.11 Spectroscopy and Electromagnetic Spectrum

▼
  • Different regions of EM radiation probe different energy transitions.
  • Spectra can identify composition and structure.

3.12 Properties of Photons

▼
\(c=\lambda\nu,\ E=h\nu\)
  • Higher frequency means higher photon energy and shorter wavelength.
Common Mistake

Mixing up wavelength and frequency direction. They are inverse: if one goes up, the other goes down.

Unit 3 Extended Notes

▼
More Detail By Topic
  • 3.1-3.3: IMF strength controls phase behavior, melting/boiling points, and volatility trends.
  • 3.4-3.6: Ideal behavior is best at high \(T\), low \(P\); real gases deviate with stronger IMFs.
  • 3.7-3.10: Solubility depends on particle interactions and can be predicted by polarity and ion effects.
  • 3.11-3.12: Spectroscopy links wave properties to quantized energy transitions in matter.

Unit 3 Example Questions and Solutions

▼
Q1: Why does water have stronger IMFs than methane?
A: Water can hydrogen bond; methane only has London dispersion forces.
Q2: Find moles in 2.50 L of gas at STP (ideal approximation).
A: \(n=\frac{PV}{RT}=\frac{(1.00)(2.50)}{0.08206(273)}\approx0.112\text{ mol}\).
Q3: If wavelength halves, what happens to photon energy?
A: Energy doubles because \(E=\frac{hc}{\lambda}\).
