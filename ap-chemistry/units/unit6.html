Unit 6: Thermochemistry

Big Picture

This unit tracks energy flow in reactions. Keep sign conventions clear: system absorbs energy (+), system releases energy (-).

6.1 Endothermic and Exothermic Processes

▼
  • Endothermic processes absorb heat (positive DeltaH).
  • Exothermic processes release heat (negative DeltaH).
Common Mistake

Thinking a temperature increase always means endothermic. If surroundings get warmer, the reaction itself is exothermic.

6.2 Energy Diagrams

▼
  • Vertical axis is potential energy; horizontal axis is reaction progress.
  • Difference between product and reactant levels is DeltaH.

6.3 Heat Transfer and Thermal Equilibrium

▼
  • Heat flows from higher to lower temperature until thermal equilibrium.
  • For an isolated pair: q_gained + q_lost = 0.

6.4 Heat Capacity and Calorimetry

▼
\(q=mC_s\Delta T\)
  • At constant pressure, measured heat is related to DeltaH.
How To Solve
  • Use \(\Delta T=T_f-T_i\).
  • Assign sign by system perspective.
  • Convert J to kJ when needed for enthalpy reporting.

6.5 Energy of Phase Changes

▼
  • Fusion and vaporization are endothermic.
  • Freezing and condensation are exothermic.

6.6 Introduction to Enthalpy of Reaction

▼
  • At constant pressure, q_p = DeltaH.
  • Enthalpy is a state function.

6.7 Bond Enthalpies

▼
\(\Delta H_{\mathrm{rxn}}\approx \sum\text{(bonds broken)}-\sum\text{(bonds formed)}\)

6.8 Enthalpy of Formation

▼
  • Standard enthalpy of formation is for forming 1 mol from elements in standard states.
  • Elements in standard state have standard DeltaHf = 0.

6.9 Hess's Law

▼
  • If reactions add algebraically, their enthalpy changes also add.
  • Reversing a reaction changes sign of DeltaH.
Why It Matters

You can build unknown reaction enthalpy values from known equations, like algebra with energy bookkeeping.

Unit 6 Extended Notes

▼
More Detail By Topic
  • 6.1-6.3: Always define system/surroundings before assigning heat-flow sign.
  • 6.4-6.5: Temperature plateaus in phase change show energy used for IMF changes, not kinetic-energy change.
  • 6.6-6.9: Enthalpy calculations can come from calorimetry, bond energies, standard formation data, or Hess cycles.

Unit 6 Example Questions and Solutions

▼
Q1: A 50.0 g sample of water warms by \(4.0^\circ C\). Find \(q\).
A: \(q=mC_s\Delta T=(50.0)(4.18)(4.0)=836\text{ J}\).
Q2: Why is \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\) usually greater than \(\Delta H_{\text{fus}}\)?
A: Vaporization separates molecules much farther than melting, so more IMF disruption energy is needed.
Q3: If you reverse an equation with \(\Delta H=-120\text{ kJ}\), what is new \(\Delta H\)?
A: \(+120\text{ kJ}\).
