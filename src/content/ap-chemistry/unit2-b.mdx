<Chapter id="t25" title="2.5 Lewis Diagrams">

**Gilbert N. Lewis** (1916) used dot pairs to show shared and lone valence pairs. AP expects **octet** for C, N, O, F (H obeys duet; third-row and below can expand octet when needed—see $\mathrm{PCl_5}$, $\mathrm{SF_6}$ with VSEPR in 2.7).

<StepsBlock title="Lewis Workflow (systematic)">

- Count valence $e^-$: sum atom valences, add one $e^-$ per $-1$ charge, subtract for $+$ on polyatomic cations (e.g. $(\mathrm{NH_4})^+$).
- Central atom: least electronegative (C over O in oxyanions), H and halogens terminal.
- Connect with single bonds; complete octets on outer atoms first; place leftover on central; form multiple bonds if central lacks octet and reasonable.
- Check total: sum of $e^-$ in structure = initial count. Assign formal charge if comparing alternatives (2.6).

</StepsBlock>

**Resonance** and **formal charge** (next section) decide between contributing structures. Odd-electron, radicals, and coordinate bonds appear on some exams—pair electrons in order of the algorithm above, then use FC to choose best drawing.

<Callout>
  $\mathrm{CO_2}$: C central, 16 valence $e^-$ total, double bonds on both sides, no FC on C if drawn symmetrically.
  $\mathrm{O_2}$ needs a double bond; $\mathrm{N_2}$ triple—match bond order to experiment where asked.
</Callout>

</Chapter>

<Chapter id="t26" title="2.6 Resonance and Formal Charge">

When a single classical Lewis form undervalues $e^-$ delocalization (e.g. $\mathrm{O_3}$, $(\mathrm{NO_3})^-$, $\mathrm{O_2}$), **resonance** = multiple valence-bond images that together describe one real hybrid—electrons are **not** zipping back and forth; the true wavefunction has lowered energy vs any one drawing.

<FormulaBlock math="FC = V - N - B/2" />

<p>
  Same formula as: $\text{formal charge}=\text{valence e}^- - (\text{nonbonding e}^- + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{bonding e}^-)$.
  Prefer <strong>lower |FC|</strong> overall; place negative FC on the <strong>more EN</strong> atom and positive on less EN when you must break ties. For $\mathrm{CO}$, the structure with a formal negative on <strong>O</strong> and positive on C is a major contributor because O is more electronegative than C, despite both having octets in some alternates.
</p>

<StepsBlock title="How To Choose Best Resonance Contributor">

- Octet rule satisfied (where applicable).
- Minimize FC magnitudes; avoid like charges on adjacent atoms in same drawing if avoidable.
- Negative on EN atoms; for $(\mathrm{NO_2})^-$ and $(\mathrm{NO_3})^-$ expect equal N–O bond lengths in the true ion (average of resonance).

</StepsBlock>

<Callout>
  In $(\mathrm{NO_3})^-$, three oxygens share a bond order of $4/3$ to N—all N–O lengths equal. Experimental bond
  length is between single and double.
</Callout>

<MistakeBlock>
  <p>
    Drawing a double bond to two different O’s and assuming one is “the real one”—the ion is a single averaged
    structure; for AP, discuss delocalized $\pi$ in qualitative terms. Also: confusing FC with _oxidation
    number_—different bookkeeping rules (oxidation: assume ionic limit).
  </p>
</MistakeBlock>

</Chapter>

<Chapter id="t27" title="2.7 VSEPR and Bond Hybridization">

VSEPR means valence-shell electron-pair repulsion: electron domains spread out to minimize repulsion. This section should feel procedural so students can solve any geometry question from scratch.

<StepsBlock title="How To Solve VSEPR Questions">

- Draw Lewis structure first.
- Count electron domains around central atom (each bond = one domain, each lone pair = one domain).
- Assign electron geometry from domain count.
- Assign molecular geometry using only atom positions (lone pairs hidden in shape name).
- Assign common hybridization by domain count: 2 → $sp$, 3 → $sp^2$, 4 → $sp^3$.

</StepsBlock>

| Domains | Electron Geometry | Molecular Geometry Examples | Hybridization (AP) |
| ------- | ------------------- | ----------------------------- | ------------------ |
| 2 | Linear | Linear (e.g. $\mathrm{CO_2}$) | $sp$ |
| 3 | Trigonal planar | Trigonal planar, Bent (e.g. $\mathrm{SO_2}$) | $sp^2$ |
| 4 | Tetrahedral | Tetrahedral, Trigonal pyramidal, Bent (e.g. $\mathrm{NH_3}$, $\mathrm{H_2O}$) | $sp^3$ |
| 5 | Trigonal bipyramidal | Seesaw, T-shaped, linear (e.g. $\mathrm{PCl_5}$, $\mathrm{SF_4}$, $\mathrm{ClF_3}$, $\mathrm{XeF_2}$) | $sp^3d$ |
| 6 | Octahedral | Octahedral, Square pyramidal, Square planar (e.g. $\mathrm{SF_6}$, $\mathrm{BrF_5}$, $\mathrm{XeF_4}$) | $sp^3d^2$ |

Lone pairs occupy more space than bond pairs—they **reduce bond angles** next to them (e.g. $\mathrm{H_2O}$ ~104.5° vs ideal tetrahedral 109.5°).

<QaItem question="1. (Polarity) For CO₂(g) and SO₂(g): (a) State the VSEPR molecular geometry (including number of lone pairs on the central atom). (b) Predict whether each molecule is polar or nonpolar and justify using bond dipoles and shape.">
  (a) CO₂: two bonding domains, zero lone pairs on C—linear. SO₂: three electron domains, one lone pair on
  S—bent. (b) CO₂ is <strong>nonpolar</strong> (identical C=O bond dipoles, $180^\circ$, net dipole 0). SO₂ is{" "}
  <strong>polar</strong> (bent geometry; S–O dipoles do not cancel).
</QaItem>

<QaItem question="2. (VSEPR) For one NH₃ molecule: (a) Give the electron-domain geometry, molecular geometry, and bond angle around N (as expected from VSEPR, vs ideal tetrahedral). (b) State the hybridization of the central atom.">
  (a) Four electron domains → <strong>tetrahedral</strong> electron geometry; <strong>trigonal pyramidal</strong>{" "}
  molecular geometry; bond angle a bit <em>less</em> than $109.5^\circ$ because the lone pair repels more. (b) $sp^3$
  on N (four hybrid orbitals for four domains).
</QaItem>

<QaItem question="3. (Resonance) The experimental N–O bond length in the nitrate ion is the same for all three oxygen atoms. (a) Explain in terms of resonance and bond order. (b) State whether the true ion is best represented as a single Lewis structure.">
  (a) The ion is a <strong>resonance hybrid</strong> of three equivalent forms; the $\pi$ electron density is
  delocalized—each N–O has an average order between single and double, so all bonds have the <em>same</em> length.
  (b) No; one drawing alone is a simplification—the real species has delocalized bonding.
</QaItem>

<QaItem question="4. (Formal charge) The CO Lewis structure with a triple bond and a lone pair on each atom gives FC(C)=+1 and FC(O)=−1. (a) Identify the most important contributor and explain (octet + charge placement). (b) A student places −1 on C and +1 on O—why is that a weaker contributor?">
  (a) The triple-bond, octet-satisfied structure is major; the negative formal charge should sit on the{" "}
  <strong>more electronegative</strong> atom (O), not C. (b) Placing a negative charge on the less EN carbon is
  destabilizing; formal charge should preferentially reside on the more EN atom in comparable octet structures.
</QaItem>
