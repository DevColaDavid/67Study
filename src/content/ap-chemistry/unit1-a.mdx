<Chapter id="t11" title="1.1 Moles and Molar Mass">

The **mole** is the chemist's count of Avogadro's number of identical particles. **Amedeo Avogadro** (early 1800s)
proposed equal volumes of gases at the same $T$ and $P$ hold equal numbers of particles; that idea became the
foundation for relating macroscopic mass to atom counts. The value $N_A \approx 6.022 \times 10^{23}\ \mathrm{mol^{-1}}$
is named the Avogadro constant.

**Molar mass** $M$ (unit $\mathrm{g/mol}$) is the mass of one mole of a substance—numerically the same as average
atomic or molecular mass in amu, but with $\mathrm{g/mol}$ for stoichiometry. In $n = \frac{m}{M}$, $n$ = amount
(moles), $m$ = sample mass (g), $M$ = molar mass (g/mol). For a compound, add atomic contributions from the formula.

<FormulaBlock math={String.raw`n = \dfrac{m}{M}, \text{particles} = n \times N_A`} />

<StepsBlock title="How To Solve Mole Conversions">

<ul className="notes-list">
  <li>Write given quantity with units; circle the unknown.</li>
  <li>Multiply by a conversion so unwanted units cancel (mole bridge or $N_A$).</li>
  <li>Report with correct significant figures; never round before the final step on multi-step problems.</li>
</ul>

</StepsBlock>

<Callout variant="example">
  Example: Molar mass of $\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2} = 40.08 + 2(16.00) + 2(1.008) = 74.10\ \mathrm{g/mol}$. Mass of
  $0.150\ \mathrm{mol}$ is $m = nM = 0.150 \times 74.10 = 11.1\ \mathrm{g}$.
</Callout>

<MistakeBlock>
  <p>
    Inverting the factor $\frac{1\ \mathrm{mol}}{M\ \mathrm{g}}$. Place the unit you <em>want</em> in the
    numerator so the given unit cancels. Also: distinguish <em>formula units</em> in ionic solids from{" "}
    <em>molecules</em>—use the same mole math, but language must match the substance.
  </p>
</MistakeBlock>

</Chapter>

<Chapter id="t12" title="1.2 Mass Spectra of Elements">

Mass spectrometry ionizes atoms or molecules, sorts them by **mass-to-charge ratio** ($m/z$; often $z=1$), and
plots relative abundance. That is how chemists _weigh isotopes_ in mixture. **Isotopes** of an element have the same
number of protons, different numbers of neutrons—hence different masses.

Periodic table atomic mass is a **weighted average** of naturally occurring isotopes, not the mass of the most
common isotope. If isotope $i$ has mass $m_i$ and fractional abundance (decimal) $f_i$, then

<FormulaBlock math={String.raw`\text{Average atomic mass} = \sum_i f_i m_i`} />

Here $f_i$ are the fractional abundances (sum to 1.0); on a spectrum, relative peak _heights or areas_ (after
calibration) stand in for the $f_i$. Peak **position** (x-axis) gives $m/z$, not abundance.

<Callout variant="example">
  Example: Two isotopes with masses 10.0 and 11.0 and abundances 0.20 and 0.80: average
  $= 0.20(10.0) + 0.80(11.0) = 10.8\ \mathrm{amu}$.
</Callout>

<MistakeBlock>
  <p>
    Using percent abundances (e.g. 20%) as if they were decimals. Divide by 100 first. Or confusing peak y-value
    with isotope mass—mass is read on the <em>horizontal</em> axis in $m/z$.
  </p>
</MistakeBlock>

</Chapter>

<Chapter id="t13" title="1.3 Elemental Composition of Pure Substances">

**Law of definite proportions**: a given compound always contains elements in a fixed mass ratio. **Empirical formula**
gives the smallest whole-number atom ratio. **Molecular formula** = integer multiple of the empirical formula, where
that integer is the ratio of molar mass of the real molecule to empirical formula mass.

Given mass % of each element: convert to moles, divide by the smallest mole amount to get a ratio, then scale to
whole numbers if needed (multiply by 2, 3, … to clear 0.33, 0.5, etc.).

<StepsBlock title="How To Find Empirical Formula from Mass %">

<ul className="notes-list">
  <li>Assume a 100 g sample so mass % = grams of each element.</li>
  <li>$n = m/M$ for each element using atomic masses.</li>
  <li>Divide all $n$ by the smallest; round carefully (ratios 1.99 $\approx$ 2 if within experimental error).</li>
</ul>

</StepsBlock>

| Step | Operation        | Meaning of symbols                          |
| ---- | ---------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| 1    | g $\rightarrow$ mol | $m$ in g, $M$ in g/mol                   |
| 2    | normalize        | divide by smallest mol amount               |
| 3    | molecular (optional) | factor $= \frac{M_{\mathrm{molar}}}{M_{\mathrm{empirical}}}$ |

<Callout variant="example">
  Example: A compound is 40.0% C, 6.7% H, 53.3% O by mass. Moles: C $40.0/12.01=3.33$, H $6.7/1.008=6.6$, O
  $53.3/16.00=3.33$. Ratio C:H:O $=1:2:1$ → empirical $\mathrm{CH_2O}$.
</Callout>

<MistakeBlock>
  <p>
    Reporting $\mathrm{CH_2O}$ as the molecular formula when the true molar mass is 180 g/mol—the molecular formula
    is $\mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6}$ (factor 6). Always check with given molar mass if provided.
  </p>
</MistakeBlock>

</Chapter>

<Chapter id="t14" title="1.4 Composition of Mixtures">

A **mixture** contains two or more substances physically combined; its composition can vary. **Homogeneous**
(solution): uniform on the scale of observation. **Heterogeneous**: distinct regions (phases) visible. Pure
substances: fixed composition (element or compound).

**Mass percent** of component A: $\text{mass \%} = \frac{m_{\mathrm{A}}}{m_{\mathrm{total}}} \times 100\%$. Mole
fraction and molarity appear in later units; here focus on mass-based descriptions and dilute vs concentrated
qualitatively.

<FormulaBlock math={String.raw`\text{mass \%} = \dfrac{m_{\mathrm{component}}}{m_{\mathrm{total}}} \times 100\%`} />

<StepsBlock title="How To Track Mixture Problems">

<ul className="notes-list">
  <li>Identify what is the total sample (denominator) vs one part (numerator).</li>
  <li>
    For a solution, distinguish solute, solvent, and solution total mass or volume (careful with unit consistency).
  </li>
</ul>

</StepsBlock>

<Callout variant="example">
  An 80.0 g aqueous sample contains 12.0 g NaCl. Mass % NaCl $= 12.0/80.0 \times 100\% = 15.0\%$. The rest is mostly
  water and any other solutes.
</Callout>

<MistakeBlock>
  <p>
    Using the solvent mass in the denominator instead of total solution mass. The percent is “parts per hundred of the{" "}
    <em>whole</em> mixture” unless a problem defines otherwise.
  </p>
</MistakeBlock>

</Chapter>
