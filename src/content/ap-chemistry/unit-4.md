# Unit 4: Chemical Reactions

## 4.1 Chemical and Physical Changes

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Identify evidence of chemical and physical changes in matter. (4.1.A)

- **Physical change**: change in physical properties (state, shape, appearance) but no change in chemical composition
- **Chemical change**: formation of new substances with different chemical composition
- Physical change examples: melting, freezing, dissolving, mixing, boiling
- Chemical change indicators: color change, temperature change, gas formation (bubbling/fizzing), precipitate formation, odor change

### Physical Changes

Substances retain their chemical identity while their form transforms. Phase transitions (solid в†’ liquid в†’ gas), dissolving substances, and alterations in size or shape are all physical changes. Water cycling between ice, liquid, and steam demonstrates this вЂ” Hв‚‚O composition remains constant throughout.

### Chemical Changes

Reactions produce entirely new substances with distinct chemical compositions. Observable indicators include:
- Color shifts (but not all color changes are chemical вЂ” dye mixing is physical)
- Gas bubbling or fizzing (distinct from simple boiling)
- Solid precipitate forming in a solution
- Heat or light emission
- Odor changes indicating new compounds

A weak acid reacting with sodium hydrogen carbonate generates COв‚‚ gas effervescence вЂ” a clear chemical change indicator.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Look for observable evidence, but remember that not every color change or temperature change automatically signals a chemical reaction. Ask: does the substance's chemical identity actually change? Dissolving NaCl in water is physical (ions separate but no new substance forms); burning wood is chemical (new gases and ash form).

## 4.2 Net Ionic Equations

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent changes in matter with balanced chemical or net ionic equations for physical changes and for chemical reactions. (4.2.A)

- Balanced equations must satisfy conservation of mass (same atom counts on both sides) and conservation of charge (same total charge on both sides)
- Three forms of ionic equations:
  - **Molecular equation**: all species written as neutral formulas
  - **Complete ionic equation**: all soluble ionic compounds split into individual ions
  - **Net ionic equation**: only species that actually change; spectator ions removed
- **Spectator ions**: ions present but not involved in the reaction; identical on both sides

### Writing Net Ionic Equations

**Step-by-step:**
1. Write the balanced molecular equation
2. Split all strong electrolytes (soluble ionic compounds, strong acids, strong bases) into ions
3. Cancel ions appearing identically on both sides (spectator ions)
4. What remains is the net ionic equation

**Example вЂ” Precipitation of BaSOв‚„:**

| Form | Equation |
|---|---|
| Molecular | Naв‚‚SOв‚„(aq) + BaClв‚‚(aq) в†’ 2NaCl(aq) + BaSOв‚„(s) |
| Complete ionic | 2NaвЃє(aq) + SOв‚„ВІвЃ»(aq) + BaВІвЃє(aq) + 2ClвЃ»(aq) в†’ 2NaвЃє(aq) + 2ClвЃ»(aq) + BaSOв‚„(s) |
| Net ionic | SOв‚„ВІвЃ»(aq) + BaВІвЃє(aq) в†’ BaSOв‚„(s) |

NaвЃє and ClвЃ» are spectator ions.

**Example вЂ” Acid-Base:**

Molecular: NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) в†’ NaCl(aq) + Hв‚‚O(l)

Net ionic: OHвЃ»(aq) + HвЃє(aq) в†’ Hв‚‚O(l)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Start by writing the full molecular equation, break all strong electrolytes into ions, identify and cancel spectator ions, then check that both atoms and charges balance. Solids, liquids, and weak electrolytes are NOT split into ions.

## 4.3 Particulate Representations of Reactions

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent a given chemical reaction or physical process with a consistent particulate model. (4.3.A)

- Particulate models use dots, circles, or shapes to depict atoms, ions, or molecules at the particle level
- Models must match chemical formulas and display correct ratios based on the balanced equation
- Different symbols/shapes represent different elements or ions
- State of matter shown by arrangement: gases = dispersed, solids = fixed arrays, aqueous ions = freely floating

### Requirements for Valid Particulate Diagrams

- Use distinct symbols for each type of atom or ion
- Particle counts must reflect the molar ratios from the balanced equation
- Conservation of mass: same atom count before and after
- Conservation of charge: same total charge before and after
- Spectator ions should appear unchanged on both sides

### Precipitation Example

Balanced equation: AgNOв‚ѓ(aq) + NaCl(aq) в†’ AgCl(s) + NaNOв‚ѓ(aq)

Net ionic: AgвЃє(aq) + ClвЃ»(aq) в†’ AgCl(s)

The particulate diagram shows AgвЃє and ClвЃ» combining in a 1:1 ratio to form a solid AgCl cluster, while NaвЃє and NOв‚ѓвЃ» remain dispersed (spectator ions).

### Physical Change Example

Hв‚‚O(s) в†’ Hв‚‚O(l): tightly packed solid particles rearrange into a freely mobile liquid вЂ” same molecules, different arrangement.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> When drawing or interpreting a particulate diagram: (1) verify correct atom identities and ratios, (2) show state correctly (solid = clustered, aqueous = dispersed), (3) don't forget spectator ions still appear in solution, (4) count atoms on both sides to confirm conservation.

## 4.5 Stoichiometry

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain changes in the amounts of reactants and products based on the balanced reaction equation. (4.5.A)

- Coefficients in a balanced equation represent exact molar ratios
- All stoichiometric calculations require converting to moles first
- The **limiting reagent** controls maximum product formation; the **excess reagent** remains after reaction completes

### Mole Conversion Methods

| Starting quantity | Formula |
|---|---|
| Mass | $n = m / M$ |
| Solution | $n = M \times V_\text{(L)}$ |
| Gas (ideal) | $PV = nRT$ |
| Particles | $N = n \times N_A$ |

### Three-Step Stoichiometry Framework

1. Convert starting quantity в†’ moles (using appropriate method above)
2. Apply mole ratio from balanced equation to convert between substances
3. Convert moles в†’ requested units (grams, volume, molarity, particle count)

### Limiting Reagent

The limiting reagent is the reactant that runs out first and stops the reaction. To find it:
- Convert all given reactant amounts to moles of **product**
- The reactant producing the **least product** is the limiting reagent

$$\% \text{ yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100\%$$

### Special Stoichiometry Cases

**Gas stoichiometry**: at identical temperature and pressure, volume ratios equal molar ratios directly.

**Solution stoichiometry**: uses molarity ($n = M \times V$) to connect concentration to mole quantity вЂ” essential for titration problems.

> [!example] Worked Example
> How many grams of Hв‚‚O form when 4.0 g Hв‚‚ reacts with excess Oв‚‚?
>
> Balanced: 2Hв‚‚ + Oв‚‚ в†’ 2Hв‚‚O
>
> 1. $n_{\text{H}_2} = 4.0 / 2.02 = 1.98$ mol
> 2. Mole ratio Hв‚‚:Hв‚‚O = 1:1 в†’ 1.98 mol Hв‚‚O
> 3. $m_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} = 1.98 \times 18.02 = 35.7$ g

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Always balance the equation first. Never operate with grams directly вЂ” convert to moles. Check limiting reagent when two reactant amounts are given. Percent yield в‰¤ 100% always.

## 4.6 Introduction to Titration

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Identify the equivalence point in a titration based on amounts of titrant and analyte. (4.6.A)

- **Titration**: method used to determine the concentration of a substance (the **analyte**) by adding a reagent of known concentration (the **titrant**) until the reaction is complete
- **Equivalence point**: moles of titrant have exactly reacted with all moles of analyte per stoichiometry
- **Endpoint**: observable signal (e.g., color change from indicator) marking approximate equivalence point
- Phenolphthalein is common for acid-base titrations: colorless in acid, pink in base

### Stoichiometric Relationship at Equivalence

For a 1:1 acid-base reaction HA + OHвЃ» в†’ AвЃ» + Hв‚‚O:

$$n_\text{acid} = n_\text{base} \quad \Rightarrow \quad M_a V_a = M_b V_b$$

For non-1:1 ratios, use mole ratio from balanced equation.

> [!example] Worked Example
> A 25.0 mL sample of HCl requires 25.0 mL of 0.100 mol/L NaOH to reach the equivalence point. Find [HCl].
>
> HCl + NaOH в†’ NaCl + Hв‚‚O (1:1 ratio)
>
> $n_\text{NaOH} = 0.100 \times 0.0250 = 0.00250$ mol
>
> $[\text{HCl}] = 0.00250 / 0.0250 = 0.100$ mol/L

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Always write the balanced equation first to find the mole ratio. Keep volumes in liters. The equivalence point is defined by stoichiometry, not by the indicator вЂ” the indicator approximates it.

## 4.7 Types of Chemical Reactions

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Identify a reaction as acidвЂ“base, oxidationвЂ“reduction, or precipitation. (4.7.A)

- **AcidвЂ“base**: HвЃє (proton) transfer between species

### AcidвЂ“Base Reactions

Proton (HвЃє) transfers from an acid (donor) to a base (acceptor):

Example: HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) в†’ NaCl(aq) + Hв‚‚O(l)

Insoluble solid ("precipitate") forms from mixing aqueous ionic solutions.

Example: AgNOв‚ѓ(aq) + NaCl(aq) в†’ AgCl(s) + NaNOв‚ѓ(aq)

## 4.8 Introduction to AcidвЂ“Base Reactions

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Identify species as BrГёnstedвЂ“Lowry acids, bases, and conjugate acidвЂ“base pairs based on proton transfer. (4.8.A)

## 4.9 OxidationвЂ“Reduction (Redox) Reactions

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent a balanced redox reaction equation using half-reactions. (4.9.A)

- Redox reactions involve electron transfer: one species is oxidized (loses eвЃ»), another is reduced (gains eвЃ»)
- **OIL RIG**: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain
- Half-reactions show each process separately; electrons lost must equal electrons gained before combining

### Half-Reaction Method for Balancing

**Acidic solution:**
1. Balance all atoms except H and O
2. Balance O by adding Hв‚‚O
3. Balance H by adding HвЃє

5. Multiply half-reactions so electrons cancel, then add

**Basic solution:** after balancing in acid, add OHвЃ» to both sides to neutralize HвЃє в†’ combine with HвЃє to form Hв‚‚O.

> Balance: MnOв‚„вЃ» в†’ MnВІвЃє (in acidic solution)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Half-reactions break the problem into manageable parts and make electron flow explicit. Always check that the final combined equation has no electrons remaining and that atoms and charge both balance.
