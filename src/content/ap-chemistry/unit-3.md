# Unit 3: Properties of Substances and Mixtures

## 3.1 Intermolecular and Interparticle Forces

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain relationships between chemical structures of molecules and the relative strength of their intermolecular forces. (3.1.A)

IMFs in increasing strength order:

| Force | Present In | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| London Dispersion (LDF) | All molecules | Weakest |
| Dipole-dipole | Polar molecules | Moderate |
| Hydrogen bonding | H bonded to N, O, or F | Strong |
| Ion-dipole | Ion + polar molecule | Strongest |

- **LDF**: temporary dipoles from electron movement; strength increases with electron count, molecular size, and polarizability
- **Dipole-dipole**: between polar molecules; depends on dipole magnitude and orientation
- **Dipole-induced dipole**: polar molecule induces temporary dipole in nonpolar neighbor
- **Hydrogen bonding**: special, highly directional; requires H bonded directly to N, O, or F

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> If H is bonded to N, O, or F and interacting with another N, O, or F — it's hydrogen bonding!

> [!example] Ranking IMFs
> Cl₂–Cl₂ (LDF, weakest) → HCl–HCl (dipole-dipole) → HF–HF (H-bonding) → Na⁺–H₂O (ion-dipole, strongest)

## 3.2 Properties of Solids

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain relationships between macroscopic properties of substances, particulate-level structure, and interparticle interactions. (3.2.A)

- Stronger interparticle forces → higher melting/boiling point, lower vapor pressure
- **Vapor pressure**: pressure exerted by vapor above a liquid in a closed container

### Four Types of Solids

| Type | Particles | Forces | Properties | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ionic** | Cations + anions | Electrostatic | High MP, brittle, conducts when molten | NaCl |
| **Covalent network** | Atoms | Covalent bonds | Very high MP, very hard, non-conductive* | Diamond, SiO₂ |
| **Molecular** | Molecules | IMFs | Low MP, soft, non-conductive | Ice, I₂ |
| **Metallic** | Metal ions + e⁻ sea | Metallic bonding | Conducts, malleable, ductile | Fe, Cu |

*Graphite is a covalent network solid that does conduct electricity (delocalized electrons between layers).

### Alloys
- **Substitutional**: similar-sized atoms replace host atoms (brass: Cu + Zn)
- **Interstitial**: small atoms fill lattice gaps (steel: Fe + C → harder, blocks layer sliding)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Connect structure to properties: ionic = high MP + brittle; covalent network = extremely hard; molecular = low MP; metallic = conducts + malleable.

## 3.3 Solids, Liquids, and Gases

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent the differences between solid, liquid, and gas phases using a particulate-level model. (3.3.A)

| Phase | Particle Arrangement | Shape | Volume | Compressible? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Solid | Fixed, close-packed | Definite | Definite | No |
| Liquid | Close but mobile | Takes container shape | Definite | No |
| Gas | Far apart, random | Takes container shape | Fills container | Yes |

- Solid particles vibrate but don't move past each other (**crystalline** = regular repeating order; **amorphous** = no long-range order)
- Liquid particles move past each other; cohesive and adhesive forces significant
- Gas particles have negligible intermolecular forces; dominated by kinetic energy
- Solid and liquid molar volumes are similar (both close-packed); gas molar volume is vastly larger

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Visualize: solids = fixed and packed; liquids = mobile but touching; gases = dispersed and fast. Connect to compressibility, shape retention, and volume.

## 3.4 Ideal Gas Law

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between macroscopic properties of a gas or gas mixture using the ideal gas law. (3.4.A)

$$PV = nRT$$

| Variable | Meaning | Units |
|---|---|---|
| $P$ | Pressure | atm |
| $V$ | Volume | L |
| $n$ | Moles of gas | mol |
| $R$ | Gas constant | 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K |
| $T$ | Temperature | K (always!) |

**Assumptions of an ideal gas:**
- Particles move in constant, random, straight-line motion
- Particle volume is negligible compared to container
- No intermolecular attractions or repulsions
- Collisions are perfectly elastic (kinetic energy conserved)
- Temperature is proportional to average kinetic energy

### Gas Laws (derived from ideal gas law)

| Law | Relationship | Held constant |
|---|---|---|
| Boyle's | $P \propto 1/V$ | $n$, $T$ |
| Charles's | $V \propto T$ | $n$, $P$ |
| Gay-Lussac's | $P \propto T$ | $n$, $V$ |
| Avogadro's | $V \propto n$ | $P$, $T$ |

### Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

$$P_\text{total} = P_A + P_B + P_C + \cdots$$

$$P_A = X_A \times P_\text{total} \qquad \text{where } X_A = \frac{n_A}{n_\text{total}}$$

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Always convert temperature to Kelvin. For gas mixtures, calculate mole fractions first, then partial pressures. Check that your $R$ units match the problem's units.

## 3.5 Kinetic Molecular Theory

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Clarify connections between particle motion and observable gas properties using kinetic molecular theory. (3.5.A)

- KMT explains *why* gas laws work at the particle level
- Average kinetic energy depends only on temperature (in K), not mass:

$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

- At the same temperature: lighter particles move **faster**; heavier particles move **slower** — but both have the **same average KE**
- Absolute zero (0 K) = all particle motion stops

### Maxwell–Boltzmann Distribution

A graph showing the distribution of particle speeds at a given temperature:
- Most particles cluster near the average speed
- The curve is skewed right (some particles move much faster than average)
- **Higher temperature** → curve flattens, shifts right, spreads wider → more particles with high energy

> [!example] Worked Example
> At 300 K, which moves faster: O₂ or He?
>
> Both have the same average KE. Since $v = \sqrt{2KE/m}$ and He has lower mass → **He moves faster**.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Convert to Kelvin for any kinetic energy calculation. Higher temperature = flatter, right-shifted Maxwell–Boltzmann peak. Don't confuse speed with KE — same T means same KE, but different speeds.

## 3.6 Deviations from the Ideal Gas Law

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between non-ideal gas behaviors, interparticle forces, and particle volumes. (3.6.A)

- Real gases deviate from $PV = nRT$ at **low temperature** and **high pressure**
- Two causes of deviation:

| Condition | Cause | Effect on measurement |
|---|---|---|
| Low temperature | IMFs pull particles together (slow-moving particles can't escape) | Observed $P$ **lower** than predicted |
| High pressure | Particle volume is no longer negligible (particles packed tightly) | Observed $V$ **greater** than predicted |

- Real gases behave most ideally at **high temperature and low pressure** (conditions that minimize IMFs and particle volume effects)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Look for condensation or liquefaction clues (IMFs dominating) or very high pressure (particle volume mattering) as indicators of non-ideal behavior.

## 3.7 Solutions and Mixtures

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Calculate the number of solute particles, volume, or molarity of solutions. (3.7.A)

- **Solution**: homogeneous mixture with uniform properties throughout
- **Molarity (M)**: concentration in moles of solute per liter of solution

$$M = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{liters of solution}}$$

**Key relationships:**
- $\text{moles} = M \times V(\text{L})$
- $V(\text{L}) = \dfrac{\text{moles}}{M}$

> [!example] Worked Example
> 0.500 mol NaCl dissolved in water to make 2.00 L of solution:
> $$M = \frac{0.500}{2.00} = 0.250 \text{ M NaCl}$$

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Always check what's being asked — molarity, volume, or moles. Always convert volume to liters before calculating.

## 3.8 Representations of Solutions

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Create particulate models for mixtures representing both interactions and concentrations of components. (3.8.A)

- Particulate diagrams show solute and solvent particles at the molecular level
- **Concentration**: more solute particles in same volume = higher concentration
- **Ionic compounds** separate into individual ions (NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻), not intact formula units
- **Water orientation**: O (δ−) faces cations; H (δ+) faces anions (ion-dipole forces)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> When interpreting a particulate diagram: (1) Is the solute ionic or molecular? (2) Are solvent-solute interactions shown correctly? (3) Does the particle count reflect the correct concentration?

## 3.9 Separation of Solutions and Mixtures

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the results of a separation experiment based on intermolecular interactions. (3.9.A)

- Filtration **cannot** separate components within a solution (homogeneous at molecular level)

### Chromatography

Separates based on how strongly substances interact with stationary vs. mobile phase:
- **Paper chromatography**: paper (stationary) + liquid solvent (mobile)
- **Thin-layer chromatography (TLC)**: silica/alumina plate + liquid solvent
- **Column chromatography**: solid silica column + flowing solvent

Polar substances adhere more to polar stationary phase → travel shorter distances. Nonpolar substances travel farther.

### Distillation

Separates liquids by **boiling point differences**:
- Weaker IMFs → lower boiling point → evaporates first → collected first
- Used to separate ethanol/water mixtures, purify organic compounds

## 3.10 Solubility

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain relationships between solubility of ionic and molecular compounds in aqueous and nonaqueous solvents and IMFs. (3.10.A)

**"Like dissolves like"**: substances with similar intermolecular forces tend to dissolve in each other.

| Solute type | Best solvent | Force driving dissolution |
|---|---|---|
| Ionic | Polar (water) | Ion-dipole |
| Polar molecular | Polar (water) | Dipole-dipole, H-bonding |
| Nonpolar | Nonpolar (hexane) | London dispersion |

### Dissolving Ionic Compounds in Water

Water molecules surround ions (**hydration**): O (δ−) orients toward cations, H (δ+) orients toward anions. Dissolution occurs when hydration energy > lattice energy.

### Why Some Things Don't Dissolve

- Large polar molecules without H-bonding capability dissolve poorly in water
- Nonpolar substances (oils) can't favorably interact with water's H-bonding network
- Water-water H-bonding is stronger than water-hexanol interaction → hexanol stays separate

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Dissolution = solute-solvent interactions must be at least as strong as solute-solute and solvent-solvent interactions. "Like dissolves like" is a shortcut for this principle.

## 3.11 Spectroscopy and the Electromagnetic Spectrum

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between electromagnetic spectrum regions and associated molecular or electronic transitions. (3.11.A)

- Photons carry discrete packets of energy; matter absorbs photons, reaching excited states
- Different radiation types cause different transitions

| Radiation | Energy | Transition caused |
|---|---|---|
| Microwave | Low | Rotational transitions (polar molecules rotate faster) |
| Infrared (IR) | Moderate | Vibrational transitions (bonds stretch and bend) |
| UV/Visible | High | Electronic transitions (electrons jump to higher levels) |

- Higher frequency = shorter wavelength = **higher energy**
- IR spectroscopy identifies functional groups; UV-Vis studies electronic structure

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> You don't need to memorize specific wavelengths — understand relative energies and which type causes which transition: micro → rotate, IR → vibrate, UV/Vis → electronic.

## 3.12 Properties of Photons

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain properties of an absorbed or emitted photon in relationship to an electronic transition in an atom or molecule. (3.12.A)

$$E = h \times f \qquad c = \lambda \times f$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Value/Unit |
|---|---|---|
| $E$ | Photon energy | J |
| $h$ | Planck's constant | $6.626 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s |
| $f$ (or $\nu$) | Frequency | s⁻¹ (Hz) |
| $c$ | Speed of light | $3.00 \times 10^8$ m/s |
| $\lambda$ | Wavelength | m |

- **Absorption**: photon energy absorbed → electron jumps to higher level
- **Emission**: electron falls to lower level → photon emitted
- Photon energy = **exactly** the energy difference between the two levels

> [!example] Worked Example
> Energy of a 400 nm photon:
> 1. Convert: $\lambda = 4.00 \times 10^{-7}$ m
> 2. Find $f$: $f = c/\lambda = 3.00 \times 10^8 / 4.00 \times 10^{-7} = 7.50 \times 10^{14}$ Hz
> 3. Find $E$: $E = hf = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 7.50 \times 10^{14} = 4.97 \times 10^{-19}$ J

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Convert nm → m before using $c = \lambda f$. Shorter wavelength = higher frequency = higher energy photon.

## 3.13 Beer-Lambert Law

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain how light absorption by a solution relates to concentration, path length, and molar absorptivity. (3.13.A)

$$A = \varepsilon b c$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| $A$ | Absorbance | unitless |
| $\varepsilon$ | Molar absorptivity | L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹ |
| $b$ | Path length | cm |
| $c$ | Concentration | mol/L |

- Absorbance is directly proportional to concentration (when $\varepsilon$ and $b$ are constant)
- In lab, path length is fixed (typically 1.00 cm); instrument uses wavelength of maximum absorption
- **Calibration curve**: plot $A$ vs. $c$ → linear graph → use to find unknown concentrations

> [!example] Worked Example
> $A = 0.650$, $\varepsilon = 1.30 \times 10^4$ L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹, $b = 1.00$ cm
> $$c = \frac{A}{\varepsilon b} = \frac{0.650}{1.30 \times 10^4 \times 1.00} = 5.00 \times 10^{-5} \text{ mol/L}$$

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> When given an absorbance vs. concentration graph, verify linearity before using it as a calibration curve. The slope = $\varepsilon b$.
