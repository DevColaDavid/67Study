# Unit 2: Compound Structure and Properties

## 2.1 Types of Chemical Bonds

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between bonding type and the properties of the participating elements. (2.1.A)

- **Electronegativity**: how strongly an atom attracts electrons in a bond; increases across a period, decreases down a group
- Bond type depends on electronegativity difference (ΔEN) between atoms

| Bond Type | ΔEN | Electron Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Nonpolar covalent | ~0 | Equal sharing |
| Polar covalent | moderate | Unequal sharing (δ+/δ−) |
| Ionic | large (metal + nonmetal) | Electron transfer |

### Covalent Bonding

Electrostatic attraction between nuclei of two atoms and a shared pair of electrons. Strength and length depend on the number of shared pairs: single < double < triple (stronger and shorter).

### Metallic Bonding

Valence electrons are delocalized — they form a "sea of electrons" surrounding positive metal ions. This explains conductivity, malleability, and luster.

### The Bonding Continuum

Bonding is a spectrum, not discrete categories. Greater electronegativity difference → more ionic character.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Don't just memorize categories — examine periodic trends, compare EN values, and identify partial charge distribution. Understand *why* each bond type has the properties it does.

## 2.2 Intramolecular Force and Potential Energy

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent the relationship between potential energy and distance between atoms based on factors that influence interaction strength. (2.2.A)

- Shorter bond distance generally → stronger bond, higher bond energy
- **Bond order**: number of shared electron pairs (single = 1, double = 2, triple = 3)
- On a potential energy vs. distance graph: minimum point = bond length; depth of well = bond energy
- Coulomb's Law for ionic bonds: $F \propto \dfrac{q_1 \times q_2}{r^2}$

### Potential Energy Curve

As two atoms approach each other:
1. Attractive forces dominate → potential energy decreases
2. Minimum reached at the equilibrium **bond length**
3. Too close → nuclear repulsion → energy rises steeply

### Bond Order, Length, and Strength

Higher bond order → more shared electrons → stronger attraction → **shorter and stronger bond**.

| Bond | Order | Length | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| C–C | 1 | longest | weakest |
| C=C | 2 | medium | medium |
| C≡C | 3 | shortest | strongest |

### Ionic Bond Strength (Coulomb's Law)

$F \propto \dfrac{q_1 \times q_2}{r^2}$

- Higher charges → stronger ionic attraction (e.g., MgO stronger than NaCl: 2+/2− vs. 1+/1−)
- Smaller ions → shorter distance → stronger attraction

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Use Coulomb's law for ionic comparisons; use bond order and atomic size for covalent comparisons. On PE graphs: identify bond length at the minimum, measure well depth for bond energy.

> [!example] Worked Example
> Which bond is stronger and shorter: N≡N or N–N?
>
> N≡N is a triple bond (order = 3); N–N is single (order = 1). More shared electrons in N≡N → stronger nuclear-electron attraction → **N≡N is both stronger and shorter**.

## 2.3 Structure of Ionic Solids

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Create particulate representations of ionic solids that align with Coulomb's Law and ion properties. (2.3.A)

- Ionic compounds form repeating 3D **crystal lattices** that maximize cation-anion attraction and minimize like-charge repulsion
- Stronger ionic bonds: larger charges AND smaller ionic radii (Coulomb's Law)
- Properties arise from lattice structure: high melting points, brittleness, conductivity only when molten or dissolved

### The Ionic Lattice

Cations and anions alternate in a regular 3D grid. Each ion is surrounded by ions of opposite charge, maximizing electrostatic attraction.

**Properties explained by lattice structure:**
- **High melting point**: strong electrostatic forces require lots of energy to break
- **Brittleness**: shifting layers aligns like charges → repulsion → shatters
- **Conductivity**: only when ions are free to move (molten or in solution); solid lattice = no conductivity

> [!example] Comparing Lattice Strength
> **NaCl vs. MgO:**
> - NaCl: 1+ and 1− charges, larger ions → weaker attraction
> - MgO: 2+ and 2− charges, smaller ions → much stronger attraction → higher melting point

## 2.4 Structure of Metals and Alloys

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Use models to represent metallic solids and alloys, demonstrating essential characteristics of their structures. (2.4.A)

- Metallic bonding: positive metal cations in a **"sea of delocalized electrons"**
- Delocalized electrons explain metals' key properties

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| High melting/boiling point | Strong cation-electron attraction |
| Electrical conductivity | Free electron movement |
| Thermal conductivity | Free electron movement |
| Malleability/ductility | Ion layers slide without breaking bonds |
| Metallic luster | Mobile electrons reflect light |

### Alloys

An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with other elements, improving properties.

| Alloy Type | Structure | Example | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Interstitial** | Smaller atoms fill gaps between metal atoms | Steel (C in Fe) | Blocks layer sliding → harder |
| **Substitutional** | Similar-sized atoms replace metal atoms | Brass (Zn in Cu) | Combined properties |

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> When modeling metallic solids or alloys: reference delocalized electrons, positive metal ions, and specify whether the alloy is interstitial or substitutional, noting relative atom sizes.

## 2.5 Lewis Diagrams

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent a molecule with a Lewis diagram. (2.5.A)

- Lewis structures show valence electron arrangements: lines = bonds, dots = lone pairs
- Each line = 2 electrons shared; each dot pair = 2 nonbonding electrons

### Steps to Draw a Lewis Structure

1. Count total valence electrons (subtract 1 per negative charge; add 1 per positive charge for ions)
2. Identify the central atom (usually least electronegative; never H)
3. Connect all atoms with single bonds
4. Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs to satisfy octets (or duet for H)
5. If electrons run out before octets are satisfied, convert lone pairs to multiple bonds

### Special Cases

| Case | Rule |
|---|---|
| Hydrogen | Only 2 electrons (duet) |
| Boron, Beryllium | Often stable with fewer than 8 electrons (electron deficient) |
| Period 3+ elements | Can have expanded octets (more than 8 electrons) |

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Always count total valence electrons before and after drawing — the number must be conserved. Check every atom's octet (except H, B, Be).

> [!warning] Common Mistakes
> - Giving hydrogen more than 2 electrons
> - Forgetting to adjust for ionic charge
> - Skipping octet verification
> - Placing multiple bonds incorrectly

## 2.6 Resonance and Formal Charge

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent molecules with Lewis diagrams accounting for resonance or using formal charge to select between nonequivalent structures. (2.6.A)

- **Resonance**: when multiple valid Lewis structures exist differing only in electron (not atom) positions
- The real structure is a **resonance hybrid** — an average of all valid forms
- **Formal charge** identifies the most accurate Lewis structure

$$\text{Formal charge} = \text{valence e}^- - \text{nonbonding e}^- - \tfrac{1}{2}(\text{bonding e}^-)$$

### Choosing the Best Structure

Preferred Lewis structures:
1. Minimize formal charges (charges closest to zero)
2. Avoid adjacent like charges
3. Place negative formal charges on more electronegative atoms

### Resonance Examples

- **Ozone (O₃)**: two equivalent resonance structures; actual bond order = 1.5
- **Carbonate (CO₃²⁻)**: three equivalent structures; bond order = 4/3
- **Benzene (C₆H₆)**: alternating single/double bonds → all bonds equivalent (order = 1.5)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> When two structures are non-equivalent, use formal charge to pick the better one. Structures with formal charges closer to zero and negative charges on electronegative atoms are preferred.

### Limitations of Lewis Structures

Lewis structures cannot represent delocalized electrons well, don't predict molecular shape, and struggle with radical species (odd electron counts).

## 2.7 VSEPR and Hybridization

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain structural and electron properties of molecules based on Lewis diagrams, VSEPR theory, bond orders, and polarities. (2.7.A)

- **VSEPR theory**: electron pairs (bonding and lone) arrange to minimize repulsion
- Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs → compress bond angles
- Hybridization correlates with number of electron domains around central atom

### VSEPR Molecular Geometries

| Electron Domains | Lone Pairs | Geometry | Bond Angle |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 0 | Linear | 180° |
| 3 | 0 | Trigonal planar | 120° |
| 3 | 1 | Bent | <120° |
| 4 | 0 | Tetrahedral | 109.5° |
| 4 | 1 | Trigonal pyramidal | <109.5° |
| 4 | 2 | Bent | <109.5° |
| 5 | 0 | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°/120° |
| 6 | 0 | Octahedral | 90° |

### Hybridization

| Electron Domains | Hybridization | Geometry |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | sp | Linear |
| 3 | sp² | Trigonal planar |
| 4 | sp³ | Tetrahedral |

### Sigma and Pi Bonds

- **Sigma (σ) bonds**: direct orbital overlap; present in ALL covalent bonds (one per bond); rotation is possible
- **Pi (π) bonds**: sideways p-orbital overlap; only in double and triple bonds; restrict rotation

Single bond = 1σ | Double bond = 1σ + 1π | Triple bond = 1σ + 2π

### Molecular Polarity

A molecule is polar if it has **polar bonds AND an asymmetric shape**. Symmetric molecules with polar bonds (e.g., CO₂, linear) are nonpolar overall because dipoles cancel.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Distinguish electron geometry (all domains) from molecular geometry (only atoms). Count ALL electron domains (bonding + lone pairs) to determine hybridization.
