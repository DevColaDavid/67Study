# Unit 1: Atomic Structure and Properties

## 1.1 The Mole

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Calculate quantities of a substance or its relative number of particles using dimensional analysis and the mole concept. (1.1.A)

- Atoms and molecules are too small to count individually — the **mole** serves as a counting unit
- $1 \text{ mol} = 6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles (Avogadro's number)
- Molar mass (g/mol) numerically equals the average mass of one particle in amu
- Dimensional analysis converts between mass, moles, and particle counts

**Key conversions:**
- $\text{moles} = \dfrac{\text{mass (g)}}{\text{molar mass (g/mol)}}$
- $\text{mass (g)} = \text{moles} \times \text{molar mass (g/mol)}$
- $\text{particles} = \text{moles} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}$
- $\text{moles} = \dfrac{\text{particles}}{6.022 \times 10^{23}}$

### The Mole as a Counting Unit

A mole represents $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles. Just like "dozen" = 12 items, "mole" = this specific quantity of atoms, molecules, or formula units depending on substance type.

### Why the Mole Is Useful

Molar mass connects measurable mass to particle quantities. The numerical value of molar mass (g/mol) equals the average mass of a single particle (amu).

**Example:** Carbon's average atomic mass is 12.01 amu, so its molar mass is 12.01 g/mol — meaning 12.01 g contains exactly $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ atoms.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Specify what particles you're counting: **atoms** for elements (Cu, Fe); **molecules** for covalent compounds (H₂O); **formula units** for ionic compounds (NaCl).

> [!example] Worked Example
> **How many molecules are in 9.00 g of water (H₂O)?**
>
> 1. Molar mass of H₂O: $(1.01 \times 2) + 16.00 = 18.02$ g/mol
> 2. Convert to moles: $\dfrac{9.00}{18.02} = 0.499$ mol
> 3. Convert to molecules: $0.499 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 3.00 \times 10^{23}$ molecules

## 1.2 Mass Spectra of Elements

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the quantitative relationship between the mass spectrum of an element and the masses of the element's isotopes. (1.2.A)

- Elements naturally occur as **isotope mixtures** — same proton count, different neutron count
- **Mass spectrometry** separates isotopes by mass-to-charge ratio (m/z)
- Mass spectrum: x-axis = mass (m/z), y-axis = relative abundance (%)
- **Average atomic mass** = weighted average of all isotope masses × their abundances

$$\text{avg atomic mass} = \sum(\text{isotope mass} \times \text{fractional abundance})$$

### Isotopes

Isotopes share the same number of protons (same element) but differ in neutron count, producing different masses. Chlorine exists naturally as Cl-35 (75%) and Cl-37 (25%), giving an average mass of 35.5 amu.

### Reading a Mass Spectrum

Each peak in the spectrum represents a different isotope. Peak height = relative abundance. In AP Chemistry, only singly charged monatomic ions are considered (z = 1), so m/z = mass.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> When bar heights appear without labeled percentages, treat heights as relative values. Divide each bar height by the total to get fractional abundances.

> [!example] Worked Example
> **Calculate average atomic mass of chlorine:**
> - Cl-35: mass = 34.97 amu, abundance = 75.78%
> - Cl-37: mass = 36.97 amu, abundance = 24.22%
>
> $(34.97 \times 0.7578) + (36.97 \times 0.2422) = 26.50 + 8.96 = \mathbf{35.46 \text{ amu}}$

## 1.3 Elemental Composition of Pure Substances

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the quantitative relationship between elemental composition by mass and the empirical formula of a pure substance. (1.3.A)

- **Empirical formula**: simplest whole-number ratio of atoms
- **Molecular formula**: actual number of atoms per molecule
- **Law of definite proportions**: a pure compound always has the same elemental mass ratios
- To find empirical formula: convert mass → moles → divide by smallest → scale to whole numbers

### Empirical vs. Molecular Formula

| Formula | Meaning | Example (hydrogen peroxide) |
|---|---|---|
| Molecular | Actual atom counts | H₂O₂ |
| Empirical | Simplest ratio | HO |

Glucose: molecular = C₆H₁₂O₆, empirical = CH₂O.

### Finding the Empirical Formula

1. Convert mass or % to moles (divide by molar mass)
2. Divide all mole values by the smallest
3. Multiply to get whole numbers if needed

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Always check that the final empirical formula uses whole numbers. A ratio like 1.5 means multiply everything by 2.

> [!example] Worked Example
> **Find empirical formula for: C 52.2%, H 13.0%, O 34.8%**
>
> Assume 100 g: C = 4.35 mol, H = 12.9 mol, O = 2.18 mol
>
> Divide by 2.18: C = 2.00, H = 5.92 ≈ 6, O = 1.00
>
> **Empirical formula: C₂H₆O**

## 1.4 Composition of Mixtures

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the quantitative relationship between elemental composition by mass and the composition of substances in a mixture. (1.4.A)

- **Pure substances**: single type of atom, molecule, or formula unit; fixed composition
- **Mixtures**: two or more substances in variable proportions; separable by physical means
- Elemental analysis (% by mass) can determine purity or identify mixture composition

### Pure Substances vs. Mixtures

Pure substances maintain fixed composition and consistent properties. Mixtures (e.g., air = N₂ + O₂ + CO₂) retain the original properties of each component and can be separated by filtration, distillation, etc.

### Using Elemental Analysis to Check Purity

Compare measured mass percentages to the theoretical composition of the expected pure compound.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Don't assume all measured compositions are accurate — even small deviations can indicate impurities or a mixture.

> [!example] Worked Example
> A sample shows 60.3% Mg and 39.7% O. Is it pure MgO?
>
> Theoretical for MgO: $\dfrac{24.31}{40.31} \times 100 = 60.3\%$ Mg, $39.7\%$ O ✓ — sample is pure MgO.

## 1.5 Atomic Structure and Electron Configuration

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent the electron configuration of an element or ion using the Aufbau principle. (1.5.A)

- Atoms: dense positive nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons
- **Aufbau principle**: electrons fill lowest-energy orbitals first
- Shells (n = 1, 2, 3…) contain subshells (s, p, d, f)
- **Core electrons**: inner shells; **valence electrons**: outermost shell
- Coulomb's Law: $F \propto \dfrac{q_1 \times q_2}{r^2}$ — stronger attraction with greater charge, shorter distance

**Subshell capacity:**

| Subshell | Orbitals | Max electrons |
|---|---|---|
| s | 1 | 2 |
| p | 3 | 6 |
| d | 5 | 10 |
| f | 7 | 14 |

**Filling order:** 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p …

### Electron Configurations

**Full notation:**
- O (Z = 8): $1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^4$
- Ca (Z = 20): $1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^6 \, 3s^2 \, 3p^6 \, 4s^2$

**Noble gas shorthand:**
- Ca = [Ar] $4s^2$
- Fe = [Ar] $3d^6 \, 4s^2$

### Electron Configurations of Ions

- **Cations**: remove electrons from highest principal energy level first
  - Na → Na⁺: $1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^6 \, 3s^1$ → $1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^6$
- **Anions**: add electrons to next available orbital
  - Cl → Cl⁻: $[\text{Ne}] \, 3s^2 \, 3p^5$ → $[\text{Ne}] \, 3s^2 \, 3p^6$
- **Transition metals**: remove 4s before 3d when ionizing
  - Fe → Fe²⁺: [Ar] $3d^6$ (4s electrons removed first)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> For ions, remove electrons from the outermost shell (highest n) first. Double-check total electron count matches the element's atomic number adjusted for charge.

## 1.6 Photoelectron Spectroscopy

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between the photoelectron spectrum of an atom or ion and its electron configuration and electron-nucleus interactions. (1.6.A)

- PES measures the energy needed to remove electrons from each subshell (**binding energy**)
- X-axis: binding energy (MJ/mol); Y-axis: relative number of electrons
- Each peak = one subshell; peak height = number of electrons in that subshell
- Higher binding energy = closer to nucleus = harder to remove
- PES data directly confirms ground-state electron configurations

### Interpreting a PES Spectrum

Core electrons (1s) appear at the far high-energy end. Valence electrons appear at the low-energy end. Shielding by inner electrons reduces outer-electron binding energies.

**Oxygen** (1s² 2s² 2p⁴) produces three peaks:
- High binding energy, height 2 → 1s
- Lower energy, height 2 → 2s
- Lowest energy, height 4 → 2p

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Watch the x-axis direction — binding energy often *decreases* to the right, which can be counterintuitive. The rightmost (lowest binding energy) peak represents valence electrons.

> [!example] Worked Example
> Element X has three PES peaks: height 2 (high energy), height 2 (medium), height 5 (low energy).
>
> Configuration: $1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^5$ → Element X is **fluorine**.

## 1.7 Periodic Trends

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between trends in atomic properties and electronic structure and periodicity. (1.7.A)

- **Effective nuclear charge** $Z_\text{eff}$ = nuclear charge − shielding from core electrons
- Higher $Z_\text{eff}$ → smaller radius, higher ionization energy, higher electronegativity

| Property | Across period (→) | Down group (↓) |
|---|---|---|
| Atomic radius | Decreases | Increases |
| Ionization energy | Increases | Decreases |
| Electron affinity | More negative | Less negative |
| Electronegativity | Increases | Decreases |

### Ionization Energy

Energy to remove an electron from a gaseous atom. Increases left → right (stronger nuclear attraction); decreases top → bottom (more shielding, greater distance).

**Exceptions:** filled or half-filled subshells show slightly lower ionization energy than expected (e.g., O < N, Be > B).

### Atomic Radius

Decreases across a period (more protons pull electrons in); increases down a group (new energy shells added).

### Ionic Radius

Cations are **smaller** than neutral atoms (fewer electrons, same nuclear charge). Anions are **larger** (more electrons, more repulsion).

### Electronegativity

Fluorine has the highest electronegativity (4.0); decreases down and to the left. Francium has the lowest.

> [!example] Worked Example
> Which has higher first ionization energy: Na or Mg?
>
> Mg has one more proton (Z = 12 vs. 11) with the same core electrons → higher $Z_\text{eff}$ → holds valence electrons more tightly → **Mg has higher IE**.

## 1.8 Valence Electrons and Ionic Compounds

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between trends in reactivity of elements and periodicity. (1.8.A)

- **Valence electrons** (outermost shell) determine bonding and reactivity
- Group number = number of valence electrons (for main-group elements)
- Atoms gain/lose electrons to reach a stable octet (8 outer electrons)

**Ion formation by group:**

| Group | Type | Charge | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Metal | 1+ | Na⁺ |
| 2 | Metal | 2+ | Mg²⁺ |
| 13 | Metal | 3+ | Al³⁺ |
| 15 | Nonmetal | 3− | N³⁻ |
| 16 | Nonmetal | 2− | O²⁻ |
| 17 | Nonmetal | 1− | Cl⁻ |

### Ionic Compound Formation

Ionic compounds form through electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. Formulas reflect charge balance.

- **NaCl**: Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → 1:1 ratio
- **MgO**: Mg²⁺ + O²⁻ → 1:1 ratio
- **CaF₂**: Ca²⁺ + 2 F⁻ → 1:2 ratio (two F⁻ balance one Ca²⁺)

### Reactivity Trends

- Group 1 and 2 metals: highly reactive (readily lose electrons)
- Group 17 halogens: highly reactive (readily gain electrons)
- Same-group elements form analogous compounds due to identical valence electron counts

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> To predict ionic charge, count valence electrons and determine how many must be lost or gained to complete the outer shell. Relate charge back to periodic table position.

> [!example] Worked Example
> **Why does calcium form Ca²⁺?**
>
> Ca is in Group 2 → 2 valence electrons. Losing both achieves the [Ar] configuration (stable octet). Result: **Ca²⁺**.
