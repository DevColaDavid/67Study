# Unit 6: Thermochemistry

## 6.1 Endothermic and Exothermic Processes

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between experimental observations and energy changes associated with a chemical or physical transformation. (6.1.A)

- **Exothermic**: system releases energy → surroundings warm up; ΔH < 0
- **Endothermic**: system absorbs energy → surroundings cool down; ΔH > 0
- **System**: the reacting substances; **surroundings**: everything else (solution, container, atmosphere)
- Energy is conserved: energy lost by system = energy gained by surroundings

### Identifying from Observations

| Observation | Process type |
|---|---|
| Surroundings get warmer | Exothermic |
| Surroundings get cooler | Endothermic |
| Combustion, neutralization | Typically exothermic |
| Melting, many dissolving processes | Typically endothermic |

**First Law of Thermodynamics:** $\Delta E = q + w$ (total energy change = heat + work)

### Dissolution

Dissolution can be exothermic or endothermic depending on whether the energy released forming solute-solvent interactions exceeds the energy required to break solute-solute and solvent-solvent interactions. NaOH dissolving in water is exothermic (warms); NH₄NO₃ dissolving is endothermic (cools — used in cold packs).

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Temperature change in the surroundings indicates the direction of heat flow, not the magnitude. A warm test tube signals an exothermic process; a cold test tube signals endothermic.

## 6.2 Energy Diagrams

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Represent a chemical or physical transformation with an energy diagram. (6.2.A)

- Energy diagrams show potential energy (y-axis) vs. reaction progress (x-axis)
- Reactants on the left, products on the right
- $\Delta E$ = energy of products − energy of reactants
- $\Delta E < 0$: exothermic (products lower); $\Delta E > 0$: endothermic (products higher)

### Reading Energy Diagrams

**Exothermic reaction**: products sit lower than reactants → energy released to surroundings.

**Endothermic reaction**: products sit higher than reactants → energy absorbed from surroundings.

**Physical transitions:**
- Melting: endothermic (solid → liquid, absorbs energy)
- Freezing: exothermic (liquid → solid, releases energy)
- Vaporization: endothermic; condensation: exothermic

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> $\Delta E$ is measured from reactant level to product level — it's the net energy change, not the peak height. Don't confuse $\Delta E$ with activation energy $E_a$.

## 6.3 Heat Transfer and Thermal Equilibrium

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between the transfer of thermal energy and molecular collisions. (6.3.A)

- Heat flows from **hot → cold** until **thermal equilibrium** is reached
- Temperature = measure of average kinetic energy of particles
- Heat transfer occurs through particle collisions: fast particles (hot) transfer energy to slow particles (cold)
- At thermal equilibrium: same temperature, same average kinetic energy, no net heat transfer

### Molecular Mechanism

Warmer objects contain faster-moving particles. When a hot object contacts a cold one, collisions between fast and slow particles transfer kinetic energy — the fast particle slows, the slow particle speeds up. This continues until both have the same average kinetic energy (thermal equilibrium).

Total energy is conserved: $q_\text{lost by hot} = -q_\text{gained by cold}$

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Heat transfer is always spontaneous from higher to lower temperature. Energy does not flow from cold to hot without external work input. At thermal equilibrium, temperatures are equal — not total energy amounts.

## 6.4 Heat Capacity and Calorimetry

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Calculate heat absorbed or released by a system based on substance amount, heat capacity, and temperature change. (6.4.A)

- **Specific heat capacity** ($c$): energy required to raise 1 g of a substance by 1°C; units: J/(g·°C)
- Water has a very high specific heat: $c = 4.18$ J/(g·°C)

$$q = mc\Delta T$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Units |
|---|---|---|
| $q$ | Heat transferred | J |
| $m$ | Mass | g |
| $c$ | Specific heat capacity | J/(g·°C) |
| $\Delta T$ | Temperature change ($T_f - T_i$) | °C |

### Calorimetry

In a calorimeter, assume no heat loss to surroundings:

$$q_\text{reaction} = -q_\text{solution}$$

Energy released by reaction = energy absorbed by solution (and vice versa).

> [!example] Worked Example
> 50.0 g of water is heated from 20.0°C to 35.0°C. How much heat was absorbed?
>
> $q = mc\Delta T = (50.0)(4.18)(35.0 - 20.0) = (50.0)(4.18)(15.0) = 3135$ J = **3.14 kJ**

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Sign of $q$: positive = endothermic (system absorbs heat); negative = exothermic (system releases heat). In a coffee-cup calorimeter, all heat goes into the solution — treat the solution mass as the calorimeter mass.

## 6.5 Energy of Phase Changes

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain changes in heat absorbed or released by a system undergoing a phase transition based on moles and molar enthalpy of the phase transition. (6.5.A)

- Phase changes occur **at constant temperature** — energy goes into breaking/forming intermolecular forces, not raising kinetic energy
- **Endothermic** phase changes (absorb energy): melting, vaporization, sublimation
- **Exothermic** phase changes (release energy): freezing, condensation, deposition

$$q = n\Delta H_\text{phase}$$

| Phase change | Formula | Sign of $\Delta H$ |
|---|---|---|
| Melting | $q = n\Delta H_\text{fus}$ | + (endothermic) |
| Freezing | $q = n\Delta H_\text{fus}$ | − (exothermic) |
| Vaporization | $q = n\Delta H_\text{vap}$ | + (endothermic) |
| Condensation | $q = n\Delta H_\text{vap}$ | − (exothermic) |

$$\Delta H_\text{freezing} = -\Delta H_\text{fusion} \qquad \Delta H_\text{condensation} = -\Delta H_\text{vaporization}$$

### Heating Curves

A heating curve (temperature vs. heat added) shows:
- **Sloped regions**: temperature rises as heat is added ($q = mc\Delta T$)
- **Flat plateaus**: temperature constant during phase change ($q = n\Delta H_\text{phase}$)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> On a plateau, all energy breaks IMFs — temperature doesn't change. Steeper slopes = lower heat capacity. Longer plateaus = larger enthalpy of phase change.

## 6.6 Introduction to Enthalpy of Reaction

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Calculate the heat absorbed or released by a system undergoing a chemical reaction in relationship to moles and molar enthalpy of reaction. (6.6.A)

- **Enthalpy of reaction** ($\Delta H_r$): heat exchanged at constant pressure per mole of reaction
- $\Delta H < 0$: exothermic; $\Delta H > 0$: endothermic

$$q = n \times \Delta H_r$$

where $n$ = moles of the substance specified in the balanced equation.

### Sign Convention

The sign of $\Delta H$ reflects the system (reactants + products):
- Exothermic: system loses energy → $\Delta H < 0$, surroundings warm
- Endothermic: system gains energy → $\Delta H > 0$, surroundings cool

> [!example] Worked Example
> CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l), $\Delta H = -890$ kJ/mol
>
> Heat released by 0.500 mol CH₄:
> $q = 0.500 \times (-890) = -445$ kJ (445 kJ released)

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Match $n$ to the specific substance's moles in the balanced equation. If 2 mol H₂O has $\Delta H = -890$ kJ, then 1 mol H₂O releases 445 kJ. Always maintain consistent units (J vs. kJ).

## 6.7 Bond Enthalpies

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Calculate enthalpy change based on average bond energies of bonds broken and formed in reactions. (6.7.A)

- **Bond enthalpy**: energy required to break 1 mol of a bond in the gaseous state
- **Breaking bonds**: endothermic (requires energy input)
- **Forming bonds**: exothermic (releases energy)

$$\Delta H_r = \sum(\text{bond energies broken}) - \sum(\text{bond energies formed})$$

### Calculation Steps

1. Draw Lewis structures of all reactants and products
2. Identify all bonds broken in reactants; sum their energies (positive)
3. Identify all bonds formed in products; sum their energies (positive)
4. Apply formula: $\Delta H_r = \Sigma E_\text{broken} - \Sigma E_\text{formed}$

> [!example] Worked Example
> CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O (all gaseous)
>
> Bonds broken: 4 C–H + 2 O=O = 4(413) + 2(498) = 1652 + 996 = 2648 kJ
>
> Bonds formed: 2 C=O (in CO₂) + 4 O–H = 2(799) + 4(463) = 1598 + 1852 = 3450 kJ
>
> $\Delta H_r = 2648 - 3450 = -802$ kJ (approximate; calorimetry gives −890 kJ)

> [!warning] Limitation
> Bond enthalpy calculations are **approximations** — average bond energies vary with molecular environment. Results differ from calorimetric values. Only valid for gaseous species; add enthalpy of vaporization if reactants/products are liquids.

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> "Breaking bonds IN, forming bonds OUT" — energy goes in to break, energy comes out when formed. The sign of the answer tells you exo vs. endo.

## 6.8 Enthalpy of Formation

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Calculate the enthalpy change for a chemical or physical process based on the standard enthalpies of formation. (6.8.A)

- **Standard enthalpy of formation** ($\Delta H_f°$): enthalpy change when 1 mol of a compound forms from its elements in their standard states at 298 K, 1 atm
- Elements in their standard states have $\Delta H_f° = 0$ (e.g., O₂(g), C(graphite), H₂(g), Na(s))

$$\Delta H°_\text{rxn} = \sum\Delta H_f°(\text{products}) - \sum\Delta H_f°(\text{reactants})$$

Multiply each $\Delta H_f°$ by its stoichiometric coefficient before summing.

> [!example] Worked Example
> CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)
>
> $\Delta H_f°$ values (kJ/mol): CH₄ = −74.8; O₂ = 0; CO₂ = −393.5; H₂O(l) = −285.8
>
> Products: $1(-393.5) + 2(-285.8) = -965.1$ kJ
>
> Reactants: $1(-74.8) + 2(0) = -74.8$ kJ
>
> $\Delta H°_\text{rxn} = -965.1 - (-74.8) = \mathbf{-890.3 \text{ kJ}}$

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Always multiply $\Delta H_f°$ by the stoichiometric coefficient (moles). Elements in standard states always contribute zero. Double-check that you subtracted reactants FROM products.

## 6.9 Hess's Law

> [!note] Learning Objective
> Explain the relationship between overall enthalpy change and the sum of individual step enthalpies. (6.9.A)

- **Hess's Law**: the total enthalpy change for a reaction is the same regardless of the pathway taken — only the initial and final states matter
- Based on the first law of thermodynamics: energy is conserved

### Manipulation Rules

| Operation | Effect on ΔH |
|---|---|
| Reverse a reaction | Change sign of ΔH |
| Multiply by a factor | Multiply ΔH by same factor |
| Add reactions | Add ΔH values |

$$\Delta H°_\text{overall} = \Delta H_1 + \Delta H_2 + \Delta H_3 + \cdots$$

### Strategy

1. Identify species that must cancel (intermediates) vs. appear in the target equation
2. Reverse and/or multiply given reactions to align those species
3. Add all reactions — intermediate species cancel; sum the $\Delta H$ values

> [!example] Worked Example
> Target: C(s) + ½O₂(g) → CO(g)
>
> Given: (1) C + O₂ → CO₂, $\Delta H_1 = -393.5$ kJ; (2) CO + ½O₂ → CO₂, $\Delta H_2 = -283.0$ kJ
>
> Strategy: Use equation (1) as-is; reverse equation (2) to get CO₂ → CO + ½O₂, $\Delta H = +283.0$ kJ
>
> Adding: C + O₂ + CO₂ → CO₂ + CO + ½O₂ → C + ½O₂ → CO
>
> $\Delta H = -393.5 + 283.0 = -110.5$ kJ

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> Before adding, check that each intermediate species cancels completely. Always double-check your final equation matches the target. Hess's law is why enthalpies of formation are so useful — they let you calculate any reaction's $\Delta H$ without doing it directly.
