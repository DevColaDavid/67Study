# Period 4 — New Republic (1800–1848)

---

## 4.2 — The Rise of Political Parties and the Era of Jefferson

### The Election of 1800 ("Revolution of 1800")
- **Federalists (Adams):** Strong national government; pro-Britain; protective tariffs.
- **Democratic-Republicans (Jefferson):** States' rights; pro-France; lower tariffs.
- **Result:** Jefferson won — first peaceful transfer of power between parties.

### Jefferson's Presidency
- Maintained national bank and debt-repayment plan (pragmatic compromise).
- Reduced military, repealed excise taxes, lowered national debt.

### The Louisiana Purchase (1803)
- Napoleon sold the Louisiana Territory (needed funds for European wars + lost Santo Domingo to **Toussaint Louverture's rebellion**).
- Jefferson (strict constructionist) wrestled with constitutionality → justified via **treaty-making powers**.
- **$15 million** → doubled U.S. size; removed European presence from borders.
- **Lewis and Clark Expedition** — mapped the territory; documented species; strengthened claims to Oregon.

### John Marshall's Supreme Court ⭐
*Chief Justice 1801–1835; favored strong central government and property rights.*

| Case | Year | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Marbury v. Madison** ⭐ | 1803 | Established **judicial review** — Supreme Court can declare acts unconstitutional |
| **Fletcher v. Peck** | 1810 | First time a *state* law declared unconstitutional; sanctity of contracts |
| **Martin v. Hunter's Lease** | 1816 | Supreme Court jurisdiction over state courts on constitutional matters |
| **Dartmouth College v. Woodward** | 1819 | State cannot alter private corporation charter — protected private contracts |
| **McCulloch v. Maryland** ⭐ | 1819 | States cannot tax federal institution; affirmed **implied powers** and national bank |
| **Cohens v. Virginia** | 1821 | Supreme Court can review state court decisions involving federal authority |
| **Gibbons v. Ogden** ⭐ | 1824 | Federal government has broad control over **interstate commerce** |

## 4.3 — Politics and Regional Interests

### Era of Good Feelings (Monroe, 1816–1824)
- Federalist Party faded; apparent political unity.
- Underlying tensions: tariffs, national bank, internal improvements, **rising sectionalism over slavery**.

### Henry Clay's American System
1. **Protective Tariffs** — promote manufacturing, raise revenue.
2. **National Bank** — stable currency and credit.
3. **Internal Improvements** — federally funded infrastructure.

### Tariff of 1816
- First protective tariff; protected U.S. manufacturers from British competition.

### The Panic of 1819
- Triggered by Second Bank tightening credit.
- Bank closures, unemployment, foreclosures → weakened faith in the national bank.

### The Missouri Compromise (1820)

**Background:** Missouri's application for statehood (as a slave state) threatened the 11 free / 11 slave Senate balance.

**Henry Clay's Solution:**
1. Admit **Missouri** as a slave state.
2. Admit **Maine** as a free state.
3. Prohibit slavery north of **36° 30'** latitude in the rest of the Louisiana Territory.

> **Preserved the balance** between free and slave states for 30+ years — until the Compromise of 1850 and the Kansas-Nebraska Act.

## 4.4 — America on the World Stage

### Jefferson's Foreign Policy
- Aimed to avoid war; maintained neutrality in Napoleonic Wars.
- **Chesapeake-Leopard Affair (1807):** British attacked U.S. warship → anti-British sentiment.
- **Embargo Act (1807):** Prohibited American ships from foreign ports → hurt U.S. economy more than Britain; repealed 1809.

### War of 1812
**Causes:**
- British impressment of American sailors.
- **War Hawks** (Clay, Calhoun) — wanted to defend American honor, gain Canada, destroy Native resistance.
- Britain suspended naval blockade just as Madison declared war (news arrived too late).

**Key Events:**

| Event | Details |
|---|---|
| Invasion of Canada | Failed |
| USS Constitution | Notable naval victory |
| Chesapeake Campaign | British burned Washington, D.C.; failed to take Baltimore → **"The Star-Spangled Banner"** |
| **Battle of New Orleans** | Jackson's victory after the peace treaty was signed |
| **Treaty of Ghent (1814)** | Status quo ante bellum; prewar boundaries restored; maritime issues unresolved |
| Hartford Convention | New England Federalists (opposed war) discussed secession → labeled unpatriotic |

**Legacy:**
- U.S. gained international respect.
- Federalist Party collapsed.
- Native Americans lost British support → forced to surrender land.
- **Andrew Jackson** and **William Henry Harrison** emerged as political figures.
- Nationalism grew; Americans looked westward.

### Monroe Doctrine (1823)
- Declared Western Hemisphere **closed to further European colonization**.
- U.S. would oppose any European interference in Western Hemisphere republics.
- Backed by British navy, not U.S. military (initially).
- **Long-term significance:** Foundation of U.S. foreign policy; extended by Roosevelt Corollary (1904).

### Key Treaties Under Monroe

| Treaty | Year | Terms |
|---|---|---|
| Rush-Bagot Agreement | 1817 | Limited naval armaments on Great Lakes; unfortified US-Canada border |
| Treaty of 1818 | 1818 | Joint Oregon occupation; fishing rights; 49th parallel northern boundary |
| Adams-Onís Treaty | 1819 | Spain ceded Florida; U.S. gave up Texas claims; $5 million |

## 4.5 — Market Revolution: Industrialization

### Infrastructure

| Innovation | Details |
|---|---|
| Lancaster Turnpike | Philadelphia → Lancaster; stimulated road construction |
| National (Cumberland) Road | Maryland → Illinois |
| Erie Canal (1825) | Western farms ↔ eastern cities |
| Steamboats | Clermont (1807, Robert Fulton); reduced shipping times |
| Railroads | 1820s–1830s; competed with canals; grew cities like Cleveland, Cincinnati, Chicago |
| Telegraph (1844) | Samuel Morse; near-instant communication; transformed business and government |

### Agricultural Innovations
- **Steel plow** (John Deere) and **mechanical reaper** (Cyrus McCormick) → increased efficiency.

### Industrial Growth

| Person / System | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Samuel Slater | First U.S. textile factory (1791) |
| Eli Whitney | Cotton gin (1793); interchangeable parts → mass production |
| Lowell System | Young farm women in mills; company dormitories |
| Child Labor | Children as young as 7 worked in factories |

### Cotton and the South
- **Cotton gin** made cotton more profitable → massive demand for enslaved labor.
- Cotton dominated the Southern economy; New England and European mills relied on it.

## 4.6 — Market Revolution: Society and Culture

### Women's Changing Roles
- **Cult of Domesticity:** Women as moral leaders in the home while men worked outside.
- Limited employment options (domestic service, teaching, factory — Lowell System).
- Legal restrictions: couldn't vote; limited property rights.

### Population and Immigration
- U.S. population doubled every 25 years (1800–1850).
- Post-1832 immigration surge (peaked at 428,000 in 1854): causes — cheap ocean transport, European famines/revolutions, economic opportunity.
- Mostly settled in northern cities and the Old Northwest.

### Urbanization
- Urban population grew from 5% (1800) to 15% (1850).
- New cities at transportation hubs: Buffalo, Cleveland, Detroit, Chicago, Cincinnati, St. Louis.

### Organized Labor
- *Commonwealth v. Hunt* (1842) — affirmed right to organize and negotiate contracts.
- Challenges: depressions, employer hostility, immigrant labor supply limiting union effectiveness.

## 4.7 — Expanding Democracy

### Politics of the Common Man (1824–1840)
- New suffrage laws → removed property/religious qualifications; all White males could vote.
- Voter turnout: 350,000 (1824) → 2.4 million (1840).
- Political parties used nominating conventions instead of caucuses.

### Spoils System (Jackson)
- Appointed loyal Democrats to federal jobs.
- **Rotation in Office** — limited terms to allow more citizens to participate.

---

## 4.8 — Jackson and Federal Power

### Election of 1828
- Jackson won decisively, appealing as a war hero and man of the frontier.
- **Tariff of Abominations (1828)** — hurt Southern planters; worsened sectional tensions.

### Jackson's Presidency
- "Representative of the Common Man"; exercised strong executive power.
- Vetoed more bills than all previous presidents combined.

### Indian Removal Act (1830)
- Forced relocation of Native Americans west of the Mississippi.
- **Trail of Tears** — ~4,000 Cherokees died.
- **Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831):** Cherokees couldn't sue as a foreign nation.
- **Worcester v. Georgia (1832):** Georgia laws had no force in Cherokee territory — Jackson **refused to enforce** this ruling.

### Nullification Crisis
- South Carolina declared federal tariffs unconstitutional; threatened **nullification** (right of states to void federal laws).
- Jackson: "The Union must be preserved"; passed **Force Bill** asserting federal authority.
- Compromise tariff ended the crisis.

### Bank War
- Jackson saw Second Bank as unconstitutional and serving the wealthy.
- **Vetoed recharter (1832)** — denounced bank as a monopoly.
- Withdrew federal funds to state "**pet banks**."
- **Specie Circular** — required land purchases in gold/silver → contributed to **Panic of 1837**.

### Democrats vs. Whigs

| Party | Positions |
|---|---|
| **Democrats** (Jackson) | Opposed national bank, federal improvements; concerned about business monopolies |
| **Whigs** (Henry Clay) | Supported national bank, federal improvements; concerned about crime from immigrants |

## 4.9 — Development of an American Culture

### Cultural Nationalism
- Art: Gilbert Stuart, Charles Willson Peale, John Trumbull honored Revolutionary heroes.
- Literature: Washington Irving ("Rip Van Winkle," "Sleepy Hollow").
- Education: Noah Webster's speller promoted patriotism.

### Transcendentalism
- **Ralph Waldo Emerson:** American culture, self-reliance, independent thinking; anti-slavery.
- **Henry David Thoreau:** *Walden* (1854); civil disobedience (refused to pay taxes opposing Mexican-American War) → inspired Gandhi and MLK Jr.

### Communal Experiments

| Community | Details |
|---|---|
| **Shakers** | Celibacy; communal living; 6,000 members by 1840s; died out |
| **Brook Farm** | George Ripley; blended intellectual and manual labor; hosted Emerson, Hawthorne |
| **Oneida Community** | John Humphrey Noyes; shared property; complex marriage; silverware production |
| **New Harmony** | Robert Owen; secular utopian; failed due to financial/ideological conflicts |

### Hudson River School
- Thomas Cole and Frederick Church — Romantic depictions of American landscapes.

## 4.10 — The Second Great Awakening

### Causes of Religious Reform
- Democratic/individualistic emphasis; reaction against Enlightenment rationalism; fears of materialism; market revolution disruptions.

### Key Figures
- **Rev. Timothy Dwight** — Yale revivals; motivated evangelical preachers.
- **Charles Grandison Finney (1823)** — Upstate NY revivals; salvation through faith and hard work.
- **Baptists and Methodists** — circuit preachers; became largest Protestant denominations by 1850.

### New Denominations
- **Seventh-Day Adventists** — from William Miller's failed 1844 prophecy.
- **Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (1830)** — Joseph Smith; moved to Utah under Brigham Young.

### Social Reforms Sparked
- Temperance Movement
- Abolitionist Movement
- Mental health reform

---

## 4.11 — An Age of Reform

### Temperance
- Average American consumed **5 gallons of hard liquor annually** by 1820.
- **American Temperance Society (1826)** — total abstinence.
- Maine prohibited liquor manufacture/sale in 1851.
- Led to **18th Amendment (1919)** — Prohibition.

### Public Asylums
- **Dorothea Dix** — crusade for humane treatment of the mentally ill → state mental hospitals.
- **Thomas Gallaudet** — school for the deaf.
- **Dr. Samuel Gridley Howe** — school for the blind.
- Prison reform: Auburn system (NY) — strict discipline, moral instruction.

### Public Education
- **Horace Mann** — led the common school movement; compulsory attendance; longer school year; trained teachers.
- **McGuffey Readers** — taught reading and morality (hard work, punctuality, sobriety).

### Women's Rights
- **Seneca Falls Convention (1848)** — "Declaration of Sentiments": "all men and women are created equal."
- **Elizabeth Cady Stanton** and **Susan B. Anthony** led the campaign for voting, legal, and property rights.

### Anti-Slavery Movement

| Organization / Person | Role |
|---|---|
| American Colonization Society (1817) | Transported freed slaves to Liberia (Monrovia, 1822); impractical |
| William Lloyd Garrison | *The Liberator* (1831); American Antislavery Society (1833); immediate abolition |
| Liberty Party (1840) | Moderate abolitionists; political means |
| **Frederick Douglass** | Escaped slave; published *The North Star*; political and direct action |
| **Harriet Tubman** | Underground Railroad |
| **Nat Turner's Revolt (1831)** | 55 White deaths; brutal retaliation; ended antislavery sentiment in the South |

## 4.12 — African Americans in the Early Republic

### Free African Americans

**In the North:**
- Economic/political equality limited; displaced by immigrants; formed own congregations (e.g., African Methodist Episcopal Church).

**In the South:**
- Could own property but faced constant threat of kidnapping; proximity to family kept many from leaving.

### Slave Resistance
- Work slowdowns, sabotage.
- **Underground Railroad** — escape network; increased demands for stricter fugitive slave laws.
- **Slave Codes** — laws that stripped rights; became stricter after Nat Turner's Rebellion.

---

## 4.13 — The Society of the South in the Early Republic

### King Cotton
- By 1850s, cotton = **two-thirds of U.S. exports**.
- Westward expansion of planters into Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas.
- Enslaved population: 1 million (1800) → nearly 4 million (1860).

### Slavery
- Justified economically, then historically and religiously.
- High investment in slavery limited capital for industrialization.
- Strict slave codes; fear of revolts.

### Southern Culture
- Code of chivalry; personal honor; paternalism.
- Upper class valued education; lower classes had limited schooling; education for enslaved people prohibited.
- Methodists and Baptists grew due to pro-slavery stance.

### Social Reform in the South
- Limited impact; tradition over modernization.
- Anti-slavery seen as a northern threat.
