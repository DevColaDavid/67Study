# Period 8 — Cold War (1945–1980)

---

## 8.2 — The Cold War from 1945 to 1980

### Origins
- Intense U.S.-Soviet rivalry from late 1940s until Soviet collapse (1991).
- Historical mistrust since 1917 Bolshevik Revolution; deepened by Soviet-Nazi Non-Aggression Pact (1939).

### U.S.-Soviet Friction Points

| Issue | Details |
|---|---|
| Germany | Divided into Soviet, French, British, U.S. zones; Soviets wanted heavy reparations; U.S./Britain prioritized recovery |
| Atomic Energy | Soviets rejected U.S. Baruch Plan for international nuclear regulation → seen as aggression |
| **Iron Curtain** (March 1946) | Churchill (with Truman present): "iron curtain" descended across Europe; called for Western unity |

### Containment Policy (1947)
- Truman adopted **containment** — prevent Soviet expansion without provoking war.
- **Truman Doctrine:** $400M in economic/military aid to Greece and Turkey against communist threats.

### The Marshall Plan (1948)
- George Marshall proposed large-scale U.S. economic aid to rebuild Western Europe.
- Congress approved **$12 billion** for Western Europe over 4 years.
- Soviets and Eastern Europe **refused.**
- **Success:** Spurred Western European recovery; prevented spread of communism.
- **Effect:** Deepened East-West divide.

### Berlin Airlift (1948–49)
- Soviets blockaded West Berlin to force Allies out.
- Truman initiated **11-month airlift** of essential supplies.
- Soviets lifted blockade (May 1949); Germany formally divided: **West Germany** (U.S. ally) and **East Germany** (Soviet satellite).

### NATO (1949)
- First U.S. permanent military alliance with Western Europe and Canada.
- Soviets countered with **Warsaw Pact (1955)**.

### National Security Act (1947)
- Created: **Department of Defense, National Security Council (NSC), Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)**.
- Instituted peacetime draft (1948).

### Arms Race
- U.S. atomic monopoly ended (1949) when Soviets tested their own.
- **Hydrogen bomb** added to U.S. arsenal (1952).
- **NSC-68 (1950):** Called for significant increase in defense spending; global alliances against communism.

### Korean War (1950–1953)
- North Korea (Soviet-backed) invaded South Korea (June 25, 1950).
- Truman applied containment; UN authorized force; U.S. troops under Gen. **Douglas MacArthur**.
- **Inchon Landing** pushed North Koreans back → Chinese counterattacked → stalemate at 38th parallel.
- **MacArthur dismissed** for publicly advocating expansion of war into China.
- **Korean Armistice (July 1953):** Eisenhower + nuclear threats + Stalin's death → armistice; Korea remained divided.

### Cold War Flashpoints (Eisenhower Era)

| Event | Date | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Sputnik (1957)** | 1957 | Soviet first satellite → U.S. panic; Congress passed National Defense Education Act (NDEA); NASA created |
| **Second Berlin Crisis** | 1958–59 | Khrushchev demanded West withdraw from West Berlin; defused at Camp David |
| **U-2 Incident (1960)** | 1960 | U.S. spy plane shot down over USSR; killed Paris summit |

### Cold War (Kennedy Era)

| Event | Date | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Bay of Pigs (1961)** | 1961 | CIA-planned Cuban exile invasion failed; strengthened Castro-Soviet ties |
| **Berlin Wall (1961)** | 1961 | East Germany built wall; Kennedy: "Ich bin ein Berliner" (1963) |
| **Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)** | 1962 | U.S. discovered Soviet missiles in Cuba; naval blockade; Khrushchev removed missiles in exchange for U.S. pledge not to invade Cuba + removal of U.S. missiles from Turkey; **Nuclear Test Ban Treaty (1963)** |

### Nixon's Détente
- **Realist foreign policy** with Henry Kissinger — pragmatic; reduce Cold War tensions.
- **Visit to China (1972):** Nixon met Mao Zedong; initiated diplomatic relations; U.S. recognized Communist China (1979).
- **SALT I Agreement:** Froze number of ballistic missiles with nuclear warheads; limited ABMs.
- Nixon's resignation (1974) + fall of South Vietnam (1975) → loss of U.S. trust.

## 8.3 — The Red Scare

### Anti-Communist Measures

| Measure | Details |
|---|---|
| **Loyalty Review Board (1947–51)** | Investigated 3M+ federal employees; thousands dismissed |
| **Smith Act Prosecutions** | Leaders of American Communist Party jailed; upheld in *Dennis v. United States* (1951) |
| **McCarran Internal Security Act (1950)** | Made it illegal to support totalitarian governments; authorized detention camps |
| **Rosenberg Case** | Julius and Ethel Rosenberg executed (1953) for passing atomic secrets to Soviets |

### McCarthyism
- Senator Joseph McCarthy claimed Communist infiltration of government; used unproven accusations.
- **Army-McCarthy Hearings (1954):** Televised; exposed McCarthy's bullying tactics → Senate censured him.
- Economic boom and Korean armistice shifted public attention away from communist fears.

## 8.4 — Economy after 1945

### Postwar Economy Under Truman
- **Employment Act of 1946:** Created Council of Economic Advisers; less ambitious than Truman hoped.
- **GI Bill (1944):** 15 million veterans received education funds, home/farm/business loans → boosted economy; Black veterans faced discrimination.

### Baby Boom
- 50 million babies born 1945–1960 → lasting economic/social effects.
- One-third of married women employed by 1960.

### Suburban Growth
- **Levittown** — mass-produced suburban development; White families only; federal policies supported segregation in housing.
- Cities became increasingly poor and racially divided by 1960s.

### Taft-Hartley Act (1947)
- Probusiness law passed over Truman's veto:
  - Outlawed closed shop (union membership before hiring).
  - Allowed "right to work" laws.
  - Banned secondary boycotts.
  - President could impose 80-day cooling-off period before strikes.

### Election of 1948
- Truman defeated Dewey; proposed **Fair Deal** (national health insurance, civil rights) — mostly blocked by Congress.

### Eisenhower's Presidency (1953–1961)
- **Interstate Highway System (1956 Highway Act):** 42,000 miles; boosted economy, suburban growth.
- Steady economic growth; low inflation; average family had double the real income of 1920s.

### Kennedy's New Frontier
- Domestic agenda: education, health care, urban renewal, civil rights.
- Many programs struggled in Congress → passed under LBJ.

### Nixon's Domestic Policy
- **New Federalism:** Shifted responsibilities to state and local governments; $30B in block grants.
- **Stagflation:** 90-day wage/price freeze; took dollar off gold standard; import surtax.

## 8.5 — Culture after 1945

### Consumer Culture
- **Television:** By early 1960s, one set per 3.3 Americans; reinforced conservative suburban values (*Leave It to Beaver*, *Father Knows Best*).
- **Advertising** and suburban shopping centers; credit cards; chain restaurants.

### Women's Roles
- Baby boom and suburban living made homemaking a full-time job.
- Despite increasing workforce participation, lower wages and traditional expectations persisted.

### Beatniks
- **Beat Generation (Jack Kerouac, Allen Ginsberg):** Spontaneity, rebellion against norms; laid groundwork for 1960s youth rebellion.

### Kennedy's Assassination
- **November 22, 1963, Dallas, Texas:** Kennedy shot; Lee Harvey Oswald killed on live TV → tragic end to postwar optimism.

## 8.6 — Early Steps in the Civil Rights Movement (1945–1960)

### Presidential Leadership
- **Harry Truman (1948):** Executive Order 9981 — desegregated U.S. military.
- Cold War context: racial segregation was hypocritical given U.S. promotion of freedom vs. communism.

### Desegregating Schools
- **NAACP** worked through courts to overturn *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896).
- **Brown v. Board of Education (1954):** NAACP lawyer **Thurgood Marshall** argued before Supreme Court; Chief Justice **Earl Warren's** unanimous decision: "separate facilities are inherently unequal."

### Resistance in the South
- 101 members of Congress signed "Southern Manifesto" condemning the ruling.
- **1956 Little Rock:** Governor Orval Faubus blocked 9 Black students; Eisenhower sent federal troops.
- By 1964: only **2% of Black students** in South attended integrated schools.

### Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56)
- **Rosa Parks** arrested for refusing to give up bus seat.
- Led by **Martin Luther King Jr.** → Supreme Court ruled segregation on buses unconstitutional.

### Nonviolent Protests
- **SCLC (1957):** MLK Jr. mobilized churches in civil rights struggle.
- **Sit-in Movement (1960, Greensboro, NC):** Protested segregated lunch counters → formed **SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee)**.

### Federal Laws
- Eisenhower signed Civil Rights Acts of **1957 and 1960** — established Civil Rights Commission; protected Black voting rights.

## 8.7 — America as a World Power

### Middle East

| Event | Date | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Suez Crisis** | 1956 | Nasser nationalized Suez Canal; Britain/France/Israel attacked; Eisenhower condemned → decline of British/French influence; U.S. prominence rose |
| **Eisenhower Doctrine** | 1957 | U.S. aid to any Middle Eastern country threatened by communism |
| **OPEC formed** | 1960 | Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Iraq, Iran, Venezuela; coordinate oil policies |
| **Yom Kippur War** | 1973 | Syria/Egypt attacked Israel; U.S. supported Israel; Arab OPEC oil embargo → global oil crisis |
| **Camp David Accords** | 1978 | Carter brokered Egypt-Israel peace; Egypt first Arab nation to recognize Israel |
| **Iran Hostage Crisis** | 1979 | Khomeini overthrew Shah; 50+ Americans held 444 days; failed rescue mission damaged Carter's presidency |

### Latin America (Johnson)
- Prioritized anti-communism over democracy.
- Troops sent to Dominican Republic (1965) to prevent communist takeover.
- Resembled Roosevelt's "Big Stick" approach.

### Panama Canal
- Carter renegotiated 1903 treaty; Senate ratified (1978) — transfer of Canal to Panama by 2000.

### Human Rights Diplomacy (Carter)
- Promoted human rights globally; appointed Andrew Young as UN ambassador.
- Cut aid to Argentina and Chile over human rights violations.

## 8.8 — The Vietnam War

### Background
- After WWII, Vietnamese (Ho Chi Minh) resisted French recolonization.
- U.S. supported French; China and USSR supported Viet Minh.
- **French defeat at Dien Bien Phu (1954):** Geneva Accords divided Vietnam at 17th parallel.
- **Domino Theory (Eisenhower):** If one country falls to communism, neighbors follow.

### Escalation Under LBJ

| Event | Date | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Tonkin Gulf Resolution (1964)** | 1964 | Alleged attack on U.S. ships → "blank check" for Johnson to take "all necessary measures"; never a formal declaration of war |
| **Operation Rolling Thunder (1965)** | 1965 | Prolonged bombing of North Vietnam; 184,000 troops by end of 1965; 485,000 by 1967 |
| **Credibility Gap** | — | Misinformation from military leaders; public lost trust in government |
| **Tet Offensive (Jan 1968)** | 1968 | Surprise Vietcong attack on multiple cities; military failure for N. Vietnam but political victory; demoralized American public |
| **Johnson Ends Escalation** | March 31, 1968 | Limited bombing; sought peace talks; declined reelection |

### Nixon's Vietnam Policy
- **Vietnamization:** Gradual withdrawal of U.S. troops; transfer combat to South Vietnamese.
- U.S. troops: 540,000 (1969) → under 30,000 (1972).
- Invaded Cambodia → protests; **Kent State and Jackson State** deaths.
- Senate repealed Gulf of Tonkin Resolution.
- **My Lai massacre** and **Pentagon Papers** (exposed government deceptions) revealed to public.
- **Paris Accords (1973):** U.S. troop withdrawal; POW return; war continued between North and South Vietnam.
- **War Powers Act (1973):** President must report to Congress within 48 hours of military action; Congressional approval needed for actions >60 days.

## 8.9 — The Great Society

### LBJ's War on Poverty
- Inspired by Michael Harrington's *The Other America* (1962) — 40 million Americans in poverty.
- LBJ declared "unconditional war on poverty" (1964).

### Election of 1964
- LBJ won by landslide (61% popular vote); Goldwater (R) wanted to end the welfare state.
- Democrats controlled 2/3 of Congress → enabled sweeping reforms.

### Great Society Programs

| Program | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Food Stamp Act | 1964 | Federal assistance for low-income food purchases |
| Medicare | 1965 | Health insurance for those 65+ |
| Medicaid | 1965 | Medical care for poor and disabled |
| Elementary and Secondary Education Act | 1965 | Funded poor school districts; special education |
| Higher Education Act | 1965 | Federal scholarships for postsecondary education |
| **Immigration Act** | 1965 | Abolished discriminatory national-origin quotas → opened U.S. to global immigration |
| Child Nutrition Act | 1966 | Added breakfast to school lunch program |

### Immigration Changes Post-1965
- By 1980s: 47% of immigrants from Latin America, 37% from Asia, fewer than 13% from Europe.

## 8.10 — The African American Civil Rights Movement (1960s)

### Leadership of MLK Jr.
- Civil rights activists (freedom riders) faced violence while registering voters and integrating public spaces.
- **1963 Birmingham:** MLK jailed during illegal march → **"Letter from Birmingham Jail"** — nonviolent protest upholds democratic values.
- Inspired Kennedy to support a tougher civil rights bill.

### March on Washington (1963)
- ~200,000 participants; MLK delivered **"I Have a Dream"** speech.

### Federal Civil Rights Legislation

| Law | Year | Key Provision |
|---|---|---|
| **Civil Rights Act of 1964** | 1964 | Segregation illegal in all public facilities; created Equal Employment Opportunity Commission |
| **24th Amendment** | 1964 | Abolished poll tax |
| **Voting Rights Act of 1965** | 1965 | Ended literacy tests; federal registrars in areas of discrimination |

### "Bloody Sunday" (March 1965)
- Voting rights march from Selma to Montgomery met with violent resistance.
- Televised violence → national outrage → passage of Voting Rights Act.

### Malcolm X and Black Power
- **Elijah Muhammad (Black Muslims):** Black nationalism; separatism.
- **Malcolm X:** Advocated self-defense; criticized MLK; later moderated; assassinated 1965.
- **Stokely Carmichael (SNCC):** Rejected nonviolence; promoted "black power."
- **Black Panthers (1966):** Huey Newton, Bobby Seale; revolutionary socialist movement.

### Urban Riots
- **Watts riots (1965):** 34 deaths; 700+ buildings destroyed.
- Race riots continued through 1968.
- **Kerner Commission:** Blamed racism and segregation; warned U.S. becoming "two societies, one black, one white."

## 8.11 — The Civil Rights Movement Expands

### The Women's Movement
- Factors: increased education and employment; civil rights movement; sexual revolution.
- **Betty Friedan:** *The Feminine Mystique* (1963) → co-founded **NOW (National Organization for Women, 1966)**.
- **Equal Pay Act (1963)** and **Civil Rights Act (1964)** — prohibited sex-based discrimination.
- **ERA (Equal Rights Amendment, 1972):** Passed Congress; failed to be ratified by 38 states.
- Achievements: more women in business, law, medicine, politics; concept of "glass ceiling" persisted.

### The Warren Court and Individual Rights

*Chief Justice Earl Warren (1953–1969) — emphasized individual rights over property rights.*

| Case | Year | Ruling |
|---|---|---|
| **Brown v. Board** | 1954 | Ended school segregation |
| **Mapp v. Ohio** | 1961 | Illegally seized evidence inadmissible |
| **Gideon v. Wainwright** | 1963 | State courts must provide attorneys for those who can't afford one |
| **Escobedo v. Illinois** | 1964 | Right to attorney during police questioning |
| **Miranda v. Arizona** | 1966 | Police must inform arrested individuals of their rights ("Miranda rights") |

### Gay Rights Movement
- **Stonewall Inn Riot (1969):** Police raid sparked riots → birth of modern gay rights movement.
- By mid-1970s: homosexuality no longer classified as mental illness.

## 8.12 — Youth Culture of the 1960s

### Baby Boom Generation
- College enrollment quadrupled (1945–1970).
- Not shaped by Depression or WWII; influenced by civil rights and demands for equality.

### New Left / SDS
- **Students for a Democratic Society (SDS, 1962):** Port Huron Statement — participatory democracy.
- **Free Speech Movement (Berkeley, 1964):** First major student protest; demanded political freedom on campus.

### Anti-Vietnam Protests
- Draft-card burning; sit-ins; opposition to ROTC; disrupted campuses.
- **Assassinations of MLK Jr. and RFK (1968)** intensified protests.
- **Chicago Convention (1968):** Democratic National Convention protests → "police riot."
- **Weather Underground:** Most radical SDS faction; bombings of Capitol, Pentagon, State Dept.

### Counterculture
- "Hippies" — rejected mainstream values; communal living; drug use.
- Folk music (Baez, Dylan); rock (Beatles, Rolling Stones, Joplin, Morrison).

### Sexual Revolution
- Birth control pill (1960); antibiotics for STDs.
- Premarital sex, contraception, abortion, homosexuality became more visible and accepted.
- 1980s backlash: illegitimate births, AIDS crisis.

## 8.13 — The Environment and Natural Resources (1968–1980)

### Environmental Movement
- **Earth Day (1970):** 100,000 in NYC; millions nationwide.
- Apollo photos of Earth from space ("Earthrise") raised awareness.
- Sierra Club membership: 123,000 (1960) → 819,000 (1970).

### Key Legislation

| Law | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **EPA (Environmental Protection Agency)** | 1970 | Enforce pollution, radiation, pesticide, waste programs |
| **Clean Air Act** | 1970 | Regulated air emissions; EPA set standards |
| **Endangered Species Act** | 1973 | Protected critically endangered species |

### Oil Crisis and Fuel Economy
- 1973 OPEC embargo → speed limits reduced; fuel economy standards enacted.

### Antinuclear Movement
- Peaked in 1970s–1980s; Three Mile Island accident (PA) increased concerns; slowed nuclear plant construction.

## 8.14 — Society in Transition

### American Society
- By 1970s: half of Americans in South and West; population aging; minorities = 25% by 1990.
- Cultural pluralism replacing the melting pot model.

### Nixon's Southern Strategy
- Appealed to "silent majority" — conservative Americans disillusioned by civil rights and counterculture.
- United southern Whites, northern blue-collar workers, suburbanites into a Republican majority.

### Watergate Scandal
- Break-in at Democratic headquarters by Nixon's reelection committee.
- Broader illegal activities: wiretaps, "plumbers" unit, enemies list.
- Nixon involved in cover-up → Senate investigation, televised hearings.
- **Oval Office tapes** → Nixon resigned under threat of impeachment.
- **Gerald Ford** became president; pardoned Nixon → lost public goodwill.

### Burger Court
- Nixon appointed **Warren Burger** as Chief Justice; more conservative (but not as conservative as hoped).
- **Roe v. Wade (1973):** Struck down state laws prohibiting abortion — woman's right to privacy.
- Ordered Nixon to turn over tapes (*United States v. Nixon*, 1974).

### Election of 1976
- Jimmy Carter (outsider; former Georgia governor) won due to post-Watergate distrust; 97% of Black vote.

### Carter's Presidency
- Ended "imperial presidency" with informal style.
- Iranian hostage crisis and economic problems → 23% approval rating by 1980.
