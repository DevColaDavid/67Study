# Period 5 — Civil War and Reconstruction (1844–1877)

---

## 5.2 — Manifest Destiny

- Coined by journalist **John L. O'Sullivan** in 1845 — divine mission to spread American liberty and democracy from Atlantic to Pacific.
- **Opposition:** Many Northerners feared it was a scheme to expand slavery into new territories.

### Texas Annexation
- Mexico invited American settlers into Texas (1821); by 1830, Americans outnumbered Mexicans 3:1.
- Mexico outlawed slavery → tensions rose.
- **1836:** American settlers (Sam Houston) declared independence → **Republic of Texas**.

### Oregon Territory
- Claimed by Spain, Russia, Britain, and U.S.; settled by thousands of Americans in the mid-1840s.
- **Election of 1844:** James K. Polk won on Manifest Destiny platform → settled boundary at **49th parallel**.

### Gold Rush
- 1848 discovery of gold in California → rapid population growth, boomtowns.
- California did **not** adopt slavery → unique social landscape hinting at national tensions.

### Alaska Purchase (1867)
- Secretary of State **William Seward** purchased Alaska from Russia for $7.2 million.

---

## 5.3 — The Mexican-American War (1846–1848)

### Causes
- U.S. annexation of Texas → border dispute (Mexico: Nueces River; U.S.: Rio Grande).
- Polk wanted California and New Mexico.
- Mexican army attacked U.S. patrol near Rio Grande → pretext for war.

### Wilmot Proviso
- Proposed to prohibit slavery in any territory acquired from Mexico.
- Passed the House; **blocked in the Senate** → revealed deep sectional divisions.

### Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848)
- Mexico ceded vast territories (California, New Mexico) for $15 million.
- **Reignited the slavery debate** — where would slavery be allowed in new territories?

---

## 5.4 — The Compromise of 1850

### Background
- Southerners resented Missouri Compromise restrictions.
- California (Gold Rush) sought admission as a **free state** — south of the 36°30' line.
- Southern "fire-eaters" threatened secession.

### Three Main Positions on Slavery

| Position | Description | Supporters |
|---|---|---|
| **Free-Soil** | No slavery in new territories | Northern Democrats & Whigs; Free-Soil Party (1848) |
| **Southern** | Slavery should extend everywhere; willing to extend Missouri Compromise line to Pacific | Plantation owners |
| **Popular Sovereignty** | Let territory residents decide by vote | Senator Lewis Cass (moderate) |

### Henry Clay's Compromise of 1850
1. Admit **California** as a free state.
2. **Utah and New Mexico** territories organized with popular sovereignty.
3. Resolve Texas-New Mexico boundary; federal debt assumption.
4. Ban **slave trade** (not slavery) in D.C.
5. New, stringent **Fugitive Slave Law**.

> Passed piece by piece by **Stephen A. Douglas**; signed by **Millard Fillmore** after Taylor's death.
> **Short-term relief** only — Fugitive Slave Law and popular sovereignty increased sectional strife.

---

## 5.5 — Sectional Conflict: Regional Differences

### Immigration
- **Irish (~2 million):** Fled famine in 1840s; settled in Northern cities; faced anti-Catholic discrimination.
- **Germans (>1 million):** Fled 1848 revolutions; prospered in Midwest; supported public education and anti-slavery.
- **Nativism:** Know-Nothing Party (American Party) — proposed 21-year naturalization period.

### Fugitive Slave Law (1850)
- Federal involvement in retrieval of runaway slaves; denied jury trials; compelled local officials to participate.
- Deepened North-South divide; galvanized the abolitionist cause.

### Underground Railroad
- Network of Black and White abolitionists; **Harriet Tubman** led hundreds to freedom.

### Influential Literature

| Work | Author | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| *Uncle Tom's Cabin* (1852) | Harriet Beecher Stowe | Shaped Northern attitudes; bolstered abolition; international sympathy |
| *Impending Crisis of the South* (1857) | Hinton R. Helper | Critiqued slavery's economic harm; banned in South |

---

## 5.6 — Failure of Compromise

### Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854)
- Senator **Stephen A. Douglas** divided Nebraska Territory into Kansas and Nebraska.
- Slavery decided by **popular sovereignty** → effectively nullified the Missouri Compromise.

### "Bleeding Kansas"
- Violent clashes between pro- and anti-slavery settlers.
- Incidents: Sack of Lawrence, **Pottawatomie Creek Massacre** (John Brown).

### Caning of Senator Sumner (1856)
- Abolitionist Senator Charles Sumner beaten by Rep. Preston Brooks → North was horrified; South celebrated.

### Birth of the Republican Party (1854)
- Anti-slavery ex-Whigs, Democrats, and Free-Soilers; formed in Wisconsin.
- Goal: stop the *spread* of slavery.

### Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)
- Supreme Court: African Americans were **not U.S. citizens**; Congress had no authority to prohibit slavery in territories → Missouri Compromise unconstitutional.
- Outraged the North; celebrated in the South.

### Lincoln-Douglas Debates (1858)
- Abraham Lincoln (Republican) vs. Stephen Douglas (Democrat) for Illinois Senate seat.
- Lincoln argued against moral expansion of slavery; Douglas supported popular sovereignty.
- Boosted Lincoln's national profile → 1860 presidential candidacy.

---

## 5.7 — Election of 1860 and Secession

### John Brown's Raid at Harpers Ferry (1859)
- Brown attempted to seize federal arsenal to start a slave uprising.
- Captured by **Robert E. Lee**; tried, convicted, hanged.
- South: proof of Northern aggression. North: Brown as martyr.

### Election of 1860
- Democratic Party **split** — Northern Democrats (Douglas) vs. Southern Democrats (Breckinridge).
- Constitutional Union Party nominated John Bell.
- **Abraham Lincoln** won without a single Southern state — less than 40% of popular vote but majority of Electoral College.

### Secession
- South Carolina seceded (Dec 1860); six more Deep South states followed → **Confederate States of America**.
- **Crittenden Compromise** (constitutionally protect slavery at 36°30') — rejected by Republicans.
- **Fort Sumter (April 1861):** Confederate attack → four more Upper South states seceded.
- **Border States** (Maryland, Missouri, Kentucky) kept in Union through political maneuvering and martial law.

## 5.8 — Military Conflict in the Civil War

### Union vs. Confederate Advantages

| Category | Union | Confederacy |
|---|---|---|
| Population | 22 million | 5.5 million |
| Economy | Banks, factories, railroads, farmland | Cotton exports; hoped for European support |
| Military | Large navy; larger manpower | Defensive war; experienced leaders; shorter supply lines |
| Political | Established central government | States' rights weakened Confederate central authority |

### Union Strategy (Anaconda Plan)
1. **Naval blockade** of Southern ports.
2. Control the **Mississippi River** — split the Confederacy.
3. Capture **Richmond** (Confederate capital).

### Key Battles

| Battle | Date | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| First Bull Run | July 1861 | Union unprepared; no short war |
| **Antietam** | Sept 1862 | Union halted Lee's advance into MD; Lincoln issued Emancipation Proclamation |
| **Battle of Fredericksburg** | Dec 1862 | Devastating Union defeat under Burnside |
| Monitor vs. Merrimac | March 1862 | First ironclad battle; wooden warships obsolete |
| **Vicksburg** | July 4, 1863 | Grant gained control of Mississippi River; split Confederacy |
| **Gettysburg** | July 1–3, 1863 | Largest battle of war; Pickett's Charge failed; last major Confederate offensive in North |
| **Sherman's March to the Sea** | Late 1864 | Total war tactics; scorched earth from Atlanta to Savannah |
| **Appomattox Court House** | April 9, 1865 | Lee surrendered to Grant; generous terms |

### Diplomatic Dimensions
- **Trent Affair (1861):** Capture of Confederate diplomats from British ship nearly brought Britain into the war.
- **Cotton Diplomacy Failed** — Britain sourced cotton from Egypt and India; Emancipation Proclamation prevented European support for Confederacy.

---

## 5.9 — Government Policies During the Civil War

### Emancipation Proclamation (Jan 1, 1863)
- Declared freedom for enslaved people in **Confederate-held territories** (exempt: border states and Union-controlled areas).
- **Shifted war aim** from preserving Union to abolishing slavery.
- **Prevented foreign intervention** — supporting Confederacy would mean endorsing slavery.
- Nearly **200,000 African Americans** served in the Union Army and Navy (e.g., Massachusetts 54th Regiment).

### Civil Liberties
- **Suspension of Habeas Corpus** — Lincoln arrested suspected Confederate sympathizers without trial.
- **Ex Parte Milligan** (post-war): Civilians cannot be subjected to military tribunals when civilian courts are available.

### The Draft (1863 Conscription Act)
- Men could pay a fee or find a substitute → **New York Draft Riots** (anti-Black violence).

### Key Legislation

| Law | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Morrill Tariff Act | 1861 | Protect domestic industries; generate revenue |
| Homestead Act | 1862 | 160 free acres to settlers who farmed for 5 years |
| Morrill Land Grant Act | 1862 | Federal land for agricultural/technical colleges |
| Pacific Railway Act | 1862 | Authorized transcontinental railroad |

### Assassination of Lincoln
- John Wilkes Booth (Confederate sympathizer) shot Lincoln on **April 14, 1865**.

## 5.10 — Reconstruction

### Competing Plans

| Plan | Who | Key Terms |
|---|---|---|
| **Lincoln's 10% Plan** | Lincoln | 10% of voters swear loyalty + accept emancipation → form new state government |
| **Wade-Davis Bill (1864)** | Radical Republicans in Congress | 50% of voters + only non-Confederates vote for new constitutions → Lincoln vetoed |

### Freedmen's Bureau (1865)
- Provided aid to Black and White Southerners; founded thousands of schools.
- Land redistribution largely undone by Johnson's pardons.

### Andrew Johnson
- Succeeded Lincoln; originally welcomed by Republicans.
- Issued personal pardons restoring former Confederates to power.
- **Vetoed** Freedmen's Bureau expansion and Civil Rights bills → alienated Republicans.
- Vetoed legislation **29 times**; Congress overrode many.

### Congressional Reconstruction (1866–)
- Radical Republicans took control; passed **Reconstruction Acts of 1867** — South divided into 5 military districts; states required to ratify 14th Amendment and guarantee adult male voting rights.

### Constitutional Amendments

| Amendment | Year | Key Provision |
|---|---|---|
| **13th** | 1865 | Abolished slavery throughout the U.S. |
| **14th** | 1868 | Citizenship to all born in U.S.; equal protection; due process; foundation of later civil rights law |
| **15th** | 1870 | Cannot deny vote based on race, color, or previous servitude |

### Impeachment of Andrew Johnson (1867–1868)
- Violated **Tenure of Office Act** by dismissing Secretary of War Edwin Stanton.
- House impeached; **acquitted by one vote** in the Senate.

### Civil Rights Act of 1875
- Equal accommodations in public places; no exclusion from juries.
- Poorly enforced; Congress abandoned Reconstruction by 1877.

### African Americans in Reconstruction
- Reunited families, established Black churches and schools.
- Black colleges founded: Howard, Atlanta, Fisk, Morehouse.

---

## 5.11 — Failure of Reconstruction

### White Supremacy & the KKK
- **Ku Klux Klan** (founded 1867 by Nathan Bedford Forrest) — violence against Black voters and White reformers.
- **Force Acts (1870–1871)** — Congress tried to stop Klan violence.

### Black Codes
- Laws restricting African Americans' rights and movements; forced labor contracts resembling slavery.

### Sharecropping
- Landlords provided seed/supplies for a share of the harvest → cycle of debt and dependency.

### Compromise of 1877
- Disputed 1876 election (Hayes vs. Tilden) resolved: **Rutherford B. Hayes** (R) became president in exchange for:
  - Withdrawal of federal troops from the South.
  - Support for a Southern transcontinental railroad.
- **Marked the end of Reconstruction** → rollback of Black rights; rise of Jim Crow.
