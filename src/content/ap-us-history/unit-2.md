# Period 2 — Colonial America (1607–1754)

---

## 2.2 — European Colonization

### General Motivations
- **Wealth** — agriculture, mining, fur trade.
- **Religion** — spread of Christianity.
- **Escape from Persecution** — religious and political refugees.

### Colonies by Power

| Power | Key Colonies / Notes |
|---|---|
| **Spanish** | Florida (St. Augustine, 1565 — *oldest European city in mainland US*), New Mexico (Santa Fe, 1610), California (missions, 1769) |
| **French** | Quebec (1608, Champlain), New Orleans (1718); focused on fur trade; relied on Native American alliances |
| **Dutch** | New Amsterdam (later NYC); Dutch West India Company; trade-focused |
| **British** | Diverse settlers; higher proportion of families; claimed Native lands → more conflict |

---

## 2.3 — The Regions of British Colonies

### Charter Types
- **Corporate colonies** (e.g., Jamestown) — managed by joint-stock companies.
- **Royal colonies** (e.g., Virginia post-1624) — ruled directly by the Crown.
- **Proprietary colonies** (e.g., Maryland, Pennsylvania) — granted to individuals.

### New England Colonies

| Colony | Founded | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Plymouth** | 1620 | Pilgrims (Separatists); Mayflower Compact; fishing/fur/lumber |
| **Massachusetts Bay** | 1630 | Puritans; John Winthrop; "Great Migration" (~15,000 settlers); self-governance |
| **Rhode Island** | 1636 | Roger Williams & Anne Hutchinson (expelled from MA); religious tolerance; church/state separation |
| **Connecticut** | 1636 | Thomas Hooker; *Fundamental Orders* — one of the first written constitutions |

### Middle Colonies

| Colony | Founded | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **New York** | 1664 | Originally Dutch New Amsterdam; diverse population; major trade hub |
| **New Jersey** | 1664 | Split from NY; unified as royal colony 1702; religious freedom |
| **Pennsylvania** | — | William Penn; **"The Holy Experiment"**; Quaker ideals; *Frame of Government* (1682–83); *Charter of Liberties* (1701); Philadelphia planned on grid |
| **Delaware** | 1704 | Separated from Pennsylvania |

### Southern Colonies

| Colony | Founded | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Maryland** | 1634 | Lord Baltimore (Catholics); *Act of Toleration* — religious freedom to all Christians |
| **Virginia** | 1607 | First permanent English colony (Jamestown); tobacco (John Rolfe); **headright system**; Virginia Company → royal colony |
| **The Carolinas** | 1663 | Split into North (small farms) & South (plantations) in 1729 |
| **Georgia** | 1732 | James Oglethorpe; buffer colony vs. Spanish Florida; social experiment for debtors; initially banned slavery; → royal colony 1752 |

### Early Political Institutions

- **Virginia House of Burgesses (1619)** — First representative assembly in America.
- **Mayflower Compact (1620)** — Early form of self-government via majority rule.
- **Town Meetings** — Local governance in New England; voted for governor and assembly.
- **Limits:** Women, landless men, indentured servants, enslaved people excluded from politics.

## 2.4 — Transatlantic Trade

### Triangular Trade
- **New England → West Africa:** Rum
- **West Africa → West Indies:** Enslaved Africans
- **West Indies → New England:** Sugarcane → more rum
- African elites played a key role in supplying captives.

### Mercantilism & Navigation Acts
- **Mercantilism:** Promote exports over imports; colonies supply raw materials, serve as markets.
- **Navigation Acts:** Required English ships; goods routed through English ports. Boosted shipbuilding but restricted colonial manufacturing and trading partners.

### Effects
- Constrained colonial economies; limited trading partners and prices.
- **Smuggling** and economic disobedience → strained relations with England.

### Dominion of New England (1686–1688)
- Created by King James II; single governor over several colonies.
- Governor Sir Edmund Andros: taxed without consent, restricted town meetings.
- Dissolved after the **Glorious Revolution (1688)**.

### Salutary Neglect
- British policy of loosely enforcing parliamentary laws → colonies grew accustomed to autonomy.
- When Britain later tightened control, colonial resentment ignited → **American Revolution**.

---

## 2.5 — Interactions Between American Indians and Europeans

### New England Confederation (1643–1684)
- Formed by Plymouth, Massachusetts Bay, Connecticut, New Haven — mutual protection against Native tribes, Dutch, and French.

### Metacom's War (King Philip's War, 1675–1676)
- Wampanoag chief **Metacom** ("King Philip") resisted English encroachments.
- Devastating on both sides → effectively ended major Native resistance in New England.

### Bacon's Rebellion (1676) — Virginia
- Governor William Berkeley accused of ignoring frontier settlers' safety.
- **Nathaniel Bacon** led rebellion against Native villages and Berkeley.
- **Long-term Impact:**
  - Showed class disparity and resistance to royal authority.
  - Revealed instability of indentured servitude → **planters shifted toward slavery**.

### Pueblo Revolt (1680) — Spanish Southwest
- Unified Pueblo uprising expelled Spanish for over a decade.
- Post-revolt: Spanish adopted less harsh governance.

---

## 2.6 — Slavery in the British Colonies

### Shift from Indentured Servitude to Slavery
- Initially, Virginia relied on **indentured servants** (headright system: 50 acres for passage).
- Bacon's Rebellion showed instability of indentured servitude.
- By late 17th century, planters favored the permanent, hereditary labor of slavery.

### Institutionalization of Slavery
- 1619: Africans arrived in Virginia as indentured servants.
- By **1660s**: Laws defined African slaves and their offspring as **property for life**.
- Slavery became a **hereditary condition** passed down maternally.
- Christian baptism no longer granted protective status.

### Economic Impact
- Triangular trade and cash crops (tobacco, rice, indigo) dependent on enslaved labor.

### Slave Resistance
- Work slowdowns, sabotage, escape, outright rebellion.
- **Cultural retention**: music, dance, storytelling, religious practices.

## 2.7 — Colonial Society and Culture

### Economy by Region

| Region | Economic Activity |
|---|---|
| **New England** | Subsistence farming, logging, shipbuilding, fishing, rum-distilling |
| **Middle Colonies** | Fertile lands (wheat, corn), iron industry, cities (Philadelphia, NYC) |
| **Southern Colonies** | Plantations (tobacco, timber, rice, indigo); some subsistence farming |

### Religious and Cultural Dynamics
- **Religious Diversity:** Strict Puritan norms in New England; inclusive in Rhode Island & Pennsylvania.
- **Social Structure:** No hereditary aristocracy; more social mobility than Europe; growing middle class.

### The Great Awakening
- Religious revival emphasizing personal salvation and emotional faith.
- **Leaders:** Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield.
- **Impact:** Fragmented denominations; rise of religious self-determination → mirrored growing political independence.

### Education

| Region | Approach |
|---|---|
| New England | Tax-supported schools (1647 MA law); Puritan literacy emphasis |
| Middle | Church-sponsored & private schools |
| South | Home tutors; no formal system |

**Higher Education:**
- Harvard (1636) — Puritans; Yale (1701) — Congregationalists; William & Mary (1694) — Anglicans.
- **College of Philadelphia (1765)** — Benjamin Franklin; first major *non-sectarian* college.

### The Press & Zenger Case (1735)
- John Peter Zenger acquitted of libel — established that **truth is a defense against libel**.
- Precedent for freedom of the press; encouraged scrutiny of government.

### The Enlightenment
- **John Locke:** Governance based on inherent human rights; sovereignty resides with the people.
- Fueled revolutionary ideas → U.S. Constitution.
