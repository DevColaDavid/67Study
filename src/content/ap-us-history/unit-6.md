# Period 6 — The Gilded Age (1865–1898)

---

## 6.2 — Westward Expansion: Economic Development

### Transcontinental Railroads
- First completed **1869** (Omaha, NE → Sacramento, CA); employed Irish and Chinese immigrants.
- By 1883, additional lines connected the country.
- **Cultural impact:** Near-extermination of buffalo; displacement of Native Americans.

### Mining Frontier
- California Gold Rush (1849) sparked silver/gold rushes across the West.
- Boomtowns → often ghost towns after resources depleted (exceptions: San Francisco, Denver).

### Cattle Frontier
- Vast grasslands from Texas to Canada; influenced by Mexican vaquero traditions.
- Railroads enabled cattle drives → Dodge City, Abilene as trade hubs.
- Declined in 1880s: overgrazing, harsh weather, homesteaders fencing off range with **barbed wire** (Joseph Glidden, 1874).

### Homestead Act (1862)
- 160 acres free to any family settling for 5 years.
- Best land often taken by railroad companies; ~2/3 of homestead farms failed by 1900.

### Problems and Solutions

| Challenge | Solution |
|---|---|
| No wood for homes | Sod bricks |
| No fencing | Barbed wire |
| No water | Windmills for wells |
| Poor yields | "Dry farming," Russian wheat strains, federal irrigation |

### Falling Farm Prices
- Increased global production → deflation; farmers grew more to pay debts (vicious cycle).

### Farmers Organize

**National Grange (founded 1868, Oliver H. Kelley)**
- Started social/educational → moved to economic/political action.
- Cooperatives; lobbied for railroad regulation.
- Victory: *Munn v. Illinois* (1877) — upheld state regulation of public businesses.

**Farmers' Alliances**
- 1 million members by 1890; included Black and White farmers.
- **Ocala Platform (1890):** Direct election of senators, graduated income tax, lower tariffs, federal banking reform.
- Ideas influenced the **Populist movement**.

## 6.3 — Westward Expansion: Social and Cultural Development

### Turner's Frontier Thesis (1893)
- Frederick Jackson Turner: The frontier shaped American democracy, individualism, and innovation.
- Closing of the frontier raised fears of lost "safety valve" for social discontent.

### American Indians in the West

| Policy / Event | Details |
|---|---|
| **Reservation Policy** | Tribes assigned to defined reservations from 1850s |
| **Indian Wars** | Sioux War; **Battle of Little Big Horn (1876)** — Custer's defeat |
| **Ghost Dance / Wounded Knee (1890)** | Over 200 Sioux killed; end of Indian Wars |
| **Assimilation** | Carlisle School in Pennsylvania; education, job training, Christianity |
| **Dawes Severalty Act (1887)** | Divided tribal lands into individual plots; citizenship for those who adopted Western lifestyles; massive land loss for tribes |

### Conservation Movement
- **Forest Reserve Act (1891)** and **Forest Management Act (1897)** — protected timberlands.
- **John Muir** and the **Sierra Club (1892)** — preserve natural areas from human interference.
- Yosemite and Yellowstone preserved.

## 6.4 — The "New South"

### Industrial Growth
- Rise of Birmingham (steel), Memphis (lumber), Richmond (tobacco).
- Profits largely flowed north; limited investment in education limited full industrialization.

### Agriculture and Poverty
- Most Black farmers: sharecroppers or tenant farmers → cycle of debt.
- Cotton overproduction → price decline.
- **George Washington Carver** at Tuskegee Institute promoted peanuts and sweet potatoes as alternatives.

### Segregation
- **Civil Rights Cases (1883):** Congress cannot prohibit racial discrimination by private citizens.
- **Plessy v. Ferguson (1896):** "Separate but equal" — upheld segregation.
- **Jim Crow Laws** — racial segregation in all public facilities.
- **Disenfranchisement:** Literacy tests, poll taxes, White-only primaries, grandfather clauses.

### Responses to Segregation

| Leader | Philosophy |
|---|---|
| **Booker T. Washington** | Economic self-help; vocational training; Tuskegee Institute; "Atlanta Compromise" — accept segregation for now, focus on economic gains |
| **W.E.B. Du Bois** | Immediate civil rights and higher education; criticized Washington's accommodation; founded NAACP |

## 6.6 — The Rise of Industrial Capitalism

### Railroads
- After Civil War, mileage expanded dramatically; federal subsidies (loans + land grants).
- Created a **national market**; standardized time zones.
- **Cornelius Vanderbilt** — merged local railroads into NY Central Railroad (vertical integration; "robber baron").
- Problems: overbuilding, fraud, "watering stock" (Jay Gould), rebates to favored shippers.
- Panic of 1893 → J.P. Morgan consolidated bankrupt railroads into regional monopolies.

### Key Industrialists

| Person | Industry | Method | Label |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Andrew Carnegie** | Steel | Vertical integration (raw materials → transport) | Both "captain of industry" & "robber baron" |
| **John D. Rockefeller** | Oil (Standard Oil) | Horizontal integration; controlled 90% of refineries | "Robber baron" |
| **J.P. Morgan** | Banking/Finance | Holding companies; railroad consolidation | "Robber baron" |

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Trust** | Organization managing assets of other companies |
| **Horizontal integration** | Controlling all former competitors in one industry |
| **Vertical integration** | Controlling all stages of production |
| **Holding company** | Owning and controlling diverse companies |

### Laissez-Faire and Social Darwinism
- **Adam Smith (1776):** "Invisible hand" — free market is most efficient.
- **Social Darwinism (Spencer, Sumner):** Only the "fittest" businesses survive; aiding the poor interferes with natural economic evolution.
- By 1890s: richest 10% controlled 90% of U.S. wealth.

## 6.7 — Labor in the Gilded Age

### Conditions
- 10-hour days, 6 days/week; low wages due to immigrant labor supply.
- ~20% of children working by 1900.

### Management Tactics Against Labor

| Tactic | Description |
|---|---|
| Lockout | Close factory to break labor movement |
| Blacklist | Share names of pro-union workers |
| Yellow-dog Contract | Workers agree not to join union as condition of employment |
| Court Injunction | Legal action to stop strikes |

### Major Labor Organizations

| Organization | Founded | Key Facts |
|---|---|---|
| National Labor Union | 1866 | First to organize all workers; 8-hour day for federal workers |
| Knights of Labor | 1869 (public 1881) | All workers incl. women & Black workers; peaked 730,000 (1886); declined after Haymarket Bombing |
| **AFL** | 1886 | Samuel Gompers; skilled workers; collective bargaining; 1 million members by 1901 |

### Major Strikes

| Strike | Date | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Great Railroad Strike** | 1877 | 500,000 workers; Hayes used federal troops; 100+ deaths |
| **Homestead Strike** | 1892 | Carnegie/Frick cut wages; private guards defeated union; 16 deaths; set back steel unions until New Deal |
| **Pullman Strike** | 1894 | Eugene Debs/ARU; Cleveland used army to deliver mail; injunction broke strike; *In re Debs* (1895) upheld injunctions against strikes; Debs → founded American Socialist Party |

### Haymarket Bombing (1886)
- Chicago; bomb killed 7 police; 8 anarchist leaders tried; Knights of Labor lost popularity and membership.

## 6.8 — Immigration and Migration in the Gilded Age

### Immigration Waves

| Wave | Origins | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **"Old" Immigrants** (through 1880s) | Northern/Western Europe (British Isles, Germany, Scandinavia) | Protestant; English-speaking; high literacy; easier assimilation |
| **"New" Immigrants** (from 1890s) | Southern/Eastern Europe (Italy, Greece, Croatia, Slovakia, Poland, Russia) | Often poor, illiterate; non-Protestant; ethnic neighborhoods; many "birds of passage" |
| **Asian Immigrants** | China (Gold Rush 1848), Japan, Korea, Philippines | Anti-Asian laws; primarily Hawaii and California |

### Urbanization
- By 1900: 40% of Americans in towns/cities.
- By 1920: more Americans in urban than rural areas.
- Ethnic neighborhoods: maintained language, culture (e.g., NYC dumbbell tenements).

---

## 6.9 — Responses to Immigration in the Gilded Age

### Opposition to Immigration
- Labor unions: immigrants depressed wages, broke strikes.
- Nativists: cultural dilution; prejudice against Catholics.
- Social Darwinists: viewed southern/eastern Europeans and non-Europeans as inferior.

### Restriction Laws

| Law | Year | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Chinese Exclusion Act** | 1882 | Banned all new Chinese immigration |
| Contract Labor Law | 1885 | Restricted temporary workers |
| Literacy tests | 1892+ | At Ellis Island; targeted "undesirables" |

### Boss and Machine Politics
- **Tammany Hall (NYC):** Political machine; provided jobs/housing to immigrants for votes.
- Known for corruption and fraud.

### Settlement Houses
- **Hull House (Chicago, 1889)** — Jane Addams; English classes, childcare, arts.
- By 1910: 400+ settlement houses in major cities.

## 6.10 — Development of the Middle Class

### The Gospel of Wealth (Andrew Carnegie)
- Wealthy have a **moral responsibility** to use fortunes to benefit society.
- Carnegie distributed over $350 million to libraries, universities, public institutions.

### Working Women
- By 1900: 1 in 5 adult women worked for wages.
- Moved into clerical positions (secretaries, bookkeepers, telephone operators).
- Lower pay and status in "feminized" fields (nursing, teaching).

### Suburban Growth
- Low-cost land, cheap transportation, construction methods → middle-class suburbs.
- Frederick Law Olmsted designed suburban communities with curved roads, open spaces.

### Changes in Education
- Compulsory education → literacy rate ~90% by 1900.
- College enrollment: 50,000 (1870) → 600,000+ (1920).
- New social sciences: psychology, sociology, political science.

### Popular Culture
- **Mass-circulation press:** Pulitzer's New York World, Hearst's papers — sensationalist journalism.
- **Sports:** Baseball (urban pastime), football (started 1869 at college), basketball (1891).

## 6.11 — Reform in the Gilded Age

### Social Gospel Movement
- Protestant clergy applied Christian principles to social problems.
- **Walter Rauschenbusch** — linked Christianity with Progressive reform.

### Women's Suffrage
- **NAWSA (1890)** — Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Susan B. Anthony; Wyoming full suffrage (1869).
- By 1900: some states allowed women in local elections.

### Temperance
- **WCTU** (1874, Frances Willard) — 500,000 members by 1898.
- **Anti-Saloon League (1893)** — 21 states closed saloons by 1916.

## 6.12 — Controversies over the Role of Government

### Interstate Commerce Act (1887)
- Response to *Wabash v. Illinois* (1886) — Supreme Court nullified state railroad regulation.
- Required railroad rates to be "reasonable and just."
- Created **Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC)** — initially helped railroads more than farmers.

### Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)
- First federal legislation to curb monopolistic practices.
- Prohibited "combination in restraint of trade."
- Initially **ineffective** — vague wording; *U.S. v. E.C. Knight Co.* (1895) limited it to commerce (not manufacturing).
- Strengthened during Progressive Era.

### Political Issues

| Issue | Details |
|---|---|
| **Civil Service Reform** | Pendleton Act (1881) — competitive exams; prohibited political donations by civil servants |
| **Money Question** | Debtors: "easy" money; creditors: gold standard. Greenback Party → Bland-Allison Act (limited silver coinage) |
| **Tariff** | High tariffs benefited industrialists; hurt farmers (agricultural surpluses, lower prices) |

---

## 6.13 — Politics in the Gilded Age

### Political Stalemate
- Neither party addressed industrial/urban issues; campaigns focused on patronage.
- Republicans: northern base; Democrats: South + urban immigrants.

### Rise of the Populists
- **Omaha Platform (1892):**
  - Direct election of senators.
  - Initiatives and referendums.
  - Unlimited coinage of silver (expand money supply).
  - Graduated income tax.
  - Government ownership of railroads, telegraphs, telephones.
  - Loans and federal warehouses for farmers.
  - 8-hour workday.

### Key Political Events

| Event | Details |
|---|---|
| Election of 1892 | Populist James Weaver: 1M+ votes, 22 electoral votes; Cleveland won |
| Panic of 1893 | Stock market crash → severe depression; Cleveland's gold standard adherence worsened it |
| **Coxey's Army (1894)** | Jacob Coxey led unemployed to Washington demanding public works |
| **Election of 1896** | William Jennings Bryan ("Cross of Gold" speech) vs. **William McKinley** (gold standard, high tariffs); McKinley won → Republican dominance; triumph of urban/industrial interests |

> Bryan's defeat ended the Populist Party as a major force. McKinley's presidency set the stage for U.S. emergence as a world power.
