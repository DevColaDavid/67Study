# Period 3 — American Revolution (1754–1800)

---

## 3.2 — The Seven Years' War (The French and Indian War)

### Wars Leading Up to It

| War | Dates | Key Events |
|---|---|---|
| King William's War | 1689–1697 | British failed to seize Quebec |
| Queen Anne's War | 1702–1713 | Britain gained Nova Scotia, trading rights in Spanish America |
| King George's War | 1744–1748 | British captured Louisbourg (later returned) |

### The Seven Years' War (1754–1763)
- **Cause:** French built forts in the Ohio River Valley.
- **Initial Setbacks:** General Braddock's failed 1755 expedition.
- **Turn:** Prime Minister William Pitt shifted focus to Canada → key victories at Louisbourg, Quebec, Montreal.

### Albany Plan of Union (1754)
- Ben Franklin's proposal for unified colonial government and defense.
- **Failed** (colonies unwilling to give up tax control) but **precedent** for future revolutionary congresses.

### Treaty of Paris (1763)
- Britain gained **French Canada** and **Spanish Florida**; France ceded Louisiana to Spain.
- Britain emerged as the dominant European power in North America.

### Aftermath & Tensions

| Issue | Details |
|---|---|
| **End of Salutary Neglect** | Britain shifted to direct, stringent control |
| **Pontiac's Rebellion (1763)** | Native coalition against British frontier encroachment; Britain sent regular troops |
| **Proclamation of 1763** | Banned colonial settlement west of the Appalachians → widespread colonial anger |

## 3.3 — Taxation Without Representation

### Key Acts & Colonial Responses

| Act | Year | Key Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Proclamation of 1763** | 1763 | Banned settlement west of Appalachians |
| **Sugar Act** | 1764 | Duties on foreign sugars; admiralty courts (no juries) |
| **Quartering Act** | 1765 | Colonies must house/supply British troops |
| **Stamp Act** | 1765 | First *direct* tax; stamps on all printed materials → **Stamp Act Congress** |
| **Declaratory Act** | 1766 | Parliament has authority to legislate for colonies in ALL cases |
| **Townshend Acts** | 1767 | Duties on tea, glass, paper; Writs of Assistance (general warrants) |

### Colonial Unity & Protest
- **Stamp Act Congress (1765):** Delegates from 9 colonies declared only elected representatives could levy taxes.
- **Sons & Daughters of Liberty:** Formed to oppose Stamp Act enforcement.
- **Boycotts** of British goods → economic pressure on Britain.

### Crisis Deepens

| Event | Year | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Boston Massacre** | 1770 | British troops fired on crowd; 5 killed (incl. Crispus Attucks); Paul Revere's engraving = propaganda |
| **Committees of Correspondence** | 1772 | Samuel Adams; network of colonial opposition; spread to Virginia |
| **Gaspee Incident** | 1772 | Colonists attacked British customs ship; Britain tried suspects in England → increased grievances |
| **Boston Tea Party** | 1773 | Tea Act retained tax; colonists (disguised as Native Americans) dumped 342 chests of tea |
| **Intolerable Acts** | 1774 | Port Act, MA Government Act, Justice Act, expanded Quartering Act, Quebec Act |

## 3.4 — Philosophical Foundations of the American Revolution

### Enlightenment Influence
- **Deism:** God creates natural laws but does not intervene in human affairs.
- **Rationalism:** Human reason can solve societal problems.
- **Social Contract Theory (Locke, Rousseau):** Governments formed by compact among the people to ensure liberty; challenged divine right of monarchs.

### Thomas Paine's *Common Sense* (January 1776)
- Argued it was irrational for a vast continent to be ruled by a distant island and a corrupt monarch.
- Directly attacked **King George III** and monarchy itself.
- Shifted public opinion from discontent to active support for independence.

## 3.5 — The American Revolution

### First Continental Congress (September 1774)
- Convened in Philadelphia to address Intolerable Acts.
- **Actions:** Suffolk Resolves (repeal Intolerable Acts, military prep), Continental Association (enforce boycotts), set to reconvene May 1775 if demands unmet.

### Outbreak of Hostilities

| Event | Date | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Lexington & Concord** | April 18, 1775 | British sought colonial arms; colonial militias forced British retreat |
| **Battle of Bunker Hill** | June 17, 1775 | Technically British victory; showed colonial capacity to inflict heavy casualties |

### Second Continental Congress (May 1775)
- Appointed **George Washington** commander-in-chief.
- Sent **Olive Branch Petition** to King George III → rejected (Prohibitory Act, Aug 1775).

### Declaration of Independence
- Richard Henry Lee's resolution (June 7, 1776) → **Thomas Jefferson** drafted it.
- **Adopted July 4, 1776** — enshrined Enlightenment principles of individual rights.

### Revolutionary War (1775–1783)

**Allegiances:**
- **Patriots (~40%)** — supported independence.
- **Loyalists/Tories (~25%)** — supported Britain.
- **African Americans (~5,000)** — served in Patriot forces after Britain offered freedom to those who joined their side.
- **American Indians** — mostly sided with Britain (promised restricted colonial expansion).

**Key Turning Points:**

| Event | Date | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Battle of Saratoga** | Oct 1777 | American victory → secured **French alliance (1778)**, then Spain & Holland |
| **Valley Forge** | Winter 1777–78 | Symbol of Patriot suffering and resilience |
| **Battle of Yorktown** | 1781 | Final major battle; French navy cut off British; Cornwallis surrendered |

### Treaty of Paris (1783)
- Britain recognized U.S. independence.
- Mississippi River = western boundary.
- Americans gained fishing rights off Canada.
- Americans agreed to pay pre-war debts and compensate Loyalists.

## 3.6 — The Influence of Revolutionary Ideals

### Women
- **Daughters of Liberty** — boycotts and wartime support.
- **Republican Motherhood** — women educated to instill republican values in children; indirect political influence (not public equality).

### Enslaved African Americans
- Slavery contradicted revolutionary principles of liberty.
- Some northern states began abolition.
- **Cotton gin (1793)** revitalized demand for slave labor → undermined ideals.

### Native Americans
- Largely supported the British; suffered greatly after American victory — increased dispossession.

### International Repercussions
- Declaration of Independence inspired: French Revolution, Haitian Revolution, Latin American independence movements.

## 3.7 — The Articles of Confederation

### Structure
- **Unicameral Congress** — one vote per state; key decisions needed supermajority; amendments required unanimity.
- **No executive branch, no federal judiciary.**
- Congress could conduct diplomacy, borrow money, manage war — but **could NOT tax or regulate commerce**.

### Accomplishments
- Oversaw end of Revolutionary War and Treaty of Paris.
- **Land Ordinance of 1785** — systematic survey/sale of western lands; land set aside for public education.
- **Northwest Ordinance of 1787** — created process for new states; prohibited slavery in the Northwest Territory.

### Weaknesses

| Problem | Details |
|---|---|
| **Foreign Affairs** | Could not enforce treaties; British kept frontier posts |
| **Economic** | Could not tax → no stable currency; national debt unpaid |
| **Internal Conflicts** | States acted as sovereigns; tariff wars; unresolved boundary disputes |

### Shays's Rebellion (1786–1787)
- Massachusetts farmers (led by **Daniel Shays**) closed debtors' courts; attempted to seize a federal armory.
- **Showed the Articles' inability to maintain public order** → catalyzed the Constitutional Convention.

## 3.8 — The Constitutional Convention and Ratification

### The Convention (Philadelphia, 1787)
- 12 states attended (Rhode Island abstained).
- Goal: Revise Articles → resulted in entirely **new Constitution**.

### Key Debates & Compromises

| Issue | Plans / Solution |
|---|---|
| **Representation** | Virginia Plan (pop.) vs. New Jersey Plan (equal) → **Great Compromise** (Roger Sherman): bicameral Congress — Senate (equal) + House (proportional) |
| **Slavery** | **Three-Fifths Compromise** — enslaved person counted as 3/5 for taxation/representation; slave trade protected until 1808 |
| **Commerce** | Congress regulates interstate/foreign commerce; no export taxes |
| **Presidency** | 4-year term; **Electoral College**; presidential veto |

### Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

| Group | Key Views | Notable Figures |
|---|---|---|
| **Federalists** | Strong federal government; loose interpretation | Madison, Hamilton, John Jay |
| **Anti-Federalists** | Feared tyranny; wanted states' rights; demanded Bill of Rights | George Mason, Patrick Henry |

### The Federalist Papers
- 85 essays by **Madison, Hamilton, and Jay** arguing for ratification.

### Ratification
- Key objection: **No Bill of Rights**.
- Federalists promised to add one → **Bill of Rights ratified 1791**.

## 3.9 — The Constitution

### Federalism
- **Federal:** National defense, foreign affairs, interstate commerce, postal services.
- **States:** Education, local elections, etc.
- Federal power has grown over time via amendments (e.g., 19th Amendment) and economic interconnection.

### Separation of Powers & Checks and Balances

| Branch | Role |
|---|---|
| **Legislative (Congress)** | Makes laws, taxes, spending |
| **Executive (President)** | Recommends/enforces laws; veto |
| **Judicial (Supreme Court)** | Interprets laws and Constitution |

### Bill of Rights (Ratified 1791)

| Amendment | Protection |
|---|---|
| 1st | Religion, speech, press, assembly, petition |
| 2nd | Right to bear arms |
| 3rd | No quartering of soldiers |
| 4th | No unreasonable searches/seizures |
| 5th | Due process; self-incrimination; double jeopardy |
| 6th | Speedy/public trial; counsel |
| 7th | Jury trial in civil cases |
| 8th | No cruel/unusual punishment or excessive bail |
| 9th | Unlisted rights still protected |
| 10th | Powers not delegated → reserved to states/people |

## 3.10 — Shaping a New Republic

### Washington's Presidency
- First cabinet: Jefferson (State), Hamilton (Treasury), Knox (War), Randolph (AG).
- **Judiciary Act of 1789**: Supreme Court — 1 Chief Justice + 5 Associates; district/circuit courts.

### Hamilton's Financial Plan
1. **Pay off debt** at face value (federal assumes state war debts).
2. **Protective tariffs** to promote industry.
3. **National Bank** for stable currency.
- Opposition from Jefferson (favored farmers); compromise → lower tariffs + capital moved to Washington, D.C.

### Foreign Affairs Under Washington
- **French Revolution** divided Americans; Washington declared **Proclamation of Neutrality (1793)**.
- **Jay Treaty (1794):** Britain evacuated frontier posts but didn't address impressment.
- **Pinckney Treaty (1795):** Spain opened Mississippi River and New Orleans to American trade; Florida boundary set at 31st parallel.

### Domestic Issues

| Event | Details |
|---|---|
| **Northwest Confederacy / Battle of Fallen Timbers (1794)** | General Anthony Wayne defeated Native tribes; Treaty of Greenville → Ohio Territory opened |
| **Whiskey Rebellion (1794)** | Western PA farmers refused whiskey tax; Washington sent 15,000 militia → rebellion collapsed; showed federal authority |

### First Political Parties

| Party | Leader | Views |
|---|---|---|
| **Federalists** | Hamilton | Strong central government; loose Constitution; northern business |
| **Democratic-Republicans** | Jefferson | States' rights; strict Constitution; small farmers |

### Washington's Farewell Address
- Warned against: **permanent alliances, political parties, sectionalism, European entanglements**.
- Set **two-term precedent** (until FDR; codified by **22nd Amendment, 1951**).

### John Adams' Presidency
- **XYZ Affair:** French demanded bribes from U.S. diplomats → outrage but Adams avoided war.
- **Alien and Sedition Acts:** Suppressed Democratic-Republican opposition; criminalized criticism of government.
- **Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions (Jefferson & Madison):** States could **nullify** unconstitutional federal laws → precursor to states' rights disputes.
