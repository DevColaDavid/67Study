# Period 7 — Progressive Era and World Wars (1890–1945)

---

## 7.2 — Imperialism: Debates

### Context
- **Alaska (1867):** Secretary of State Seward purchased from Russia for $7.2M ("Seward's Folly").
- **Hawaii (1893):** American settlers overthrew the Hawaiian monarchy; Cleveland opposed annexation; McKinley later annexed it.

### Arguments FOR Imperialism
- **Economic:** Expand markets; secure raw materials.
- **Military:** Alfred Thayer Mahan — strong navy and overseas bases essential for world power.
- **Social/Racial:** Anglo-Saxon superiority; moral duty to spread Christianity and Western civilization.
- **Social Darwinism:** U.S. expansion as natural selection on a global scale.

### Arguments AGAINST
- Contradicted self-determination and democracy.
- Rejected racial superiority theories.
- Isolationists invoked Washington's advice against foreign entanglements.
- Financial burden of navy and overseas territories.

## 7.3 — The Spanish-American War and Foreign Policy

### Causes of the Spanish-American War

| Cause | Details |
|---|---|
| Cuban Revolt | Spain's harsh response (Gen. Weyler — "The Butcher") created concentration camps; American sympathy |
| Yellow Journalism | Pulitzer (New York World) and Hearst (New York Journal) sensationalized Spanish atrocities |
| De Lôme Letter (1898) | Spanish diplomat criticized McKinley → outrage |
| **Sinking of the USS Maine (Feb 15, 1898)** | 260 Americans killed; yellow press blamed Spain (likely an accident) |

### Outcomes (Treaty of Paris, Dec 10, 1898)
- U.S. gained: Cuba's independence, **Puerto Rico, Guam**, control of **Philippines** ($20M to Spain).

### Philippine-American War
- Emilio Aguinaldo resisted U.S. control; 3-year war; significant casualties.
- Heated debate: annexation violated self-governance principles.

### Cuba and the Platt Amendment (1901)
- Made Cuba a U.S. protectorate; allowed U.S. intervention; Guantanamo Bay naval base.

### Open Door Policy (1899)
- Secretary of State John Hay — equal trading rights for all nations in China.

### Roosevelt's "Big Stick" Policy
- "Speak softly and carry a big stick" — military strength to achieve diplomatic goals.
- **Panama Canal:**
  - Roosevelt supported Panamanian independence from Colombia (1903).
  - Hay-Bunau-Varilla Treaty granted U.S. control of Canal Zone.
  - Canal completed **1914** — linked Atlantic and Pacific.

### Roosevelt Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine (1904)
- U.S. would intervene in European-Latin American conflicts to prevent European enforcement.
- Positioned U.S. as **police power** in Latin America.

### Other Foreign Policies

| Policy | President | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Gentlemen's Agreement (1907)** | Roosevelt | Restricted Japanese immigration; avoided official exclusion act |
| **Great White Fleet (1907–09)** | Roosevelt | Global naval tour; demonstrated U.S. naval power |
| **Dollar Diplomacy** | Taft | U.S. financial investment abroad replaces military action |
| **Moral Diplomacy** | Wilson | Promoted democracy; opposed imperialism |
| **Jones Act (1916)** | Wilson | Philippines — full territorial status; male suffrage; promised independence |

## 7.4 — The Progressives

### Origins
- Middle-class Americans disturbed by big business power, economic disparities, political corruption.
- Embraced **pragmatism** (William James, John Dewey) — truth tested by practical outcomes.

### Muckrakers
- Journalists who exposed societal issues (coined by TR).
- Key works: Upton Sinclair (*The Jungle* — meatpacking), Ida Tarbell (Standard Oil).

### Political Reforms

| Reform | Details |
|---|---|
| Secret Ballot | Voter privacy; reduced manipulation |
| Direct Primaries | Party members choose candidates directly |
| **17th Amendment (1913)** | Direct election of U.S. Senators (previously chosen by state legislatures) |
| **18th Amendment (1920)** | Prohibition of alcohol |
| **19th Amendment (1920)** | Women's suffrage |

### Theodore Roosevelt's Presidency

**Square Deal ("Three C's"):**
1. **Control of Corporations** — trust-busting.
2. **Consumer Protection** — food safety.
3. **Conservation** of natural resources.

**Key Actions:**

| Action | Details |
|---|---|
| **1902 Coal Strike** | Mediated between miners and owners; 10% wage increase, 9-hour workday; first time president intervened in labor dispute |
| **Trust Busting** | Standard Oil lawsuit → breakup 1911 (Sherman Antitrust Act) |
| Elkins Act (1903) | Prohibited railroad rebates to favored customers |
| Hepburn Act (1906) | ICC set maximum railroad rates |
| Pure Food and Drug Act (1906) | Banned adulterated/mislabeled food and drugs → FDA |
| Meat Inspection Act (1906) | Federal inspection of meat processing plants |
| Newlands Reclamation Act | Funded irrigation in arid western lands |
| National parks/forests | Established numerous protected areas |

### William Taft
- Continued progressive policies; more cautious.
- **Mann-Elkins Act (1910):** Extended ICC authority to telephone/telegraph.
- **16th Amendment (1913):** Federal graduated income tax.

### Election of 1912
- TR vs. Taft (Republican split) → **Woodrow Wilson** (Democrat) won.

### Wilson's "New Freedom"

**Goal:** Dismantle the "triple wall of privilege" (tariffs, banks, trusts).

| Reform | Details |
|---|---|
| **Underwood Tariff (1913)** | First significant tariff reduction in 50+ years; graduated income tax to compensate |
| **Federal Reserve Act (1914)** | Created the Federal Reserve System — 12 district banks; central banking structure |
| **Federal Trade Commission (FTC)** | Investigate and prevent unfair trade practices |
| **Clayton Antitrust Act** | Strengthened Sherman Act; exempted labor unions |
| Federal Farm Loan Act | Low-interest loans for farmers |
| Child Labor Act (1916) | Prohibited products made by children under 14 (later struck down) |

### Social Workers
- **Jane Addams (Hull House)** — civic reform; child-labor laws; housing reform; women's rights.
- **Frances Perkins** and **Harry Hopkins** → later New Deal leaders.

### NAACP and National Urban League
- **NAACP (1909):** Legal challenges against segregation and discrimination.
- **National Urban League (1911):** Helped African Americans transition to urban life.

### African Americans in the Progressive Era
- **Booker T. Washington:** Economic self-help, vocational education.
- **W.E.B. Du Bois:** Immediate civil rights, political participation.
- Progressive Era largely ignored racial inequalities.

### Women's Suffrage
- **NAWSA** and more radical **National Woman's Party (Alice Paul)** pushed for constitutional amendment.
- **19th Amendment (1920):** Women's right to vote — aided by women's contributions in WWI.

## 7.5 — World War I: Military and Diplomacy

### Wilson's Neutrality
- Declared U.S. neutral at the outbreak of WWI.
- "Freedom of the seas" — neutral nations should trade without interference.
- British naval blockade and German submarine warfare complicated neutrality.

### Road to War

| Event | Date | Details |
|---|---|---|
| Lusitania Sinking | May 1915 | 1,198 passengers (128 Americans) killed by German U-boat |
| **Zimmermann Telegram** | March 1917 | Germany proposed military alliance with Mexico against the U.S. |
| Renewed Submarine Attacks | 1917 | Germany resumed unrestricted submarine warfare |
| Declaration of War | April 6, 1917 | Wilson: "make the world safe for democracy" |

### Wilson's Fourteen Points (1918)
- Transparent agreements; freedom of navigation; arms reduction; self-determination; **League of Nations**.

### Treaty of Versailles (1919)
- Imposed harsh penalties on Germany: reparations, territorial losses, military restrictions.
- Included League of Nations and self-determination principles.

### Rejection of the Treaty
- **Irreconcilables:** Opposed League under any circumstances.
- **Reservationists (Lodge):** Demanded significant changes.
- Senate rejected the treaty; U.S. never joined the League of Nations.

## 7.6 — World War I: Home Front

### Mobilization
- **Herbert Hoover (Food Administration):** Voluntary conservation; increased food shipments to Allies.
- **Railroad Administration (McAdoo):** Government took control of railroads; standardized equipment.
- **National War Labor Board (Taft):** Resolved labor disputes; higher wages, shorter workdays.
- **Committee on Public Information:** Propaganda; maintained public support.

### Financing the War
- Raised **$33 billion**; **Liberty Bonds** encouraged citizen investment.

### Civil Liberties Curtailed

| Law / Case | Details |
|---|---|
| **Espionage Act (1917)** | Prohibited interference with military operations; harsh penalties |
| **Sedition Act (1918)** | Expanded to include disloyal speech about the government |
| **Schenck v. United States (1919)** | Holmes' "clear and present danger" test — speech can be restricted during wartime |

### African Americans
- ~400,000 served in segregated units.
- Great Migration: ~1 million moved from South to northern cities for jobs.

### Postwar Problems
- **Red Scare (1919):** Fear of communism → xenophobia, restrictive immigration.
- **Palmer Raids:** Mass arrests of anarchists, socialists; 6,000 arrests, 500 deported including Emma Goldman.

## 7.7 — 1920s: Innovations in Communication and Technology

### Roaring Twenties
- 64% rise in manufacturing output (1919–1929).
- Assembly line (Ford) → mass production across industries.
- Oil and electricity fueled economic growth; electrical generation grew 300%+.

### Key Economic Features

| Topic | Details |
|---|---|
| **Consumer Economy** | Electric appliances; buying on credit expanded purchasing power (and later instability) |
| **Automobiles** | Transformed society; boosted steel, rubber, gasoline; introduced commuting |
| **Farm Problems** | Crop prices fell; overproduction; rural America left behind |
| **Labor Unions** | Membership declined 20% due to "open shop" policies and welfare capitalism |

### Technology and Culture
- **Radio** — mass medium alongside newspapers.
- **Jazz** — African American musicians; symbolized modern urban culture.
- **Art Deco** — new architectural styles.

## 7.8 — 1920s: Cultural and Political Controversies

### Modernism vs. Fundamentalism

| Group | Beliefs |
|---|---|
| **Modernists** | Urban Protestants; reconciled religion with science; less literal Bible interpretation |
| **Fundamentalists** | Rural conservatives; literal Bible; opposed evolution as threat to morality |

### Scopes Trial (1925)
- John Scopes charged for teaching evolution in Tennessee.
- Clarence Darrow (defense) vs. William Jennings Bryan (prosecution).
- Scopes convicted (overturned on technicality); illustrated cultural battle over science and religion.

### Prohibition
- **18th Amendment + Volstead Act** — outlawed manufacture, sale, transport of alcohol.
- Led to speakeasies, bootlegging, organized crime (Al Capone).
- Repealed by **21st Amendment (1933)**.

### Immigration Restrictions
- Quota laws targeted "undesirable" immigrants from southern/eastern Europe and Asia.

### Revival of the KKK
- Expanded to target Jews, Catholics, immigrants (not just African Americans).
- Millions of members at peak; criminal activities led to decline by late 1920s.

### Harlem Renaissance
- Flourishing of African American culture — poetry, music, art — centered in Harlem, NYC.
- **Langston Hughes, Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong** — gained national recognition.
- Jazz became defining feature of the period.

### Republican Dominance

| President | Key Notes |
|---|---|
| **Warren Harding (1921–23)** | Teapot Dome Scandal — Interior Secretary Albert Fall illegally leased oil reserves; first Presidential Cabinet member convicted |
| **Calvin Coolidge** | Laissez-faire; tax cuts; vetoed farm/veteran bills; extended 1920s prosperity |
| **Herbert Hoover** | Capable administrator; elected 1928; soon faced Great Depression |

## 7.9 — The Great Depression

### Stock Market Crash (1929)
- **Black Thursday (Oct 24)** — massive sell-off.
- **Black Tuesday (Oct 29)** — complete collapse.

### Underlying Causes

| Cause | Details |
|---|---|
| Uneven income distribution | Richest 5% earned 33%+ of all income |
| Stock market speculation / buying on margin | Prices fell → financial ruin |
| Excessive use of credit | Over-borrowing → loan defaults, bank failures |
| Overproduction of consumer goods | Surplus → financial strain |
| Weak farm economy | Ongoing issues since 1920s |
| Government policies | Lax regulation; high tariffs; Federal Reserve gold standard |
| Global economic problems | Europe still recovering from WWI |

### Effects
- U.S. GNP: $104B (1929) → $56B (1932).
- **25% unemployment by 1933** (13 million workers).
- "Hoovervilles" — shantytowns of homeless.
- 20% of banks failed → 10 million savings accounts erased.

### Hoover's Policies
- Initially: voluntary action, private charity; opposed direct federal aid.
- **Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930):** Highest tariffs in history → retaliatory tariffs → reduced global trade.
- **Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC, 1932):** Emergency loans to banks and railroads ("trickle-down").

## 7.10 — The New Deal

### FDR's Election (1932)
- Promised "new deal"; repealed Prohibition; aid for unemployed; reduced government spending.
- Won decisively: ~60% popular vote; all but 6 states.

### New Deal Philosophy
- **Three R's:** Relief (unemployed), Recovery (economy), Reform (economic institutions).

### First New Deal Programs

| Program | Director | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **FERA** | Harry Hopkins | Federal funds to local governments for direct relief |
| **PWA** | Harold Ickes | Infrastructure projects (roads, bridges) |
| **CCC** | — | Young men on conservation projects |
| **TVA** | — | Regional development; dams and power plants for Tennessee Valley |
| **Glass-Steagall Act** | — | Banking regulations; restricted use of customer deposits |
| **FDIC** | — | Government insurance for bank deposits |
| **SEC** | — | Regulated stock market; corporate transparency |
| **AAA** | — | Paid farmers to reduce crop production → raise prices (later unconstitutional) |
| **NRA** | — | Set fair practices and labor standards (later unconstitutional) |
| Bank Holiday | Roosevelt | Temporarily closed banks; Emergency Banking Act → stable reopening |

### Fireside Chats
- Radio broadcasts by FDR; first on March 12, 1933; restored public confidence in banking.

### Second New Deal Programs

| Program | Details |
|---|---|
| **WPA (1935–40)** | Millions of jobs; large-scale public works; supported unemployed artists and writers |
| **Wagner Act (NLRA)** | Guaranteed union rights and collective bargaining; National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) |
| **Social Security Act (1935)** | Insurance for retirees 65+; unemployment and disability benefits |
| **Revenue Act (1935)** | Increased taxes on high incomes, large gifts, capital gains |

### Election of 1936 and Coalition
- FDR reelected; New Deal coalition: Solid South, urban ethnic groups, Midwestern farmers, labor unions, liberals, northern Black voters (shifted from Republican to Democrat).

### Supreme Court Challenges
- Court struck down NRA and AAA.
- FDR proposed "court-packing" plan (appoint additional justices) → widely criticized and defeated.
- Court later upheld Wagner Act and Social Security Act.

### Fair Labor Standards Act (1938)
- Minimum wage; standard workweek with overtime; restrictions on child labor.

### Social Conditions

| Issue | Details |
|---|---|
| Dust Bowl | Severe droughts in Great Plains → migration (depicted in Steinbeck's *The Grapes of Wrath*) |
| Women | Lower wages; Eleanor Roosevelt advocated for them |
| African Americans | Higher unemployment; often excluded from relief programs |

> **New Deal criticized** for not being an equal opportunity for all.

### Limitations
- Did not fully end the Depression; economy remained unstable.
- Political resistance by 1937 (coalition of Republicans + conservative Democrats).
- Focus shifted to threats from Nazi Germany by late 1930s.

## 7.11 — Interwar Foreign Policy

### Disarmament Efforts (1920s)
- **Washington Conference (1921):** Five-Power, Four-Power, Nine-Power Treaties; Pacific stability.
- **Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928):** Renounce war as national policy; no enforcement mechanism.
- **Dawes Plan (1924):** Germany paid reparations → Allies paid U.S. war debts; collapsed after 1929 crash.

### FDR's Foreign Policy
- **Good Neighbor Policy (1933):** Ended U.S. intervention in Latin American affairs.

### Rise of Fascism and Militarism

| Country | Leader | Key Events |
|---|---|---|
| **Italy** | Mussolini | Fascist regime (1922); aggressive nationalism |
| **Germany** | Hitler | Nazi Party gained power (1933); anti-Semitic policies; militaristic expansion |
| **Japan** | Military leaders | Invaded China; Second Sino-Japanese War (1937); Nanjing Massacre |

### American Isolationism
- Nye Committee (1934): WWI caused by bankers and arms manufacturers.
- **Neutrality Acts (1935–37):** No arms shipments, no travel on belligerent ships, no financial transactions with warring nations.

### Roosevelt's Adjustments
- **Quarantine Speech (1937):** Collective quarantine of aggressors → public opposition.
- **Cash and Carry (1939):** Belligerents could buy U.S. arms with their own transport/funds (favored Britain).
- **Selective Service Act (1940):** First peacetime draft.
- **Lend-Lease Act (1941):** Britain and Allies could purchase military supplies on credit.
- **Atlantic Charter (1941):** FDR and Churchill outlined post-war vision: self-determination, free trade.

### Pearl Harbor
- **December 7, 1941:** Japan attacked U.S. naval base → Roosevelt's "Infamy Speech" → Congress declared war on Japan.
- Germany and Italy declared war on the U.S. → full U.S. entry into WWII.

### Formation of the United Nations
- Discussions at Dumbarton Oaks (1944) → 50 nations drafted UN Charter in San Francisco (April 1945).
- U.S. Senate approved membership — contrast with League of Nations rejection after WWI.
- UN officially formed **October 24, 1945**.

## 7.12 — World War II Mobilization

### U.S. Government Agencies
- **WPB (War Production Board, 1942):** Converted peacetime industries to war production.
- **OPA (Office of Price Administration):** Froze prices, wages, rents; rationed meat, sugar, gasoline → black market.

### Economic Impact
- By 1944: unemployment virtually **eradicated** — **WWII ended the Great Depression**.
- U.S. war-related industrial output in 1944 **doubled that of all Axis powers combined**.
- Manhattan Project: first atomic weapons (Leslie Groves, J. Robert Oppenheimer).

### Society During WWII

| Group | Experience |
|---|---|
| Women | Filled industrial jobs; "Rosie the Riveter"; boosted case for equality |
| African Americans | 1.5 million migrated north for industrial jobs; first African American combat aviators; "Double V" campaign (victory overseas + equality at home); race riots in NYC and Detroit (1943) |

### Japanese Internment
- **Executive Order 9066 (Feb 1942):** Forced relocation/internment of 100,000+ Japanese Americans (2/3 U.S. citizens).
- **Korematsu v. United States (1944):** Supreme Court upheld internment 6-3 — national security outweighed individual rights (later widely criticized).

## 7.13 — World War II: Military

### European Theater
- **German advance halted (1942):** Soviet victory at Stalingrad; U.S. entry.
- Allied strategic bombing reduced Germany's war capacity.
- **D-Day (June 6, 1944):** Invasion of Normandy.
- **Hitler committed suicide (April 1945)** → unconditional German surrender **May 7, 1945**.
- **Holocaust:** U.S. troops uncovered systematic genocide of 6 million Jews and millions of others.

### Pacific Theater
- **Island Hopping Strategy:** Attacked strategic islands; bypassed heavily fortified positions.
- **Kamikaze attacks** — Japanese suicide pilots; notably at Leyte Gulf and Okinawa.

### Atomic Bombs
- **Trinity Test:** July 16, 1945, New Mexico — first successful atomic bomb test.
- **Hiroshima:** August 6, 1945.
- **Nagasaki:** August 9, 1945.
- ~250,000 Japanese casualties; Japan surrendered **August 15, 1945**.
- **Formal surrender:** September 2, 1945, aboard USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay.

## 7.14 — Postwar Diplomacy

### Wartime Conferences

| Conference | Date | Key Outcomes |
|---|---|---|
| **Tehran** | Nov 1943 | Planned D-Day; Soviet entry against Japan |
| **Yalta** | Feb 1945 | Division of Germany; free elections in Eastern Europe (Soviets later violated); Soviet entry vs. Japan; United Nations groundwork |
| **Potsdam** | July 1945 | Finalized Japan surrender demands; emerging U.S.-Soviet tensions |

### Post-War Economic Landscape
- U.S. infrastructure intact → dominant economic power.
- Europe devastated → Marshall Plan (later) to rebuild Western Europe.
