# Unit 6: Consequences of Industrialization (1750–1900 CE)

## 🧠 Core Idea

Industrialization fueled a second wave of imperialism as European powers — armed with superior weapons, nationalist ideology, and Social Darwinist justifications — carved up Africa, Asia, and the Pacific. Colonized peoples were forced into **export economies**. Mass migration followed. Resistance took many forms — from armed rebellion to religious movements to the creation of new states.

---

## ⚡ Key Takeaways

- Three ideologies justified 19th-century imperialism: **nationalism**, **Social Darwinism**, and the **civilizing mission**.
- The **Berlin Conference (1884–85)** divided Africa with **zero African input**.
- **Economic imperialism** = controlling a state through economic means (not just military).
- The **economics of imperialism** = the effect of imperialism on global trade (export economies).
- India and Egypt were forced to grow **cotton for export** — destroying their manufacturing.
- Mass migration was driven by economic hardship, famines, and semi-coerced labor systems.
- **Ethnic enclaves** formed; **nativism** and racial prejudice emerged as backlash.
- Resistance: armed revolt (Asante), religious movements (Xhosa), new states (Zulu, Cherokee), European critique.

## 📑 Section Breakdown

### 💭 Why Imperial States Expanded — Ideologies

#### Nationalism
- Belief in one's national greatness → desire for territory → **intense competition** to build the largest empire.

#### Social Darwinism
- Misapplication of "survival of the fittest" to human societies.
- **"Scientific racism"**: non-Western peoples seen as like children who hadn't matured.
- Industrialized states believed they were "fitter" → entitled to take over "unfit" states.

#### The Civilizing Mission
- Imperial nations claimed a **duty** to develop conquered peoples.
- Included converting to **Christianity** and educating in western-style schools.
- Example: "kill the Indian in him to save the man" (forced assimilation of indigenous children in US/Canada).

---

### 🗺️ How Imperial States Expanded

#### Berlin Conference (1884–1885)
- Called by **Otto von Bismarck** (Germany) due to competition for Africa.
- European powers **carved up Africa** with **no input from African leaders**.
- Combined rival groups; divided unified peoples → created built-in future conflicts.
- British Royal Niger Company used **fill-in-the-blank contracts** to buy land from chiefs.

#### Boer Wars (South Africa)
- British fought **Dutch (Afrikaner)** settlers in South Africa.
- Drove both Dutch Afrikaners and indigenous South Africans into **refugee camps** → brutal conditions + high death tolls.

#### Settler Colonies
- **Australia and New Zealand**: British settlers → new diseases killed indigenous populations (same pattern as Americas c. 1450–1750).

#### United States
- **Manifest Destiny**: Westward expansion; indigenous people restricted to **reservations**.
- **Spanish-American War**: US defeated Spain → took **Philippines** → expanded into Southeast Asia.

#### Russia
- Conquered **Siberia** to the Pacific; added southern and western lands.
- **Pan-Slavism**: Ideology — Russia should unite all Slavic peoples.

#### Japan
- **Meiji Restoration** industrialization → expanded influence over **Korea, Manchuria**, and parts of China.

#### Belgian Congo — Special Case
- **King Leopold II**: Made Congo his **private property**; brutal rubber extraction.
- Belgian government took control away from him → state control.

---

### 💸 Economic Imperialism

**Definition**: Extending control over a state through **economic means**, not outright military conquest.

#### Britain and China — Opium Wars
- Trade imbalance: Britain wanted Chinese goods; China only wanted silver.
- British solution: Sold **opium** to China → China banned it → **First Opium War**.
- Result: **Treaty of Nanjing** — opened more ports; favored British interests.
- **Second Opium War** + **Taiping Rebellion** → **spheres of influence** in China carved up by western powers, Japan, Russia.

#### United Fruit Company — Latin America
- Built railroads and ports in exchange for control of land → **banana production**.
- Gave economic control of nations → textbook example of economic imperialism.

---

### 📦 The Economics of Imperialism (Effects)

> [!warning] Key Distinction
> - **Economic imperialism** = CAUSE (method of control)
> - **The economics of imperialism** = EFFECT (how global economics changed)

#### Export Economies
- Economy organized around **export of raw commodities** (not finished goods).
- Continuity from c. 1450–1750: conquered nations expected to support conquerors.
- Colonies also expected to **buy finished goods** from imperial parent → economic dependency.

#### Examples
- **India and Egypt**: Forced to grow **cotton for export**. Britain had **banned Indian cotton textile exports** → eliminated competition for British textiles.
- **West Africa**: **Palm oil** to lubricate industrial machines.
- **Other regions**: Coffee; meat from livestock.

> [!note] Key Irony
> Industrialized nations adopted free-market capitalism at home but applied **mercantilist principles** to their colonies.

---

### 🚶 Migration in the Industrial Age

#### Why People Migrated
- **Demographic pressure**: Growing European populations → rural job shortages.
- **Famines**: Irish Potato Famine (1840s) → millions emigrated, many to US.
- **Technology**: Railroads and steamships made migration cheap and allowed **return migration**.

#### Types of Migration

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Voluntary** | Free choice; e.g., Irish to US |
| **Indentured Servitude** | British moved Indian and Chinese workers across empire; workers signed contracts they often couldn't read; long hours, terrible conditions. Semi-coerced. |
| **Convict Labor** | British and French sent convicts to **penal colonies** (Australia, French Guiana) for hard labor. Semi-coerced. |

#### Social Effects on Places People Left
- Mostly men migrated → **gender imbalance** → women took on traditionally male roles.

#### Cultural Effects on Receiving Societies
- **Ethnic enclaves**: Chinatown, Little Italy — familiar food and culture.
- **Nativism**: Prejudice against immigrants rooted in **Social Darwinism**.
  - US: **Chinese Exclusion Act**.
  - Australia: **White Australia Policy**.

---

### 🛡️ Resistance to Imperialism

#### Causes of Growing Resistance
- **Civilizing mission backfired**: Western education taught **popular sovereignty** and **natural rights** → questioned imperial authority.
- **European critics**: Joseph Conrad (*Heart of Darkness*); J.A. Hobson (economist); Karl Marx.

#### Direct Armed Resistance
- **Yaa Asantewaa War** ("War of the Golden Stool"): Asante Kingdom fought Britain. **Queen Mother Yaa Asantewaa** rallied her people. British industrial weapons ultimately won, but the resistance was fierce.

#### Creating New States at Periphery
- **Cherokee Nation**: Forced to relocate East Coast → Oklahoma; organized own government; tried to preserve culture.
- **Zulu Kingdom**: Created new state at edge of British South Africa; resisted British takeover for a time.

#### Religious Resistance
- **Xhosa Cattle Killing Movement**: After losing land and cattle, the Xhosa believed slaughtering their cattle would bring new cattle and drive away invaders. Instead, many Xhosa **died from starvation** and the British took complete control.
